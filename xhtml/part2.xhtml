PART 2

Part I made the case that leading is managing through friction. Leading means working with the emotional reality of people, their feelings, moods, and emotions. There is no leading around that layer; you must lead through it.

Now we get practical.

Before we hand you some tools, we want to be transparent about how we think change happens, because this book has a theory, and it’s worth naming.

We are not betting on insight alone. If you are anything like us you have probably read or heard something that made you think yes, exactly! I will do that and my life will be better and I will be more successful and so much happier!—and then found yourself three weeks later behaving in the same way you always have. Insight is necessary but not sufficient. What we are betting on is something more modest and more durable: a shift in orientation.

Here is the theory of change behind everything that follows.

First, we are asking you to see friction as a signal rather than a problem. That reframe is the foundation. Without it, the experiments in this section are just tactics. With it, they become a way of reading your own experience.

Second, we are asking you to get curious about the friction in your life. Not every hard thing is a lesson in disguise—we said that in Part I and we meant it. But most friction is pointing at something. When you start approaching tension with genuine curiosity instead of reflexive avoidance or brute-force resolution, you give yourself something you didn’t have before: a moment of choice.

Third, we are asking you to try something. Pick an experiment. Try it. See what happens. Adjust. The willingness to experiment, to treat your own leadership as a laboratory rather than a performance, is what turns orientation and curiosity into actual change. We aren’t saying this isn’t challenging. In leadership, especially formal roles, it can feel like you are always “in the game” with no “practice time.” There is a mindset shift required to approach leadership like an ongoing set of experiments, but we have seen amazing transformations in people who have committed to this shift.

That’s it. See it differently, get curious, and try something. Those three moves, practiced over time, are what this section is designed to support.

Pause Here

Before you charge into the experiments, it is worth taking a few minutes to do something most readers skip. Think about where you actually want to start.

Ask yourself:

•  Where is the friction in my life and leadership right now? Not in theory. Specifically.

°  Is it internal—the spinning, the dread, the identity pressure, the way you brace before certain conversations?

°  Is it interpersonal—the relationship that has accumulated unspoken energy, the feedback you are not giving, the trust that has quietly eroded?

°  Is it systemic—the meeting load, the norm no one named, the incentive structure that is asking people to do one thing while rewarding another?

•  What would I most like to change? If something in Part I landed hard (a data point, a story, a finding that felt uncomfortably familiar), start there. That resonance is telling you something about where your edge is.

•  What am I actually willing to try? This is the most honest question. Don’t pick an experiment that sounds most impressive or most thorough; pick what you might actually do, this week, in the middle of your real life.

You don’t need to answer all of these before you proceed. But pausing to ask them, even for five minutes, will make the difference between reading Part 2 and using it.

How This Section Is Organized

Part 2 is a field guide. Most leaders don’t struggle due to lack of insight. They struggle because insight alone doesn’t hold up under pressure. So, rather than giving you more frameworks to analyze, what follows is a set of experiments to play with.

We’ve organized Part 2 by type of friction. The three sections that follow correspond to where the friction comes from:

•  Internal friction: When you’re stuck in your own head or body—we’ll explore things like forecasting doom, bracing, spinning, and letting your emotional response shape your worldview.

•  Interpersonal friction: When you’re tangled up with other people—we’ll consider things like misread tone, defensiveness, conflict, repair, and trust.

•  System friction: When your environment is shaping your experience—we’ll examine things like norms, incentives, meeting load, tools, policies, and the costs of coordination.

Within each section, the experiments are grouped into three tiers.

•  The first five experiments are Hacks or quick interventions that involve low time investment and can be applied immediately. Start here if you are in the middle of something and need a tool today.

•  The second five experiments are Habits or practices worth building over weeks or months. They may require repetition before they become second nature, but results compound over time.

•  The last five experiments are Hard Things, what we consider longer-term structural changes. They require more sustained effort and often involve other people or systems.

A Few Guardrails Before You Begin

Maggie: If you’ve ever read something or heard someone explain an idea and thought—wait, I’ve been trying to say that for years—you’ll understand what happened to me when I read Anne-Laure le Cunff’s Tiny Experiments. I’ve been encouraging leaders to “micro-experiment” since my early days facilitating programs at the Center for Creative Leadership. Don’t wait for the perfect plan, I used to say. Try something small, get a new dataset, and adjust. Anne articulated that spirit beautifully, and it gave me language for something I’d seen over and over again: When you can shift from control to curiosity, you can discover something unexpected about yourself. The experiments can set you free to try.

Don’t read Part 2 straight through. The strategies offered in this book are not a curriculum to complete. And we’re certain that trying to read it straight through will begin to feel overwhelming.

There are so many things to try, so many things that are possible. Go exploring. Go snacking. Skim through and see what interests you and what’s relevant to your own experience.

These experiments are designed to be doable even when you are busy and stressed. But simple doesn’t mean easy. These will take your time, energy, and attention. You are not proving you are good at emotions. You are noticing what reliably happens in you, between people, and inside the systems you’re a part of and/or lead.

If you feel like you do an experiment “wrong,” congratulations: You’ve done the most leadership-like thing possible and learned something in motion. As le Cunff puts it, “not all experiments need to happen inside the problem space … experiments help when the problem is too painful to touch directly.” Try something you don’t think could work, you might surprise yourself.

If you feel like you do an experiment “wrong,” congratulations: You’ve done the most leadership-like thing possible and learned something in motion.

And if something doesn’t resonate, abandon it, and try something else. That’s data too. It isn’t about collecting right answers; you’re here to find what reliably helps you lead with more choice in any given moment.
