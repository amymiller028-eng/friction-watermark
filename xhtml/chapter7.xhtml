CHAPTER 7 SYSTEMIC FRICTION EXPERIMENTS

Some friction is not personal and not interpersonal, it is baked into the design of your meetings, your processes, your org chart, your culture. System friction can be the hardest kind to see at times because it tends to feel like just “the way things are.”

The experiments in this section invite you to look at the systems you operate within and, where you have influence, work to redesign them. Some of these are quick fixes, a single meeting changed, a single process simplified. Some are longer-term investments in the kind of organization you are trying to build. All of them start with the question: Is this system designed for the humans inside it?

Just as with the first two chapters of experiments, you don’t need to start at the top of this list. If your team is buried in meetings that produce more friction than progress, Design Meetings with Emotion in Mind or Make the Right Things Easy are the fastest on-ramps. If you suspect people are burning out but you can’t quite see it yet, start with Track Burnout Like a Business Metric or Build Human Buffer Zones. If you’re working on something more foundational—the kind of culture change that takes quarters, not weeks—Values That Do Something, Your People System is One Thing, and Protect What AI Can’t Replace are where you’ll find interesting things to work on. The fifteen experiments below move from quick interventions toward longer-term structural changes, but the right place to begin is wherever the system you’re in is costing people the most.

Design Meetings with Emotion in Mind

Most meetings begin the same way. Someone shares their screen, someone else joins late and apologizes, a third person joins and starts talking but they are on mute. “You are on mute,” a chorus that has become synonymous with post-pandemic corporate, working world. The agenda appears. Before a single substantive word has been said, the room has already decided what kind of meeting this is going to be. The friction has already started. A quiet, ambient friction that signals to everyone present that their time and attention are not being taken seriously. It accumulates invisibly, meeting by meeting, virtual or in person, until people stop arriving fully and start arriving defensively.

This is a systemic problem in most organizations. Priya Parker, in her book The Art of Gathering, makes an argument that most leaders have never seriously considered: The way we gather matters as much as why we gather, and most of us are gathering on autopilot, importing formats and habits we inherited without ever asking whether they serve the people in the room. Parker’s central provocation is that a gathering without a specific, defensible purpose is not a neutral event. It is an actively bad one—one that consumes time and trust while producing the illusion of connection and progress without the substance of either.

The opening of a gathering, from a team standup to a company all-hands, sets the cognitive and emotional conditions for everything that follows. How people arrive, what they attend to first, what emotional register they enter, what they believe is expected of them: All of this is shaped in the first five minutes, mostly by accident. The question is not whether your meetings have an opening culture. They do. The question is whether you designed it.

The science here is explicit. Every gathering either builds or spends the organizational trust that makes hard work possible.

Ritual amplifies this further. Research from Harvard Business School found that performing group rituals can enhance the meaningfulness of work, and that this enhanced meaning produces downstream increases in organizational citizenship behaviors. Group rituals characterized by physical action, psychological significance, and communality (even small ones) change how people experience what comes after them. The first five minutes of a well-designed meeting can function as exactly this kind of ritual: a shared signal that something intentional is beginning, that the people in the room matter, and that what happens here is worth being present for.

Steve Jobs understood this viscerally. At Apple’s 2002 Worldwide Developers Conference, Jobs faced a stubborn organizational problem. Developers were deeply attached Fugue to Mac OS 9, the classic operating system Apple needed them to abandon in order to fully embrace OS X. Argument had not worked. Encouragement had not worked. So Jobs opened the conference with a mock funeral. A coffin rose from the stage. Bach’s Toccata and in D Minor played through the hall. Stained glass lit up the slides. Jobs eulogized Mac OS 9 as “a friend to us, always at our beck and call.” The ceremony was theatrical, absurd, and completely deliberate. Before a single technical session had begun, he had reframed the entire cultural moment: We are not abandoning something. We are burying it with dignity and moving forward.

That is the power of the first five minutes at scale. He knew that the emotional and symbolic opening of the gathering would do work that no amount of subsequent persuasion could accomplish. The coffin did not make the case for OS X. It made people ready to hear it.

Parker would recognize this immediately. She argues that the best gatherers are not those who run the tightest agendas; they are those who understand that before the content, there is a room full of people who have come from somewhere else, carrying something else, and who need to be brought across a threshold into the shared space you are trying to create. That threshold moment is the opening, but most leaders leave it entirely to chance.

Most leaders do not need a coffin. But they do need to ask what their openings are actually doing. A meeting that begins with a check-in question, genuinely asked, genuinely heard, signals that the people in the room are more than their function. A meeting that begins with a moment of explicit purpose—why are we here, what are we deciding, what would good look like—signals that this time is intentional, not obligatory. A meeting that begins with the same rushed, defaulted, apologetic scramble signals something too: that no one thought carefully about this, and no one should expect that to change.

The friction that accumulates inside organizations is rarely dramatic. It is the slow erosion of people who stop expecting gatherings to be meaningful, who arrive defended, distracted, and already half-checked out because experience has taught them that is the rational response. “Meetings that could have been an email” is a popular vent from employees across the globe.a Every gathering either builds or spends trust the organizational trust that makes hard work possible. Designing the first five minutes is how you decide which, and also how you briefly demonstrate cultural values.

The Experiment: Run One Meeting Differently

Duration: One week

Time required: 15 minutes of preparation, 5 minutes at the start of the meeting, 10 minutes to debrief afterward

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Run one meeting differently, paying close attention to what changes and drawing conclusions from that.

Step One: Choose the Meeting

Pick one recurring meeting you own or co-own, ideally one that has developed a default, unremarkable opening. A team standup, a weekly sync, a project check-in. Something ordinary enough that people have stopped noticing how it begins.

Step Two: Design the Opening

Spend 15 minutes before the meeting answering two questions:

•  What do I want people to feel or think in the first five minutes, not about the agenda, but about this gathering and the people in it?

•  What is one specific thing I can do in the first five minutes to produce that?

The design does not need to be elaborate. Some of the most effective openings are simply a single well-chosen question asked slowly and genuinely. Others involve naming the purpose of the gathering with unusual specificity, not “we’re here to discuss the project” but, “we’re here to make one decision today and I want us to leave aligned on it.” Others involve acknowledging something true about the moment: the pressure the team is under, a win worth naming, a transition underway.

What matters is that it is intentional, not improvised. You are choosing what the first signal will be.

Some options to consider:

•  A check-in question—something specific enough to require a real answer. Not “how is everyone?” but “what is one word for how you are arriving today?” or “what is something that happened this week that is worth naming before we get into the work?”

•  An explicit statement of purpose—what this meeting is for, what it is not for, and what you need from the people in the room.

•  A brief acknowledgment—of the context everyone is operating in, the stakes of what you are deciding, or the progress the team has made.

•  A transition ritual—something consistent and brief that signals the group has shifted from wherever they came from into this shared space.

Step Three: Run It and Notice

During the meeting, pay attention to two things:

•  When did people arrive, not physically, but attentionally? When did you feel the room engage?

•  Did the opening produce the condition you designed for?

You do not need to share these observations with the group during the meeting. Just notice.

Step Four: Debrief

Immediately after the meeting—within an hour—write a short debrief answering the following:

•  What did I design for, and did the opening deliver it?

•  What did I notice about the energy or quality of the meeting compared to the default version?

•  What would I keep, change, or try differently next time?

If you can, ask one person who was in the meeting what they noticed about how it began. You do not need to explain that you designed it. Just ask: “How did this meeting feel to you compared to usual?” The gap between your design intent and their experience is where the most useful information lives.

Pay particular attention to the moment the room shifts. In a well-opened meeting, that moment often comes earlier than expected, sometimes within the first 60 seconds. In a poorly opened one, it may never come at all, which is its own finding worth sitting with.

What do you notice becomes possible? Honest disagreement. A question someone had been holding. A quieter voice that rarely joins in. What you build over time besides just better meetings, it is a team that has learned, through repeated experience, that showing up fully is worth it.

Make the Right Things Easy

Before a professional kitchen produces a single plate of food, the chef has already made hundreds of decisions. Where every tool lives. What is prepped, portioned, and within reach. What order things happen in. The French call it mise en place, everything in its place, and it is not a prep technique so much as a philosophy of design. Adnan Ibrahim knows this as well as anyone. He came up through the ranks at Petit Trois, a French Los Angeles treasure, eventually reaching executive sous chef before moving on to lead the kitchen at Elephante. “Mise en place is about self-accountability,” he says. “For a chef, it is the last bit of control they have in an uncontrollable situation. You never know what the shift or service will bring, but you know you have given yourself what’s necessary to get through it.” A kitchen that runs on mise en place does not rely on people making good decisions under pressure. It relies on an environment so deliberately arranged that the right actions become nearly automatic and the wrong ones require extra effort to make.

Most organizations work exactly the opposite way.

In most workplaces, the easiest thing a person can do is schedule a meeting, add someone to a distribution list, launch a new initiative, or generate a report that accumulates without anyone quite deciding it was needed. The things that make the organization heavier and slower pile up not because anyone chooses them, but because they required no effort to begin and considerable political effort to stop. And because no one designed it otherwise, that is the path people take, not out of laziness or bad intent, but because that is how defaults work.

Earlier, we talked about The Friction Projectb and where we agree with Sutton and Rao’s framing and where we see it differently. Here is an experiment that lives squarely in the territory where we agree. Whatever else friction is trying to tell you, there is a category of organizational friction that is pure cost: the accumulated weight of rules, red tape, pointless meetings, and cascading approvals that make the right behaviors hard and the wrong ones effortless. The authors call this out with precision, and their core prescription is worth taking seriously: The best-designed environments make the right things easy and the wrong things hard. Most organizations do neither deliberately. This experiment asks you to do it deliberately, starting with your own corner of the system.

This is a leadership problem, not a management problem. The distinction matters. Management optimizes within existing systems. Leadership asks whether the systems themselves are pointing people in the right direction. A leader who adopts the mise en place mindset is not asking “How do we get people to do the right things?” They are asking something harder and more useful: “Have we arranged the environment so that the right things are inevitable?”

The answer, in most organizations, is no. But the good news is that the levers are more concrete than they appear.

Consider what some of the most operationally serious companies have done with something as mundane as meetings. In early 2023, Shopify canceled all recurring meetings with more than two people and instituted no-meeting Wednesdays, two moves that cut 10,000 events, or the equivalent of more than 76,500 hours of meetings, from their calendars. The company’s COO framed it explicitly as “useful subtraction.” Engineering, product, and user experience teams were projected to complete around 25% more projects by year’s end under the new policy. Shopify did not motivate its people to focus more. It removed the structural conditions that made fragmentation the default.

At Asana, a “meeting doomsday” exercise asked employees to identify recurring meetings that lacked value and remove all standing meetings with fewer than five attendees from their calendars for 48 hours. A few months later, most 30-minute meetings had converted to 15 minutes, some weekly meetings had moved to biweekly or monthly, and others had been deleted entirely. Each person was saving an average of 11 hours per month, roughly 3.5 workweeks per year.

Neither of these companies changed their people, they changed their people’s defaults.

The same logic applies far beyond meetings. Some companies protect the first two hours of the workday from all internal interruptions—no meetings, no Slack, no requests—so that the hardest cognitive work gets done before the reactive work begins. Google and Microsoft have both built Focus Time features directly into their calendar tools that automatically block off that window, treating protected concentration as a structural default rather than a personal act of willpower.

But here is the honest friction underneath all of this: Some of us do not want protection from meetings. We want more of them.

Maggie: I am an extroverted processor. I have never met a meeting I did not like, mostly because meetings mean people, and people are where I come alive. When the Center for Creative Leadership’s San Diego campus moved to an open-concept floor plan, I had to self-impose work-from-home daysc just to get focused work done. If I could hear two people talking anywhere in the building, I felt my body physically pulled out of my chair to go find out what they were talking about. The structural problem was not that I lacked discipline. It was that the environment was designed in a way that made distraction inevitable for someone wired like me. The right design depends entirely on knowing which behaviors you are trying to protect, and for whom.

Others have adopted what productivity expert Brian Tracy popularized as the “eat the frog” rule: the most important, most avoided task goes first, before the day has a chance to fragment around it, based on the principle that cognitive resources are sharpest early and that starting with the hardest thing removes its weight from the rest of the day. Amazon famously requires meeting organizers to prepare a written six-page narrative memo rather than a slide deck, forcing thinking to happen before the room assembles rather than during it. As Bezos explained in a 2004 email to his team that became company policy: The narrative structure of a good memo forces better thought than a PowerPoint ever could, because slides allow the presenter to gloss over ideas while prose exposes the logic, or the gaps in it. These are structural decisions, friction deliberately placed in front of the wrong behaviors and deliberately removed from in front of the right ones.

A 2022 MIT Sloan Management Review study found that companies implementing no-meeting days in the work week reduced total meetings by 40% and saw measurable increases in autonomy, communication, engagement, and productivity. When the environment is designed to protect focused work, focused work happens. When it is not, fragmentation fills the space, because fragmentation is always easier than concentration, and concentration requires structure to survive.

Sutton and Rao describe one of the most persistent failure modes in organizations as “addition sickness,” the tendency to solve problems by adding rather than subtracting. New process, new initiative, new meeting, new approval layer. Addition feels productive. Subtraction feels risky, because somewhere there is a person who owns the thing you are trying to remove. This asymmetry means that bad friction compounds over time while good friction, the kind that slows down unethical decisions, forces genuine deliberation, or protects people’s time for deep work, rarely gets built in deliberately.

The mise en place leader inverts this. They are asking what to remove, what to protect, and what to arrange so that the behaviors the organization most needs are the ones that require the least effort to perform. They are, in the most literal sense, designing for the inevitable.

The kitchen does not produce great food because the cooks are trying harder. It produces great food because the environment was set up so that great food was the most likely outcome. That is the standard worth building toward.

The Experiment: The Friction Audit

Duration: One week

Time required: 30 minutes for the audit, 10 minutes daily for observation

Materials: A notebook or notes app divided into two columns

Goal: This experiment asks you to map the friction landscape of your working environment. The goal is to surface the gap between the behaviors you want and the behaviors the environment is set up to produce.

Step One: The Two-Column Map

Draw two columns. Label the first Easy and the second Hard.

In the Easy column, list every recurring behavior, action, or decision in your team or organization that currently requires minimal effort. Do not filter for whether these things are good or bad, just list what the path of least resistance actually produces. Some examples to prompt your thinking include the following:

•  Adding people to meetings

•  Sending status update emails

•  Creating new Slack channels

•  Launching new workstreams

•  Requesting someone’s time

•  Generating decks

•  Approving small expenditures

•  Saying yes to requests

In the Hard column, list the behaviors that your team or organization genuinely values but that currently require significant effort, coordination, or willpower to execute. Again, be honest rather than aspirational. Some prompts include the following:

•  Protecting two uninterrupted hours for deep work

•  Canceling a meeting that has outlived its purpose

•  Saying no to a low-priority request

•  Getting a decision made without a meeting

•  Finishing one priority before starting another

•  Documenting a decision so it does not need to be re-litigated

Step Two: The Inversion Question

Look at both columns and ask: Which items in the Hard column should be easy, and which items in the Easy column should be harder or should not exist at all?

This is the diagnostic at the heart of the audit; you are looking for the highest-leverage misalignments and the places where the environment is most actively working against the outcomes you care about.

Circle no more than three items: one from the Easy column that should be harder or eliminated, and one or two from the Hard column that should be easier or protected by design.

Step Three: One Design Decision

For each circled item, ask: What is the smallest structural change that would shift this?

Some examples of what this can look like in practice include the following:

•  Blocking the first 90 minutes of every workday on the team calendar so meetings cannot be scheduled during that window

•  Requiring anyone who calls a meeting to state the decision that will be made in it

•  Removing yourself from distribution lists that do not require your action

•  Setting a recurring meeting to biweekly instead of weekly and seeing if anything breaks

The difference between a cultural aspiration and a mise en place intervention is that the aspiration requires people to remember to do the right thing, while the intervention makes the right thing the thing that happens by default.

One Less Click, One More Human Signal

At some point in the last decade, the performance review stopped being a conversation and became a process. A platform to log into. A rubric to complete. Competency ratings on a five-point scale. Development goals entered into fields with character limits. A due date that generates automated reminders until the form is submitted and the notification clears. The system was designed to make performance management more consistent, more trackable, and more defensible. What it produced, in many organizations, is a form of elaborate theater in which the documentation of performance has entirely replaced the experience of it.

The logical endpoint of this trajectory is already here. Managers, exhausted by the operational weight of the review cycle, are now using AI to write performance reviews for their direct reports. The irony is almost too neat: A system designed to capture human judgment about human performance has become so friction-heavy that humans have quietly exited the building. What remains is a completed form, A closed notification, and an employee who received a review that their manager did not write about work that an algorithm did not witness.

This is what over-systematized employee experience looks like. The original intent was reasonable. Consistency matters. Documentation matters. But somewhere in the design process, the system optimized for completion rather than connection, and the thing that was supposed to support the conversation became a replacement for it.

The fix is not to tear the system down. It is to find the places where a single human signal, a specific observation, a direct question, an unscripted moment of acknowledgment, can be reintroduced without blowing up the process around it. One less click means finding a step in the workflow that exists for the system’s benefit rather than the employee’s and removing it. One more human signal means finding the moment in the process where a real exchange could happen and protecting it.

In the case of performance reviews, this might look like a standing norm that the written review is never the first time an employee hears an assessment of their work. Or a requirement that every review form includes one sentence the manager wrote without a template. Or simply a conversation before the form, so that the documentation reflects something that was genuinely discussed rather than something that was composed in a browser tab and submitted before the deadline.

Small changes. But they signal something important: that the person on the other side of the process is a person, and that their manager noticed.

The Experiment: Finding Clicks and Signals

Duration: One week

Time required: 30 minutes upfront, 10 minutes per touchpoint during the experiment

Materials: A notebook or notes app, and access to one employee experience process you own or influence

Goal: Find one place in a team process where a step can be removed and one place where a human moment can be added back in.

Step One: Pick Your Process (10 Minutes)

Choose one recurring employee experience touchpoint you are responsible for or have influence over. It could be performance reviews, onboarding, one-on-ones, project retrospectives, or any process that involves both a system and a person. Write down:

•  What does this process ask of the manager or team member at each step?

•  Which steps exist for the system’s benefit rather than the employee’s?

•  When was the last time someone said this process felt meaningful rather than just completed?

Step Two: Find The One Less Click (10 Minutes)

Look at the process and identify one step that could be removed, simplified, or made optional without losing anything that genuinely matters. It does not need to be dramatic. Examples:

•  A rating field that generates data no one looks at

•  A required comment box that everyone fills with a sentence copied from the one above it

•  An approval step that adds a week to the timeline and changes nothing about the outcome

Write down what you would remove and what, honestly, would be lost. If the answer is nothing, remove it.

Step Three: Find the One More Human Signal (10 Minutes)

Now identify one moment in the same process where a direct human exchange could be added or restored. Keep it small and specific. Examples:

•  A norm that the manager shares one specific observation before the written review is submitted

•  A standing opening question in the review conversation: “What do you want me to know about this period that the form won’t capture?”

•  A handwritten or untemplated note sent after a significant milestone

•  A five-minute check-in the day after a hard review conversation

Write down exactly what you will add, when it will happen, and what would prevent you from doing it.

Step Four: Run it Once and Notice

Apply both changes to the next instance of the process. Afterward, note:

•  What did removing the step cost, if anything?

•  What did adding the human signal produce, if anything?

•  How did the person on the receiving end respond?

Reflection prompt: Think about the last time a process you manage produced a completed form but not a real conversation. What was the system optimizing for, and what did it cost the person on the other end? What is one signal you could add this week that would tell someone on your team that you noticed them?

The Unsent Resignation Letter

Exit interviews are, by design, postmortems. An employee has already made the emotional decision to leave, has already rehearsed the story they’ll tell their friends, and has already decided what they will not say out loud because they don’t want to burn a bridge. Employers ask for the truth after the consequences are set. (And when leaders don’t act on what they hear, people learn a quiet lesson: Don’t bother next time.)

Stay interviews are the more hopeful cousin. They ask, “What makes you stay?” and “What might tempt you to leave?,” while there’s still time to change the conditions. Research and practice-oriented evidence suggest they can surface retention drivers earlier, especially when they are done consistently rather than as a one-off initiative. But even stay interviews often have a problem: They depend on what someone can name in the moment, out loud, to another human being whose paycheck is signed by the same system they’re about to critique.

That’s why the “unsent resignation letter” works differently. It borrows the structure of resignation—a culturally familiar container for truth—and removes the finality. Instead, the person gets to write from the ledge without having to jump. It invites clarity. It organizes messy experience into a coherent narrative: Here’s what I’ve been carrying. Here’s what I tried. Here’s what tipped the scale. And because it’s written, not spoken, it also bypasses a common organizational failure mode: Only the most articulate, most confident, or most politically safe people end up shaping the story of what’s broken.

Writing changes what we can see.

Psychologists have studied versions of expressive writing for decades—brief, structured writing about stressful or emotionally significant experiences. The effects are not magic and not uniform; meta-analyses find small-to-moderate benefits and lots of nuance about who benefits, when, and for what outcomes. And in work-stress contexts, results can be mixed, reminding us that writing alone doesn’t replace structural change. But the mechanism is still powerful. Writing helps people translate diffuse experience into language, and language is one of our best tools for turning emotion into information.

Neuroscience adds a helpful layer: “putting feelings into words” (which you’ll recall is known as affect labeling) can reduce emotional reactivity in the brain by shifting activity toward regulatory regions in the prefrontal cortex and away from the amygdala. In plain terms: Naming what’s happening can dial down the alarm system enough for the thinking brain to come back online. Not because the problem disappears, but because the nervous system stops treating it like an immediate threat.

Burnout is rarely a single person’s failure; it’s a systemic one, shaped by job demands, resources, and the social context of work. We examine this in depth in Track Burnout Like a Business Metric. What matters here is the diagnostic implication. The unsent resignation letter often reveals exactly where the system is creating the conditions burnout research predicts.

Policies can look neutral on paper and still produce predictable friction in practice. A meeting norm can be “just how we do things” while quietly privileging the loudest voices. A performance metric can incentivize urgency while punishing reflection. A “flexible” culture can be flexible only for the people with the most power. These are systemic frictions, but they don’t always announce themselves as systemic. They show up as private exhaustion and quiet resentment.

The unsent resignation letter is a visibility tool. It makes the invisible visible at two levels at once: inside the person and inside the organization.

Inside the person, it reveals where they may have been splitting themselves in two.

Sociologists have a term for one kind of splitting: emotional labor—the effort of managing feelings and expressions to meet workplace expectations. A large meta-analysis found that “surface acting” (faking the required emotion while feeling something else) is reliably associated with worse well-being and more exhaustion, the cost of chronic self-editing is real. Over time, a person can become a slightly smaller version of themselves at work, more careful, more muted, more guarded, until the role starts to feel like a costume they can’t take off.

This is where the letter does something tender and specific. It gives people permission to tell the truth without having to make a case, win an argument, or defend their feelings in real time. It helps them see what they want, what they’ve tolerated, what they’ve normalized, and what they’ve been calling “fine”d because naming it felt too risky.

And then comes the organizational level.

What leaders often miss is that an employee’s “reason for leaving” is rarely a single reason. It’s more like a braid: a little inner friction (identity, meaning, energy), a little interpersonal friction (trust, respect, conflict), and a little systemic friction (work design, incentives, process, fairness). When we only talk about retention in individual terms—“She wanted growth,” “He didn’t like his manager,” we miss the pattern. We reduce what could be diagnosed to what is merely anecdotal.

When leaders read across a set of unsent resignation letters (or, more safely, a de-identified synthesis of themes), they’re essentially doing organizational sensemaking. Karl Weick described sensemaking as the process of extracting cues from lived experience and building a shared, plausible story that allows coordinated action. The letters are cues. They are lived data. And they often reveal the gap between the organization’s stated values and its operational defaults.

This matters because voice is about courage as much as it is about conditions. Without psychological safety, which we examine in depth in Build the Felt Experience, the system doesn’t get accurate information about itself, which brings us back to why this belongs in the System Friction section.

The unsent resignation letter experiment can be a diagnostic. It tells the truth about where the system creates unnecessary load:

•  where processes steal attention,

•  where policies punish boundaries,

•  where incentives reward the wrong behavior,

•  where cultural norms create emotional labor,

•  where managers are under-supported, and

•  where teams are drowning in coordination costs.

It also reveals where the problem is not systemic:

•  where a dyad needs repair,

•  where role clarity is missing, and

•  where expectations were never negotiated.

And it does one more important thing: It prevents leaders from outsourcing “emotion work” back onto employees.

If you invite people to disclose the truth and then do nothing with it, you’ve merely extracted vulnerability. That’s not repair; that’s consumption. The ethical core of this practice is follow-through: Leaders must be willing to change defaults, not just collect stories. As medicine has learned through narrative practice, stories are both cathartic and informational, and they carry context that lab values can’t.

The real intervention isn’t the letter; the real intervention is what the letter makes visible, and what leaders do next.

The Experiment: Run an Unsent Resignation Letter Audit

Duration: One session plus two-week follow-up

Time required: 15 minutes for individual writing, 60–90 minutes for team synthesis

Materials: A shared document or private prompt sheet; optional neutral facilitator

Goal: Surface hidden frictions (inner, interpersonal, systemic), then separate what needs policy/process change from what needs team/manager intervention.

Step One: Create Real Psychological Safety

•  Put in writing: no retaliation, no “performance consequences,” no “who said this” hunting.

•  Offer two paths: private letter (for self) and shareable letter (for the organization).

•  If trust is low, use a neutral facilitator to collect letters and produce a de-identified theme report. (Don’t skip this if people are scared.)

Step Two: The Prompt (10–15 Minutes)

Ask people to write two letters:

Letter A: “If I Resigned in Six Months …”

Finish these stems:

•  “I’d be leaving because …”

•  “I’ve tried to address this by …”

•  “The moment I realized it wasn’t sustainable was …”

•  “The part I don’t say out loud is …”

•  “If nothing changes, the cost to me will be …”

Letter B: “If I Chose to Stay …”

Finish these stems:

•  “I stay when …”

•  “The best version of me at work looks like …”

•  “The smallest change that would make the biggest difference is …”

•  “A boundary I need respected is …”

•  “A policy/process that quietly makes things harder is …”

•  Complete this sentence: “I don’t want to leave the company. I want to stop becoming ___ here.”

Step Three: Individual Reflection (Make the Invisible Visible to the Person)

Have each person label their friction in three buckets:

•  Inner: energy, meaning, identity, values, capacity

•  Interpersonal: trust, conflict, communication, fairness, respect

•  Systemic: workload design, tools, meeting norms, policies, incentives, staffing, decision rights

Then ask:

“What part is mine to own?” and “What part is the system’s to fix?”

Step Four: Organizational Synthesis (Make the Invisible Visible to the System)

Leaders (or the facilitator) code themes into:

•  Policy/process fixes (e.g., meeting load, unclear priorities, unclear decision rights, staffing math, inequitable flexibility)

•  Manager enablement needs (coaching, role clarity, feedback routines, conflict support)

•  Team norm repairs (how decisions happen, how conflict happens, how work is handed off)

Step Five: Close the Loop in 14 Days

Within two weeks, publish:

•  Top three themes you heard

•  1–2 changes you will make now

•  1 longer-term change you will evaluate

•  What you are not changing and why (this builds trust; ambiguity destroys it)

Step Six: Repeat Quarterly (Because Systems Drift)

System friction isn’t solved once. It’s managed. Schedule it like maintenance.

Track Burnout Like a Business Metric

Burnout has become so common it seems almost unavoidable. Everyone is feeling burned out, recovering from burnout, or worried about burning out. What’s interesting is that it’s so predictable we can see it coming, and yet we keep treating it like a surprise.

Ross: During the writing of this book, my wife and I had our third child. In the hospital, near the nurses’ station, there was a bulletin board that caught my eye. Two pieces of paper were posted there. The first listed “common signs of burnout”:

•  Constant exhaustion, no matter how much you rest

•  Dreading responsibilities you once enjoyed

•  Feeling detached, unmotivated, or emotionally numb

•  Struggling to focus or making more mistakes than usual

•  Feeling irritable, anxious, or overwhelmed more often than not

•  Frequent physical symptoms like headaches, digestive issues, or muscle tension

The second listed “ways to combat burnout”:

•  Take time off

•  Set boundaries

•  Prioritize rest

•  Seek support through self-care and professional help

The nurses in that hospital were great, and the first list is genuinely useful. But the second list is where the problem lives. Every item on it places the solution squarely on the individual. Burned out? Rest more. Set better limits. Take a vacation. The implicit message is that burnout is something you developed, and therefore something you need to fix.

But burnout is not primarily an individual failure. It is a systemic one. Research by Christina Maslach, whose work remains the foundation of burnout science, identifies three core dimensions of burnout: exhaustion, cynicism, and a reduced sense of efficacy. Critically, Maslach’s research locates the causes of burnout not in individual weakness but in mismatches between people and the conditions of their work: unsustainable workload, lack of control, insufficient reward, breakdown of community, absence of fairness, and values conflicts. Fix the person without fixing the system and you are putting a bandage on a broken limb.

This matters for leaders because burnout is information. It tells you something about how your system is designed, what it asks of people, and whether its structure sustains performance over time. Organizations routinely track turnover, absenteeism, and engagement. Burnout deserves the same treatment. When you begin measuring strain and depletion as operating data rather than as private struggles individuals should manage quietly, you gain visibility into where your system extracts more than it returns. That visibility is the first step toward changing it.

Taking time off may provide temporary relief. But sustainable performance requires sustainable conditions. The leaders who get this right are not the ones who tell their teams to practice better self-care. They are the ones who look at depletion data and ask: What is our system doing to produce this, and what can we change?

The Experiment: The Burnout Audit

Duration: Two weeks

Time required: 30 minutes upfront, 5 minutes per day

Materials: A notebook or notes app, and if you manage a team, access to any existing engagement or pulse survey data

Goal: Begin treating burnout signals as measurable operating data and identify at least one systemic factor contributing to strain on your team or in yourself.

Step One: Establish Your Baseline (30 Minutes to Start)

Using Maslach’s classic three dimensions as your framework, rate yourself or your team honestly on each:

•  Exhaustion: How depleted do people seem at the end of the week, on a scale of 1 to 10?

•  Cynicism: How much genuine investment or enthusiasm do you observe in the work, on a scale of 1 to 10?

•  Efficacy: How often do people feel like their work is making a real difference, on a scale of 1 to 10?

Write two or three sentences describing what you observe in each area with the goal of simply establishing a starting point.

Now write your answers to these questions (bonus points for taking the time to ask and learn from your team—you may not have good insight into these answers):

•  What is the workload asking of people right now?

•  Where do people have control over how they work, and where do they not?

•  Where is recognition or reward out of step with effort?

•  Where is there friction in how the team works together?

Step Two: Track the Signals Daily (Two Weeks, Five Minutes Per Day)

Each day, note one observation about strain or depletion, in yourself or on your team. It does not need to be dramatic. Examples: “Three people mentioned feeling behind in our standup.” “I noticed I have been dreading my one-on-ones this week.” “Someone made an error they would not normally make.”

You are building a small data set, not writing a diary. Keep entries brief and specific.

Step Three: Look for the Pattern (End of Week Two, 15 Minutes)

Review your daily notes and ask:

•  Where do the signals cluster? Is it a particular day, meeting, role, or type of work?

•  Which of Maslach’s six sources (workload, control, reward, community, fairness, values) seems most present?

•  What is the system doing to produce this?

Step Four: Name One Systemic Change

Based on what you observed, identify one concrete change to a condition of work, not a suggestion for individual self-care, that could reduce strain. It does not need to be large. Examples: redistributing a particular type of task, eliminating a meeting that generates anxiety without producing value, or creating a standing space for people to name what is hard without it being treated as a performance issue.

Write it down. Share it with someone who can help you act on it.

Reflection prompt: If burnout showed up on your organization’s dashboard the way turnover or revenue does, what would the number be right now? What would change about how leadership talked about it? What is one thing you have been treating as an individual problem that might actually be a system problem?

Build Human Buffer Zones

In the early 2000s, open floor plan offices became the dominant design choice in American workplaces, driven largely by the example of massively successful tech companies like Google that were flattening hierarchies, removing private offices, and betting that proximity would produce collaboration. The logic made sense on its face: Reduce the friction of interaction, make leadership more visible and approachable, and let the energy of the room do some of the work.

But what has that design choice actually produced?

Ross: I still work with clients whose executive teams sit at open desks on the floor alongside everyone else. There are real benefits to this. It has a democratizing effect, makes management more approachable, and pulls leadership out of the corner office mythology that had accumulated for decades. But what I notice in almost every one of these offices is that the executive team still has access to private conference rooms where they can meet without being seen or overheard. And the open seating tends to create informal no-go zones for everyone else. The CEO may be at a standing desk on the floor, but that does not mean you feel comfortable walking over there. The friction of hierarchy did not disappear. It just became invisible.

What got lost in the move toward openness is something more basic: People need different kinds of space to do different kinds of work. Not every task benefits from ambient noise and the awareness of being observed. A salesperson taking a difficult client call with colleagues sitting three feet away is not working in an open, collaborative environment. They are performing in public. A manager delivering hard feedback in a glass-walled conference room is not having a private conversation. They are staging one.

One company that has thought carefully about this is Switchyards, a co-working concept based in Atlanta. Within their spaces they offer quiet zones for focused individual work, phone booths for private calls, and open format areas for louder, more collaborative conversation. What makes it work is not any single design choice but the acknowledgment that different kinds of work require different conditions, and that giving people agency over which environment they use is itself a form of respect.

This matters most in high-stakes moments. Change announcements, difficult feedback, restructuring conversations, bad news of any kind: These are moments when people need space to process before they are expected to perform again. When organizations deliver hard news in an open room and then ask people to return immediately to their desks and carry on, they are skipping the buffer. The processing that should happen in a quiet hallway conversation, a brief walk, or a closed-door moment with a trusted colleague gets suppressed instead. It does not disappear. It shows up later as disengagement, rumor, or eroded trust.

Physical space is only half of the design problem. The other half is time.

Temporal buffer zones can be simple. A standing 15-minute gap after any all-hands. A norm that difficult feedback conversations are always followed by an unstructured check-in the next day. A calendar policy that no significant change announcement is scheduled in the last hour of the workday, when there is no time left to process before people go home with it. None of these are complicated. What they require is the deliberate acknowledgment that processing is part of the work, not a distraction from it.

Buffer zones are not a luxury. They are a design feature. The question is whether you are building them deliberately or leaving people to find them on their own.

The Experiment: Making Space

Duration: One week

Time required: 30 minutes upfront, 5 minutes of observation per day

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Identify where your team lacks the physical or temporal buffer space they need to process before performing, and design one deliberate change.

Step One: Map the Moments that Need Buffers (30 Minutes to Start)

Think about the high-pressure moments in your team’s recent experience. Write your answers to these questions:

•  When did you last deliver difficult news, announce a significant change, or ask people to navigate a hard situation? What happened immediately after?

•  Where do people go when they need to think, process, or have a candid conversation? Is that space available to them?

•  Are there conversations happening in your organization that should be private but aren’t? Where are people being asked to perform before they have had a chance to process?

Now identify two or three specific moments or recurring situations where a buffer, physical, temporal, or both is missing.

Step Two: Observe for One Week (Five Minutes Per Day)

Each day, note one observation about how people in your organization are navigating space and pressure. You are looking for the following:

•  Moments when people seem to be suppressing rather than processing

•  Conversations happening in the wrong environment for their content

•  Times when the pace of the day leaves no room between a hard moment and the next demand

Step Three: Design One Buffer (15 Minutes at End of Week)

Based on what you observed, design one concrete change. It can be physical or temporal.

Examples:

•  Designate a specific room or area as a default space for conversation and protect it from being booked for routine meetings.

•  Build a 15-minute buffer into the schedule after any all-hands or change announcement before people return to regular work.

•  Establish a norm that hard feedback conversations are always followed by an unstructured check-in the next day.

•  Create a standing option for team members to request a private space for calls or focused work without having to explain why.

Write down the change, who it affects, and how you will know if it is helping.

Reflection prompt: Think about the last time you delivered difficult news or navigated a significant change with your team. What happened in the hour after? Did people have space to process, or were they expected to perform again immediately? What would you design differently now?

Connection by Design

When we work with executives on team and leadership development, the question of scheduling almost always comes up. When should we do this kind of work? The answer is always the same: during work hours. This sometimes gets pushback. The assumption is that development work competes with real work, that if it is interfering with productivity it can wait, or better yet, happen on a weekend retreat that does not eat into the calendar.

This is shortsighted, and not only because it misunderstands what development work does. A team that grinds through tasks without the ability to communicate clearly, make decisions together, or move past recurring friction will never reach its productive potential, no matter how many hours it logs. Building development into the workday is not a concession to soft skills, it’s a signal that this is part of how we work here, not something bolted on at the margins when there is time.

But this is about more than development. The deeper argument is about connection, and about whether organizations design for it or simply hope it happens.

Most organizations leave belonging to chance. People connect informally, in hallways and over lunch and in the margins of meetings. Those connections are real and valuable. But informal connection has a design problem: It tends to favor the visible, the extroverted, and the already-connected. The people who most need belonging are often the least likely to find it without some structural support. Remote and hybrid workers, newer employees, people in roles that sit at the edges of the org chart: For them, the informal connection that others take for granted is often simply unavailable.

Rhythms, rituals, and shared activity fill that gap. Not as morale initiatives or team-building exercises bolted onto a Friday afternoon, but as designed features of how the team operates. A standing time when the team is in the same place with no agenda other than being together. A regular practice of recognizing what went well, not just what needs fixing. A seasonal offsite that mixes hard work with unstructured time. A simple ritual that marks transitions, welcomes new members, or acknowledges difficulty. These are not luxuries. They are how shared cultural values get built, how trust accumulates across difference, and how teams develop the relational capacity to perform as more than the sum of their parts.

Teams with stronger social connection show higher levels of psychological safety, better information sharing, and more resilient performance under pressure. What is less often noted is that this connection rarely develops on its own in complex, busy organizations. It requires design.

The Experiment: The Connection by Design Sprint

Duration: Two weeks plus one planning session

Time required: 45 minutes upfront, 10 minutes per week during the experiment

Materials: A notebook or notes app, and access to your team’s calendar

Goal: Identify where connection on your team is being left to chance and install one designed rhythm or ritual that makes belonging more likely for everyone.

Step One: Map Your Team’s Connection Landscape (10 Minutes)

This experiment pairs with The Offline Connection Dividend, which contains a connection mapping exercise—four prompts that surface where connection is currently happening and, more importantly, where it isn’t. If you’ve already run that experiment, pull up your map now. If you haven’t, spend 10 minutes writing fast answers to those prompts before continuing. Note which groups or individuals are least likely to appear in your answers. Those are the people your current design is failing.

Step Two: Score Your Connection Ecosystem Across Five Lenses (1 to 5)

Circle your lowest two scores. Those are your design targets.

•  Intentionality: We can explain why we gather.

•  Balance: Hybrid and remote people get genuine belonging, not secondhand access.

•  Cadence: Connection happens predictably, not only by chance.

•  Mixing: Cross-team and cross-level relationships form without special effort or permission.

Step Three: Design One Small Rhythm or Ritual (15 Minutes)

Based on your two lowest scores, design one specific practice you can run for two weeks. Keep it small enough to be able to do it. Examples:

•  An anchor hour once a week where the team is in the same place and nothing gets scheduled over it

•  A standing opening ritual in team meetings where one person shares something worth celebrating or acknowledging

•  A monthly session dedicated to sensemaking and learning, not status updates

•  A norm that new team members have a one-on-one with three people outside their immediate role in their first month

For your chosen practice, complete these sentences:

•  “We are making it slightly harder to skip ________.”

•  “So it becomes easier to ________.”

•  “The smallest two-week version of this is ________.”

•  “The thing most likely to break this is ________, so we will ________.”

Step Four: Run the Pilot for Two Weeks, Then Ask Two Questions

At the end of each week, in a pulse check or in one-on-ones, ask:

•  “Did you feel more supported this week than a typical week?”

•  “Name one meaningful interaction you had that you would not have had otherwise.”

If support increases, keep the practice and refine it. If it does not, adjust the design rather than abandon the goal. In systems work, you do not judge the value by one attempt. You iterate until the human thing you care about becomes easier than the alternative.

Reflection prompt: Think about someone on your team who is least likely to describe themselves as well-connected or belonging. What does your current design offer them? What would it take to build one thing that makes connection more available to them specifically, not as a special accommodation, but as a feature of how the team works?

Recognition as a System, Not a Personality Trait

We’ve talked about recognition as a personal practice. The specific observation, the praise that travels, and the superpower spotted in the margins. This experiment is about recognition as architecture.

Feedback cultures in most organizations are better designed for telling people what they did wrong than for helping them see what went right. The “How’s my driving?” bumper sticker on the highway captures this perfectly; it’s designed structurally to invite only criticism. The number exists for when something goes wrong. Most recognition systems work the same way.

This is not the case everywhere. If you’ve ever worked on a sales floor, you know that recognition of success is delivered early, often, and in public. Wins get called out. Boards get updated. The room knows when someone has done something worth celebrating. It would be easy to attribute this to the personalities involved, to the assumption that sales attracts people who are naturally expressive, competitive, and energized by public acknowledgment. And there is something to that. Recognition is a value some people hold more naturally than others, and some leaders are genuinely exceptional at seeing people and saying so.

But more than anything, recognition is a system. Sales cultures have built it into their architecture because sales leaders have seen, directly and repeatedly, how motivating that architecture can be. The recognition does not depend on whether the manager happens to be in a generous mood that week. It is baked into the rhythm of how the team operates.

The research bears this out. Studies on workplace recognition consistently show that feeling seen and appreciated is one of the strongest predictors of engagement, retention, and discretionary effort, and that the effects are not limited to sales or performance-driven environments. They show up across team types, industries, and roles. What varies is not whether recognition matters, it almost always does, but whether organizations have built systems to make it happen reliably, or left it to chance and personality.

Left to chance, recognition becomes episodic at best and inequitable at worst. The employees with the most visible roles, the loudest advocates, or the best timing get seen. Everyone else waits. Over time, that gap begins to communicate what the organization actually values, regardless of what its stated values say.

The fix is not to mandate enthusiasm or train managers to seem more appreciative. It is to design recognition into the routines of the team so that it happens predictably, not only when someone remembers or feels moved to say something.

The Experiment: The Recognition Redesign

Duration: Two weeks

Time required: 30 minutes upfront, 5 minutes per week during the experiment

Materials: A notebook or notes app, and access to whatever team communication channels you currently use

Goal: Identify where recognition is missing or inconsistent on your team and install one simple system that makes appreciation predictable rather than personality-dependent.

Step One: Audit Your Current Recognition Culture (30 Minutes to Start)

Write honest answers to these questions:

•  In the last two weeks, how many times did you specifically recognize someone on your team for something they did well? Who did you recognize, and who did you not?

•  When recognition does happen on your team, what tends to trigger it? A big win? A crisis averted? Or does it happen regularly regardless of scale?

•  Who on your team gets recognized most often? Who gets recognized least? Is there a pattern?

•  If someone on your team were asked “does your manager notice when you do good work,” what do you think they would say?

You are looking for gaps, inconsistencies, and places where recognition is being left to chance or mood.

Step Two: Design One Simple Recognition Practice to Try (15 Minutes)

Choose one small structural change you can make in the next two weeks. It should be specific, repeatable, and independent of how you happen to be feeling on a given day.

Examples:

•  Open every weekly team meeting with one specific acknowledgment of something a team member did well that week. Written down in advance, not improvised.

•  Add a standing “wins” thread or channel where anyone on the team can recognize anyone else, with a norm that the manager seeds it weekly.

•  End every one-on-one with one specific observation about something the person is doing well, tied to a behavior you observed.

•  Send one written recognition message per week to someone who does not typically get public acknowledgment for their work.

Write down exactly what you will do, when you will do it, and what would prevent you from doing it.

Step Three: Run it for Two Weeks

Do the thing, consistently, regardless of whether the week feels like a “recognition kind of week.” At the end of each week, note:

•  Did I do it?

•  How did people respond?

•  Who did I recognize, and who did I miss again?

Step Four: Evaluate (15 Minutes at End of Week Two)

Look back at your notes and ask:

•  Did the practice feel forced, or did it start to feel natural?

•  Did you notice any change in energy, engagement, or reciprocity on the team?

•  Who is still not getting recognized consistently, and what would it take to change that?

Decide whether to keep the practice, adjust it, or replace it with something that fits your team’s rhythm better.

Reflection prompt: Think about the last time someone recognized your work in a way that genuinely landed. What made it land? Was it the words, the timing, the specificity, or the fact that they noticed at all? Now ask: are you giving your team that experience, or are you leaving it to chance?

The Offline Connection Dividend

Just as AI has come to dominate conversations about the future of work, job design, productivity, technology, an equally loud countercurrent is building around offline connection. People are returning to physical books, device-free gatherings, and simple offline rituals to reconnect with each other; and to think more clearly, feel more grounded, and be fully present. As if emerging from a collective trance, it almost feels like we all looked up from our devices at the exact same time and remembered something obvious and ancient: We still need each other.

Not in the abstract, in the bodily, nervous-system sense of the phrase. We need other people the way we need sleep and sunlight, they are part of what makes the work possible.

The American Psychological Association reported that nearly 7 in 10 U.S. adults said they needed more emotional support in the past year than they received. That number is so large it stops being an “individual problem” and becomes an ecosystem signal. And if you lead an ecosystem (an organization, a division, a function, a team), you don’t get to treat ecosystem signals as background noise.

Unfortunately, where leaders often get this wrong is in reaching for a familiar lever: policy. Mandates, return-to-office, hybrid requirements; something crisp enough to put in an email that sounds decisive. Connection is a design problem, and it’s solved less by a single lever than by the defaults you build into the system.

That’s what system friction is: the defaults an ecosystem creates (intentionally or accidentally) about how human beings will encounter one another. It’s policy and structure, yes, but also space, incentives, meeting norms, technology norms, and the quiet emotional messages those choices send. It’s the invisible architecture that makes certain behaviors easy and other behaviors oddly difficult.

Right now, many workplaces have become astonishingly good at removing friction. You can work without commuting, without bumping into anyone, without having to make small talk, without getting pulled into a hallway conversation that turns into mentoring, without feeling the awkwardness that sometimes precedes real honesty. You can run a whole day through messages and meetings and documents and never be in the same room with another nervous system. You can be “highly collaborative” and never once feel held by a group.

The inconvenient truth is that the friction we removed was doing work for us.

Some of the most important social glue in an organization is produced by tiny, low-stakes, high-frequency contact micro-moments that build familiarity, trust, and goodwill. The tiny check-in before the meeting starts, the shared joke that lowers everyone’s shoulders, or the “walk with me” that turns into a gentle truth someone was afraid to say on camera. These subtle cues that tell you whether the room is tense, confused, energized, or shut down.

We like to imagine that the “real work” is the slide, the plan, the forecast, the code, the deal. But a lot of the real work is the human work that makes those outputs possible: trust, coordination, repair, and courage.

It might be nostalgia,e and it is also biology and social science.

The U.S. Surgeon General has described loneliness and social disconnection as a public health concern, emphasizing that social connection is protective and calling on workplaces as one of the places where connection can be built at scale. Public health researchers have also shown that social isolation and loneliness are associated with increased risk of poor health outcomes and mortality. Said another way: Connection is not just a “nice to have.”

Social Baseline Theory argues that our brains assume proximity to supportive others as a kind of baseline resource; when that resource is present, the perceived cost of threat and effort goes down. The evidence for this is kind of wild. In a 2008 University of Virginia study, participants standing at the base of a steep hill wearing heavy backpacks estimated the hill’s incline significantly lower when a friend stood beside them than when they stood alone; the closer the friendship, the shallower the hill appeared. Humans are built to share the load. When people feel alone in the system, everything gets heavier: decision-making, change, conflict, risk, feedback, the hard conversations leaders wish people would “just have,” and the resilience they wish people would “just show.”

So when leaders ask why tension feels higher, why burnout feels stickier, why cynicism spreads faster than optimism, why people are more reactive, why conflict either erupts or disappears underground—part of the answer is often painfully simple: The system no longer reliably gives people the kinds of contact that help them regulate, repair, and remember they belong.

Which brings us to the polarizing topic of where work happens. The goal is not to resolve that debate here but to tell the truth about trade-offs, then design work around the outcomes you care about.

Hybrid work can deliver real upside. A large randomized controlled trial in a major company found that hybrid work improved retention without harming performance or promotions. Gallup’s framing is also instructive: The success of hybrid work depends less on a blanket mandate and more on how teams coordinate schedules, set expectations, and build trust—making the “commute worth it” with a clear workplace value proposition.

And yet, there are also costs leaders shouldn’t pretend away. Research on remote work at scale has shown shifts in collaboration patterns—changes that can reduce cross-group ties and make networks more siloed if organizations don’t intentionally rebuild what used to form naturally.

Both things can be true at once. Flexibility can be a gift and disconnection can be a slow leak.

So, the mature question is not “Are we pro-office or pro-remote?” The mature question is: “What is our system optimized for and what is it quietly sacrificing?”

If you want connection, you must design for it the way you design for quality, safety, or customer experience. You don’t hope quality appears. You build guardrails. You create rituals. You measure leading indicators. You train people. You shape incentives. You make some things slightly harder so better things become more likely.

That’s system friction: not punishment or control or nostalgia but design.

Anthropology gives us one more helpful truth: Humans don’t bond only through deep conversation. We bond through repeated, low-stakes contact and shared experience. Robin Dunbar’s work popularized the idea that much of language evolved as a kind of social conditioning—small talk and gossip functioning to maintain cohesion in groups too large for deep conversation at every level. We tend to engage in this kind of behavior without much though, but organizations and their cultures are fueled by it. A group without this engagement becomes brittle over time.

And bodies bond in ways screens struggle to replicate. There’s evidence that synchronous activity—moving, clapping, chanting, working in rhythm—can increase prosocial behavior and feelings of social bonding. Even research on shared experiences suggests that intense, jointly attended moments can foster reciprocal prosocial attitudes, with “togetherness” functioning as a mechanism, not necessarily because everyone feels the exact same thing, but because they feel it together. Sociologists have long named this surge of shared emotional energy “collective effervescence,” the sense that a group becomes more than the sum of individuals, an old idea, but newly relevant in an era of atomized work.

This is why certain industries never fully outsourced connection to screens. Healthcare relies on huddles and handoffs not because they’re charming, but because they’re safety infrastructure. Telecom field organizations rely on in-person operating rhythms because distributed work can quietly turn into distributed identity, people doing tasks without feeling part of something. Law enforcement relies on briefings and after-action reviews because shared sensemaking is a protective factor when pressure is high. Finance and other high-velocity environments use proximity as coordination technology, lower latency for judgment, escalation, mentorship.

These are not arguments for five-days-a-week mandates but for intentional proximity. The kind that has a purpose: trust, learning, repair, belonging, better decisions made with more context than a calendar invite can hold.

So here is the strategy, stated plainly: Make it slightly harder for people to remain alone. Not by shaming them. Not by forcing extroversion. By changing the defaults.

Default calendars toward shared rhythms that don’t rely on heroic effort. Create anchor moments where teams know they will collide in person—predictably enough to build relationships, infrequent enough to respect flexibility. Make onboarding relational, not purely informational; pair new hires with real humans in real rooms early. Build “third-place” spaces inside the organization—places where people can linger without an agenda, the workplace version of community infrastructure. Protect presence with simple norms: fewer meetings, better meetings, phones down for the first five minutes, and an explicit bias for hard conversations to happen with bodies in the same space when possible.

And then (this part matters) treat the emotional reality of the system as data, not drama. If 69% of adults are saying they needed more emotional support than they received, don’t assume your workplace is magically exempt. Ask. Measure lightly but consistently. Notice patterns. Make adjustments. Leaders already run on data. The upgrade is admitting that emotion is part of the dataset that determines whether the system can hold.

AI will keep accelerating, work will keep changing, efficiency will likely keep seducing us. System friction is how you prevent the ecosystem from accidentally becoming inhuman. It’s the deliberate inconvenience that makes room for the thing technology can’t replace: people building trust and meaning together, in the same place, at the same time.

•  A monthly build day in person dedicated to sensemaking + repair + learning (not status updates).

•  A two-person mentoring loop that only happens walking or over a shared meal.

•  A conflict rule: If something feels charged, the first attempt at repair happens face-to-face (or at least voice-only, not text).

4.  Run the pilot for two weeks, then ask two questions.

Keep it simple. In a pulse or in 1:1s, ask:

•  “Did you feel more supported this week than a typical week?”

•  “Name one meaningful offline interaction you had that you wouldn’t have otherwise.”

If support increases, keep the friction and refine it. If it doesn’t, don’t abandon the goal and adjust the design. In systems work, you don’t judge the value by one attempt. You iterate the defaults until the human thing you care about becomes easier than the inhuman alternative.

Values That Do Something

Whenever we work with an organization and want to understand its culture, we ask about core values, and then we ask the more revealing follow-up: “And what would you say are the actual values of the organization?” The first answer describes the poster on the wall. The second answer, when people feel safe enough to give it, describes how things actually work.

Earlier, in the section on internal friction, we talked about the difference between what you value and what you’re valuing: what you say matters versus where your time and attention in fact go. The same gap exists at the systemic level, when groups of people try to define what they value most in how they work together. Organizations develop the same mismatch between stated and enacted values, and for many of the same reasons. The emotional and political cost of living a hard value out loud is often higher than the cost of listing it on a website.

A classic example is safety. You can almost always tell immediately how serious a company is when they say their top value is safety. If the safety report appears at the end of the annual financial presentation as a brief afterthought, you know something about the organization’s true hierarchy of priorities. Stated values that never show up in decisions, trade-offs, or consequences are not values. They are aspirations at best, and window dressing at worst.

The test of a real value is whether it costs something. A job foreman who shuts down a worksite over a safety concern, at real financial cost to the company, and gets celebrated for it rather than quietly penalized, is telling everyone in the organization something true about what the system reinforces. That story travels. It becomes part of how new employees learn what is real and what is ceremonial.

There are two practical moves worth making here. The first is to pair each stated value with two or three specific examples of the value in action: real decisions, real trade-offs, real moments where someone chose the value over the easier path. This closes the gap between abstraction and behavior, and gives people a legible signal of what the organization rewards.

The second is to stop listing values as single words and start writing them as behavioral sentences. “Integrity” tells no one anything useful. “We tell people hard truths directly, even when it would be easier to stay quiet” tells people what is expected and what will be recognized. Behavioral values are harder to write and harder to ignore. That friction is the point.

The Experiment: The Values Stress Test

Duration: Two weeks

Time required: 45 minutes upfront, 10 minutes per week during the experiment

Materials: A notebook or notes app, and a copy of your organization’s stated values

Goal: Identify where your organization’s stated values are backed by observable behavior and where they are not, and make one value more legible and real.

Step One: Map the Gap (30 Minutes to Start)

Take your organization’s stated values or your team’s and for each one write honest answers to these questions:

•  Can you name a specific recent decision or trade-off where this value visibly shaped the outcome?

•  Can you name a moment where this value was tested and the organization chose it over the easier path?

•  If a new employee asked you what this value looks like in practice, what would you say?

Values with strong answers are alive. Values with thin or vague answers are ceremonial. Circle the ones with thin answers. Those are your candidates for the experiment.

Step Two: Choose One Value to Operationalize (15 Minutes)

Pick one ceremonial value and do two things with it.

First, rewrite it as a behavioral sentence. Not a word or a phrase, but a specific description of what it looks like when someone is living this value at work. Examples:

•  “Integrity” becomes “We surface problems early, even when it is uncomfortable, rather than waiting until they are unavoidable.”

•  “Innovation” becomes “We run small tests before committing large resources, and we treat a well-designed failure as useful information.”

•  “People first” becomes “When we make a decision that affects someone’s role or workload, we tell them directly and early, not through rumor or organizational announcement.”

Second, find or create one story that illustrates the value in action: a real decision, a real trade-off, a real moment where someone chose the value and the organization recognized them for it. If you cannot find one, that is important information.

Step Three: Put it in Circulation (Two Weeks)

Find one natural opportunity per week to use the behavioral version of the value in a real context. In a team meeting, a one-on-one, a hiring conversation, or a performance discussion. You are not giving a speech about values. You are using the specific language in a specific moment. Note when you did it and how people responded.

Step Four: Evaluate (15 Minutes at End of Week Two)

Ask yourself:

•  Did the behavioral framing make the value more useful in conversation?

•  Did naming it in specific moments change anything about how the team talked about decisions?

•  What would it take to make this the default language in your organization rather than the exception?

Reflection prompt: Think about the last significant decision your organization made that was difficult or costly. Which of your stated values showed up in that decision, and which ones quietly stepped aside? What does that tell you about the actual hierarchy of values in your system, and what is one thing you could do this week to close the gap by even a small amount?

Add Friction Back In

As of this writing, public discourse about the impact of AI on relationships, work, and society is rampant. Views range from AI as the sentient savior of our work-bound selves to AI as the software-driven downfall of humanity, with every viewpoint in between. It is safe to say that no one knows what the scope or ultimate value of AI’s impact will be, and any claim to know is speculation. It is worth considering, however, the ways that our friction model may offer some insight into how the injection of AI into individual work, relationships, and systems may affect us simply by reducing friction.

The case for friction reduction goes something like this: AI increases the velocity and productivity of individuals, enabling them to do more, create more, and get more done. Why learn to write an essay when the essay already exists within the algorithm? What we need to learn is how to write the correct prompts to produce the essay we want to see. Within our relationships, these systems make it easier to communicate, easier to navigate hardship, easier to learn from one another. Coaching and therapy are a useful case in point. Coaches and therapists are expensive, unavailable at 3 A.M., and limited in their knowledge and expertise in ways that may not serve every circumstance. Why not build coaching and therapy chatbots? And in our systems, AI can increase the velocity of problem-solving and make immense amounts of data searchable and consumable in an instant. Why not bake AI into everything to make it smarter, faster, better?

And yet the case for friction says: Wait. If we offload our thinking onto AI systems, if we appeal only to chatbots for interaction and insight, if we AI-ify our systems until they are at their core run by an algorithmic black box, what happens to our capacity to think? To interact? To solve problems when the systems break? We may find ourselves pawing at our machines, wondering what makes them tick, why we cannot work together to fix them, and why we lack the resilience and attention to focus on the problem in the first place.

This is not a book about AI, and AI is not strictly the point. But it is a useful case in point, because it so singularly magnifies the implications of friction reduction in daily life. There is something that happens in the friction of working an idea through, in the inglorious fragments and false starts and revisions that precede a finished thought. That process is not inefficiency. It is how understanding gets built. When we skip it, we may get the output without the learning, the answer without the capacity to generate it again under different conditions.

This matters for leadership in particular. The judgment, relational attunement, and systems thinking that make leaders effective are not built in frictionless conditions. They are built through accumulated experience of navigating difficulty, sitting with ambiguity, and working problems through with other people in real time. Removing that friction, however well-intentioned, may in some ways hollow out the very capabilities it was meant to support.

So, the question is not whether to use the tools available to you. It is where to use them. Where might deliberately adding friction back in make group interactions richer over time? Where might including human judgment and agency make your systems more resilient rather than more efficient? The experiment that follows is designed to help you find those places and protect them.

The Experiment: Friction Restoration

Duration: One focused session plus a two-week experiment

Time required: 45 minutes upfront, 5 minutes per day during the experiment

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goals: Deliberately reintroduce one removed friction and spend two weeks learning what it was doing for you.

Step One: Map Your Current Friction Landscape (15 Minutes)

Think about the recurring tasks, interactions, and decisions in your work. Write your answers plainly:

•  Where have you (or your team) recently added speed, automation, or AI assistance?

•  Which of those changes felt like a relief? Which felt like something was lost?

•  Where do you notice the quality of your thinking, relationships, or judgment mattering most?

Now write down three areas where friction reduction may be costing you something, even if it is hard to name exactly what. Examples: “I use AI to draft responses to my team and the conversations feel thinner.” “We automated our approval process and I’ve lost visibility into why decisions are being made.” “I haven’t written out my own thinking on a hard problem in months.”

Step Two: Identify Your Friction Candidates (15 Minutes)

For each of the three areas you named, ask:

•  What was the human activity that got removed or accelerated here?

•  What judgment, reflection, or relational quality depended on that activity?

•  What is the cost if that capability atrophies over time?

Choose one area where adding friction back in seems most valuable. This is your experiment.

Step Three: Design Your Friction Intervention (10 Minutes)

Design a small, specific change you can make for the next two weeks. Keep it simple enough to actually do. Examples:

•  Write one paragraph of your own thinking before opening an AI tool on any significant problem.

•  Reinstate a weekly 30-minute conversation with a direct report that you had replaced with a shared document or dashboard.

•  Add a 24-hour pause before finalizing any decision your team has been making on autopilot.

•  Hand-write notes in your next three one-on-ones instead of typing.

Write down exactly what you will do, when you will do it, and how you will know if it is working.

Step Four: Run the Experiment (Two Weeks)

Do the thing. Each day, take five minutes to note:

•  Did I do it?

•  What did I notice?

•  What felt uncomfortable, and what felt valuable?

Discomfort and value often arrive together. That is the point.

Step Five: Evaluate and Decide (15 Minutes at the End of Week Two)

Look back at your notes and ask:

•  What did I notice about my thinking, my relationships, or my judgment during this period?

•  What would I have missed if I had kept the friction removed?

•  Is this a friction worth keeping permanently, or was it useful to revisit and then release?

Not every friction is worth restoring. The goal is not to make things harder for its own sake. It is to be intentional about where difficulty is doing something useful.

Reflection prompt: Where in your work have you optimized for speed without asking what the speed is costing you? Name one human capability, yours or your team’s, that may be quietly atrophying. What is one friction you will protect this week, and one you will consciously choose to keep removed?

Your People System Is One Thing

Most organizations have an org chart for their people functions that looks something like this: a talent acquisition team that finds and hires people, a learning and development function that trains them, an organizational development group that works on culture and change, and an HR business partner layer that handles the day-to-day. Each function has its own budget cycle, its own vendor relationships, its own metrics, and its own language. They may sit in the same department and report to the same CHRO, but in practice they often operate as neighboring silos, each pulling on the organization without much visibility into what the others are doing.

The problem is not that these distinctions exist. It is that they can easily become the primary reality, obscuring the more important truth underneath. All of these functions are touching the same people, in the same system, at different moments in the same journey.

Organizational theorists Warner Burke and George Litwin mapped this interconnection in a model that remains one of the most useful frameworks in organizational psychology. The Burke-Litwin model describes twelve organizational variables, from leadership and strategy at the top to individual motivation and performance at the bottom, and shows how each one influences the others. The key insight is not the list of variables but the arrows between them. Tug on one element and you will feel it elsewhere in the system, sometimes immediately, sometimes months later, often in places you did not expect. Change the strategy without changing the structure and the structure will quietly resist the strategy. Invest in leadership development without addressing the management practices those leaders return to and the development will not hold. Redesign a talent process without considering the culture it sits inside and the culture will absorb it or reject it.

This is what integrated people strategy means. Not a unified org chart or a shared Slack channel between HR functions. It means asking, before any significant people decision: What else in the system does this touch, and who needs to be in the room when we design it?

The organizations that get this right tend to share a few characteristics. They have a senior leader, often but not always a CHRO, who thinks in systems rather than functions and has the organizational standing to require coordination across them. They have a shared language for talking about people and performance that does not change depending on which team you are sitting with. And they have a regular forum, not a committee but a working session, where talent, development, and effectiveness decisions get made in the same conversation rather than in sequence.

The organizations that struggle tend to have the opposite: functions that protect their territory, leaders who were hired for functional expertise rather than systems thinking, and a planning process that treats headcount, learning budgets, and culture work as separate line items rather than as investments in the same underlying system.

This is a hard thing to change. It requires political will, structural redesign, and a sustained tolerance for the friction of integration. But the cost of not changing it is also real, and it accumulates quietly: development investments that do not transfer, talent decisions that surprise the culture, change initiatives that stall because no one connected the organizational levers that needed to move together.

Your people system is a single thing. The question is whether you are running it that way.

The Experiment: The Connected System Audit

Duration: Four to six weeks

Time required: Two hours upfront, one hour per week during the experiment

Materials: A notebook or notes app, the Burke-Litwin model (printable), and access to the leaders of your key people functions

Goal: Identify where your organization’s people functions are operating in isolation and design one integration that treats development, effectiveness, and talent as levers on the same system.

Step One: Map Your Current People System (45 Minutes)

Gather the leaders of your talent, L&D, OD, and HR business partner functions, or the equivalent in your organization. If you are working alone, do this as a thought exercise first and then validate with others.

Draw a simple map of your people functions and answer these questions for each one:

•  What is this function trying to accomplish this year?

•  What does success look like for this function, and how is it measured?

•  Which other functions does this one depend on to succeed?

•  Where does this function make decisions without input from the others?

Look for the gaps. Where are functions working in parallel without coordination? Where is one function solving a problem that another function created? Where are people in your organization experiencing the seams between these functions as friction, confusion, or inconsistency?

Step Two: Apply the Burke-Litwin Lens (30 Minutes)

Identify two or three organizational variables your people functions are currently touching. For each one, ask:

•  What other variables does this one connect to in the model?

•  Are those connected variables being actively considered in how we are designing this work?

•  Where are we pulling on one string without accounting for what moves on the other end?

Write down the two most significant disconnections you find. These are your design targets.

Step Three: Design One Integration (One Hour, with the Right People in the Room)

Choose one decision, initiative, or planning process that currently happens inside a single function and redesign it as a cross-functional conversation. It does not need to be large. Examples:

•  Bring talent acquisition and L&D into the same conversation about what capabilities the organization needs to hire for versus develop from within.

•  Connect the OD culture work to the performance management cycle so that the behaviors being assessed in reviews reflect the culture being built.

•  Create a shared intake process for major people initiatives so that no significant intervention gets designed without a brief systems check across functions.

For your chosen integration, write down: who needs to be in the room, what decision or process you are redesigning, and what coordination mechanism will keep it connected over time.

Step Four: Run the Integration for Four Weeks

Implement the cross-functional process you designed. Each week, note:

•  What decisions were made differently because the right people were in the room?

•  Where did integration create friction of its own, turf, process, or language differences?

•  What would have happened to this decision if the functions had handled it separately?

Step Five: Evaluate and Expand (One Hour at End of Week Four)

Look back at your notes and ask:

•  Did the integration produce better decisions, faster alignment, or fewer downstream surprises?

•  What is the minimum ongoing structure needed to keep these functions coordinated without creating a new bureaucracy?

•  Which of your two design targets still needs attention?

Decide on one permanent change to how your people functions coordinate, even a small one, and put it on the calendar.

Reflection prompt: Think about a people initiative in your organization that did not land the way you expected. A development program that did not change behavior. A hiring push that created culture strain. A change effort that stalled. Looking at the Burke-Litwin model, which connected variables were not accounted for in the design? What would you do differently if you were starting today with the whole system in view?

Premium Strategy, Regular Fuel

In the previous experiment, we looked at the people system as a whole—the interconnected functions, the Burke-Litwin levers, the cost of pulling on one without accounting for what moves on the other end. Here we’re looking at one specific alignment problem that sits above it all, which is the gap between your business strategy and the human conditions required to execute it.

A leadership team can tell you, with startling precision, how the business makes money. They can run scenarios, model risk, and forecast outcomes with the calm confidence of people who have been asked the same questions a thousand times and know exactly where the levers are. Then you ask about the people strategy required to deliver that business strategy, and the conversation often shifts in a way that’s almost physical.

That gap is one of my favorite forms of system friction, because it hides in plain sight and it flatters us while it harms us. It flatters us by letting us say the right sentence (people are our greatest asset) without having to build the operating conditions that would make the sentence true.

We keep coming back to a simple image, one that doesn’t require anyone to be the villain. Picture a high-performance engine. Beautiful design, tight tolerances, serious horsepower. Now imagine fueling it with whatever was cheapest and most convenient, not because you don’t care about the engine, but because you keep telling yourself you’ll upgrade later, once things calm down, once the quarter closes, once the reorg settles, once the hiring plan comes through. The engine will still run, at least for a while, which is part of what makes this so seductive. But it will run hotter than it should, it will lose efficiency, it will become unpredictable under pressure, and eventually the very thing you built it for—reliability at high speed—becomes the thing it can’t consistently deliver.

A business strategy without a people strategy is that kind of machine: technically functional, practically compromised.

Strategic HR research has argued for decades that it isn’t isolated perks or one-off programs that move performance, but coherent systems of people practices that align with what the business is trying to do—how you hire, develop, reward, structure work, and support leaders as they execute. When those practices reinforce each other and reinforce the strategy, performance tends to be stronger than when they fight each other or operate like unrelated initiatives.

So why do smart leadership teams still treat the people strategy as an appendix?

Because the costs of misalignment often show up sideways. You don’t see a line item labeled “coordination debt,” but you feel it in the calendar that’s packed with meetings that exist because decisions don’t travel cleanly. You don’t see “psychological safety deficit” on the P&L, but you see the delayed escalation, the late surprises, the quiet workarounds, and the slow leak of talented people who have learned that the system does not reward honesty.

Over time, leaders start describing these outcomes as execution issues, as if execution is something individuals either have or don’t have, like a personality trait. But execution is more often a system outcome that reflects whether the environment makes the desired behaviors easy, repeatable, and socially safe.

Most modern strategies such as growth in new markets, faster innovation, higher quality, more customer intimacy, and tighter cost control require humans to do some combination of the following on a regular basis: decide with imperfect information, learn quickly, coordinate across functions, speak up early about risks, adapt when plans change, and recover after setbacks without turning into cynics.

Those are not small asks, and they are not “soft skills.” They are cognitive, emotional, and relational demands that require capacity and trust in the system, because the brain and body are not neutral platforms that run the same way under all conditions. Under chronic stress, for example, the brain reallocates resources in ways that can make flexible thinking, impulse control, and perspective-taking harder to access. Researchers studying stress and the prefrontal cortex (the region heavily involved in executive function and regulation) have shown that stress can impair these functions, especially when it is persistent and paired with low control or low recovery. Burnout research, too, links chronic workplace strain with changes in attention, emotion regulation, and cognitive functioning, which is a fancy way of saying that people do not bring their best minds to work when the system repeatedly teaches them to brace.

If your strategy needs good judgment and adaptive learning, and your systems reliably produce threat, overload, and ambiguity, you are asking for premium performance while supplying regular fuel.

The fix is designing the people system as a set of defaults that make the strategy doable for real humans, in the real world, when nobody is being heroic. Defaults are what people can count on when it’s busy, tense, political, or confusing, which is to say: Defaults are what people can count on most of the time.

•  Take psychological safety as one example. As Edmondson’s research makes clear (examined fully in Build the Felt Experience), if your strategy depends on surfacing problems early and learning fast, the system has to make raising the hand a protected move rather than a punished one. That requires more than leaders saying, “My door is always open.”

•  Then consider fairness, another default that quietly shapes everything. Organizational justice research shows that when people perceive decision-making processes as consistent, transparent, and respectful, you tend to see higher commitment and cooperation, and less withdrawal and cynicism. If your strategy needs discretionary effort, but your system teaches people that decisions are arbitrary, explanations are thin, and information is hoarded, you will pay for that mismatch in ways that do not look like a single dramatic crisis, but rather like a thousand small hesitations.

•  Then consider workload design, because “doing more with less” is not a strategy. As the burnout research in Track Burnout Like a Business Metric makes clear, when demands chronically exceed resources, burnout and disengagement follow. A strategy that requires speed, innovation, and quality cannot be executed on a system that treats recovery as laziness and capacity planning as optional.

This is where we want to gently challenge the common interpretation of “people are our greatest asset.” Many leaders mean it as a statement about talent: smart people, motivated people, high performers. But strategy rarely succeeds because of individual excellence alone; it succeeds because the system turns individual excellence into collective reliability, which requires trust, clarity, feedback loops, and norms that allow the truth to travel.

In other words, what you’re really trying to protect is your talent and the social fabric that lets talent compound. You can recruit brilliant people and still fail if your system makes it hard for them to coordinate, disagree constructively, or tell you what you don’t want to hear.

The moment you say “people are our greatest asset,” you are making a promise, and the system is the only thing that can keep it.

If you want a simple test, here it is: Look at what becomes true in your organization when pressure increases.

•  Do people get clearer or do they get noisier?

•  Do decisions get faster or do they get safer?

•  Do leaders become more curious or more controlling?

•  Do problems surface earlier or later?

If the answers to these questions are problematic, you need to redesign the default.

The Experiment: The People-System Alignment Audit

Duration: One 45-minute working session

Time required: 45 minutes, best done with your leadership team

Materials: A whiteboard or shared document if working as a team, a notebook or notes app if working alone

Goal: Identify where your people system is quietly undermining your strategy, and redesign at least one organizational default to close the gap.

Set a timer for 45 minutes, and do this as an individual leader or, better yet, with your leadership team so you can see where your assumptions diverge.

Step One: Translate Your Business Strategy into Verbs (5 Minutes)

Write your top three strategic priorities as action statements, not nouns, because verbs expose what humans must do. Examples might be: reduce cycle time, expand enterprise growth, increase patient trust, integrate acquisitions, improve margin without quality loss.

Step Two: Name the Human Requirements (10 Minutes)

For each strategic verb, finish this sentence three to five times:

“For this to work, people must be able to consistently …”

Use plain language, and don’t let yourself hide behind abstractions. You’re looking for behaviors like: raise risks early, decide with incomplete data, share bad news without punishment, coordinate across functions without escalation, learn new tools quickly, recover after intense sprints, give direct feedback, ask for help sooner.

Step Three: Score Your Current Defaults (15 Minutes)

For each requirement, rate your system on a 1–5 scale where 1 means “the system fights this” and 5 means “the system makes this easy.” Use these five lenses as your prompts, because they map cleanly to what research suggests matters:

•  Clarity: Do people know what “good” looks like, and can they prioritize without guessing?

•  Capacity: Do workload and staffing make the behaviors possible without constant overreach?

•  Candor: Is it socially safe to surface uncertainty, mistakes, and risks early? (psychological safety)

•  Consistency: Do decisions feel consistent, explainable, and respectful even when outcomes aren’t ideal?

•  Coordination: Do cross-functional handoffs work without heroic effort and relationship roulette?

Circle your lowest lens across the page; that is likely where your people system is quietly undermining your strategy.

Step Four: Redesign One Default (10 Minutes)

Choose one recurring moment in the life of the organization where friction predictably appears, and write a new rule that will become the default. Use this template, and make it concrete:

“From now on, when ___ happens, we will ___, so that ___.”

Examples:

•  “When a project crosses teams, we will name a single decision owner and publish decision rights, so that speed doesn’t depend on escalation.”

•  “When risks emerge, we will surface them in the same forum as progress updates, so that early honesty becomes normal rather than brave.”

•  “When workload spikes for more than two weeks, we will explicitly trade off priorities, so that burnout stops being the hidden plan.”

Step Five: Pick Two Measures That Keep You Honest (5 Minutes)

Choose one leading indicator (something you can see weekly) and one lagging indicator (a downstream result). A leading indicator might be decision-cycle time, percent of projects with clear owners, frequency of early risk flags, a short psychological safety pulse item, or cross-functional service level agreement (SLA) adherence. A lagging indicator might be regrettable attrition, cycle time, customer impact metrics, error rates, rework, or burnout pulse trends.

Then ask one final reflection question, and answer it without defending yourself: Where are we asking for premium performance while supplying regular fuel?

The Slow Yes Rule

Hiring is one of few leadership decisions that doesn’t simply produce an outcome, it produces more decision-makers. A new hire is a new source of judgment, a new pattern of attention, a new way the organization will interpret pressure. That’s why hiring is so often the invisible hinge between an organization that scales and an organization that quietly collapses into dependence on a few exhausted people who “hold it all together.”

When the work starts to feel heavy, leaders tend to search for answers in strategy. They redraw org charts, rewrite priorities, roll out new tools, sharpen the messaging, simplify the goals. All of those moves can matter, but they’re frequently downstream of a simpler, more uncomfortable truth: The room is doing what the room is designed to do. If decisions can’t be made without escalation, if conflict metastasizes instead of resolving, if teams need constant shepherding to do the basics well, the issue is often not the plan, but the people who are interpreting the plan, translating it into choices, and carrying it into action.

That’s why we’ve always liked Mark Zuckerberg’s hiring question—would you work for this person in an alternate universe? The power of that question is that it refuses to settle for competence alone. It forces you to look for a rarer commodity: Judgment. It asks whether this person can carry complexity without collapsing it, whether they can be trusted to make decisions that protect the system rather than merely advance themselves, whether they have the kind of leadership gravity that reduces the need for oversight rather than increasing it.

Most hiring systems are not built to answer that question. They’re built to reduce discomfort and ambiguity quickly. They’re built to produce relief. The seat is empty; the work is piling up; the team is tired; the leader is stretched; the calendar is packed. In that context, “good enough” becomes seductive. The organization starts to hire for short-term comfort and then spends the next year paying the interest on that decision in supervision, conflict, churn, and burnout. Leaders call this interest payment “leadership,” the ongoing cost of a system that optimized for speed instead of discernment.

The difficulty is that hiring is not only a rational exercise in gathering evidence and making a clean choice. Hiring is also an emotional and social experience that activates some of the most predictable quirks of the human mind. In an interview, you get a fast impression, sometimes in seconds. The candidate feels sharp, or safe, or polished, or “not quite right.” Your nervous system reads cues before your conscious mind has adequate data, and we are built to judge quickly, not necessarily accurately, in the conditions that hiring creates: performance, ambiguity, incentive, fatigue.

Once an impression forms, it tends to shape the rest of the process. The halo effect is one of the classic traps. One attractive trait, like confidence, warmth, pedigree, or amazing communication skills, casts a glow over everything else, as if the presence of one strength implies the presence of many. It’s not that the interviewer is trying to be careless; it’s that the brain prefers coherent stories over complicated ones. If someone looks like leadership, we start to interpret their answers through the lens of leadership. If someone feels awkward, we start to interpret their answers as evidence of limited capability. A good interview becomes self-reinforcing, and so does a mediocre one.

Then we layer on the quieter distortion of “fit.” Fit often sounds like wisdom, but it can be a convenient disguise for familiarity. Similarity feels safe; it lowers uncertainty; it makes it easier to imagine collaboration. Organizational research has repeatedly shown that perceived similarity influences attraction and evaluation, which means “fit” can become a mechanism for reproducing the existing culture rather than strengthening it. Even leaders with the best intentions can drift toward hiring the person who feels easiest rather than the person whose judgment will hold under strain.

And because these biases are mostly invisible from the inside, we often underestimate them. The “bias blind spot” literature suggests people are generally better at seeing bias in others than in themselves, which means confidence can increase risk: “I’m a good judge of people” becomes the exact belief that makes the system vulnerable. In hiring, confidence is not proof of accuracy. Sometimes it’s the opposite.

This is where the idea of system friction becomes essential, especially if your organization is trying to grow without becoming brittle. Friction is the set of designed constraints that slows you down at the exact moments your brain wants to speed up. It’s the pause that interrupts urgency, charm, relief, and scarcity thinking. It’s the structure that protects the organization from the most predictable human errors, not by demanding that leaders become perfect evaluators, but by making the decision environment more honest.

The research on selection methods is, frankly, not subtle. Unstructured interviews—the kind where you “have a conversation” and see how it feels—are consistently weaker predictors of job performance than structured interviews, where questions are standardized and scored against a rubric. Work sample tests, where candidates do a representative slice of the actual job, perform strongly because they measure what matters: how someone thinks and executes under constraints that resemble real work. These are not academic preferences. They are practical protections against a system that would otherwise drift toward charisma and narrative instead of evidence.

But we want to underline something important: The purpose of structure is not to make hiring sterile. The purpose of structure is to make “yes” costly enough that it means something. It’s to make it slightly annoying to hire the wrong person.

In high-stakes domains, mature systems do not rely on confidence as a safeguard. Aviation uses checklists not because pilots lack skill, but because skilled people under pressure skip steps. Medicine uses time-outs and protocols not because surgeons are careless, but because expertise doesn’t erase complexity. The system inserts pauses so that humans remain competent when their nervous systems are taxed. Hiring deserves the same respect because hiring errors don’t remain local. They propagate. A bad hire pulls attention and creates hidden labor throughout the network—extra meetings, extra clarifications, extra “quick check-ins,” extra emotional repair work that nobody counts as work until they’re exhausted.

A truly good hire does the opposite. They reduce downstream friction. They lower the organization’s cognitive load. They make decisions without drama and create clarity without performative certainty. They handle conflict without escalation. They carry context cleanly. They repair quickly when they miss. They create a sense of steadiness around them that makes other people better. Over time, those people quietly build an organization that can move without heroics.

This is why the alternate-universe test works as a north star: It points you toward the type of person who can carry responsibility rather than consume it. But north stars don’t run systems. Systems run on defaults.

Tim Cook is often described as an operations-first leader, someone whose career and reputation were built on execution discipline, supply chain mastery, and organizational rigor. Apple’s own biography emphasizes his operations background and focus on end-to-end systems. Whether or not you find every anecdote about him compelling, the deeper pattern is instructive: Leaders who understand systems do not treat hiring as a relief valve. They treat it as an architecture decision. They know that when you hire, you are not merely filling a gap; you are reshaping the internal logic of how work happens. They design the doorway accordingly.

So the question is not whether you have a hiring process. Everyone has one, even if it’s accidental. The question is what your process is optimized for: speed and comfort, or accuracy and resilience. If you hire to reduce pressure today, you will increase pressure tomorrow. If you hire to increase judgment in the room, you create a company that doesn’t fall apart when you’re not involved in every decision.

This is what it means to build appropriate friction into hiring. Make the yes slower. Make the evidence louder. Make it harder for charm to outrun trust. Because “fine” is how systems become fragile. Fine is how leaders become glue. Fine is how organizations start confusing heroics for excellence.

If things feel heavy, don’t only look at your strategy. Look at the doorway. The future keeps entering through it.

The Experiment: The Slow-Yes Hiring Protocol

Duration: One uninterrupted hour plus a two-week experiment

Time required: 60 minutes upfront, 10 minutes per hiring decision during the experiment

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goals: Build the practice of hiring on evidence rather than instinct or urgency.

Step One: Identify the Load-Bearing Judgments (10 Minutes)

Choose one role you’re hiring for now (or one you hire often). Write your answers plainly:

•  What decisions will this person make when no one is watching?

•  What mistakes would be expensive here—not embarrassing, but expensive?

•  Under pressure, what behaviors do I need to see repeatedly?

Now write three judgment signals you want to hire for (e.g., “names trade-offs clearly,” “repairs quickly,” “makes decisions with incomplete information without becoming reckless”), and three failure signals you cannot afford (e.g., “blames and deflects,” “escalates conflict,” “needs constant reassurance”).

Step Two: Convert Judgment into a Structured Interview (20 Minutes)

Draft six questions tied directly to the signals you wrote:

•  Three behavioral questions (“Tell me about a time when …”) that require specific examples

•  Two scenario questions (“What would you do if …”) that mirror real constraints of your role

•  One values question that reveals what they refuse to do, even when tempted

Create a simple 1–5 scorecard for each question, with one sentence describing what a “5” sounds like. Add a rule at the top: no 5s without a concrete example. (This step is the friction. Don’t skip it.)

Step Three: Add One Work Sample (15 Minutes)

Design a work sample that resembles real work in your environment, not idealized work.

•  Give a messy inbox scenario and ask for prioritization

•  Provide a short dataset and ask for a recommendation with trade-offs

•  Offer a conflict scenario and ask for an action plan

•  Ask them to critique a process and propose an improvement

Score it using only two criteria: quality of thinking and clarity under constraint.

Step Four: Install a Cooling-Off Rule (5 Minutes)

Choose one policy for your future self:

•  No same-day offers

•  A 24-hour pause after the final interview

•  A required written debrief where someone must argue the “no” case

This is not bureaucracy. It’s a protection against urgency and relief.

Step Five: Run the Alternate-Universe Test (10 Minutes)

After you’ve scored the evidence, ask the question again:

•  Would I work for them in an alternate universe?

•  Would I trust their judgment with decisions that affect people?

•  Would I want my team learning their habits?

If your answer changes when you look at the evidence, trust the evidence. If your answer stays uncertain, treat uncertainty as a design prompt: What evidence are you missing, and how will you get it?

Reflection prompt: Where is your current hiring process optimized for speed, and where does that speed show up later as hidden labor? Name one friction you will add this week, and one habit you will stop rewarding.

Protect What AI Can’t Replace

In Add Friction Back In, we made the case that removing friction from work isn’t always progress. Some of what we’ve been optimizing away was doing something important for our thinking, our relationships, and our judgment. This experiment takes that argument one step further and gets specific. If frictionless efficiency is the risk, what exactly are we trying to protect?

Three capabilities come to mind as particularly worth designing around. The first is judgment under ambiguity: the ability to make a call when the data is incomplete, the situation is novel, and the consequences are real. AI systems are extraordinary at pattern recognition within known parameters. They are not good at knowing when the parameters have changed, or when the right answer requires weighing values that cannot be reduced to an optimization function. That is a human job, and it will remain one.

The second is relational attunement: the ability to read what is happening between people, to notice what is not being said, to adjust in real time to the emotional temperature of a room. This is not a soft skill in the pejorative sense. It is a sophisticated perceptual and interpersonal capability that takes years to develop and that no current AI system can replicate at the level of nuance that complex human relationships require. The better AI gets at simulating warmth and responsiveness, the more valuable genuine attunement becomes, because people can feel the difference even when they cannot always name it.

The third is emotional intelligence: the capacity to recognize, understand, and work skillfully with emotion, in yourself and in others. In our research, 60% of workers reported believing that emotional intelligence will become more important in the future of work. That is a striking number, and it reflects something people are already sensing: that as the technical floor rises, the human ceiling becomes the ceiling that matters.

And yet many of these same people work in organizations where emotions are still treated as liabilities. Where vulnerability is read as weakness. Where the friction of navigating feelings, conflict, and relational complexity is high enough that emotional intelligence functions less as a leadership asset and more as a survival mechanism. You need it not to thrive but to endure.

This is the gap worth closing. Not by mandating emotional intelligence training or adding another competency to the performance review rubric, but by redesigning work itself around the assumption that these capabilities are central, not peripheral. That means creating conditions where judgment can be exercised rather than bypassed. Where relationships have enough time and space to develop genuine depth. Where the emotional texture of work is treated as information rather than noise.

The organizations that will navigate the AI transition well are not necessarily the ones that adopt the technology fastest. They are the ones that are clearest about what they are trying to protect, and deliberate enough to build that protection into how they work. Technical capability is increasingly a commodity. Judgment, attunement, and emotional intelligence are not. They are harder to build, harder to scale, and harder to replace. That is exactly why they are worth the friction of protecting.

The Experiment: The Human Capability

Duration: Four to six weeks

Time required: Two hours upfront, one hour per week during the experiment

Materials: A notebook or notes app, and access to the leaders and roles most affected by AI integration in your organization

Goal: Identify where distinctly human capabilities are being eroded or underused in your organization and redesign at least one work process to protect and develop them deliberately.

Step One: Name Your Human Capabilities (30 Minutes)

Start by getting specific about what you are trying to protect. Using the three capabilities below as a starting point, write a brief description of what each one looks like in practice in your organization:

•  Judgment under ambiguity: When do people in your organization make consequential calls without complete information? What conditions support that, and what conditions undermine it?

•  Relational attunement: Where does the quality of human connection in your organization produce outcomes that process or technology cannot? Where is that connection being thinned or replaced?

•  Emotional intelligence: Where do you see emotional skill making a real difference in your organization’s performance, culture, or relationships? Where is the friction of emotional suppression costing you something?

Add any additional human capabilities that feel distinctly important in your context.

Step Two: Map Where AI Is Expanding in Your Work (30 Minutes)

List the five to ten areas where AI tools are currently being used or actively considered in your organization. For each one, ask:

•  What human activity is being replaced or accelerated here?

•  Which of the three capabilities named above depended on that activity?

•  What is the risk if that capability atrophies over the next two to three years?

You are not arguing against the tool. You are identifying what needs to be protected alongside it.

Step Three: Find the Erosion Points (One Hour, with Others if Possible)

Look for the places where human capability is already thinning. These are your design targets. Signs to look for:

•  Decisions that used to require judgment are now being routed through automated systems without a human review step.

•  Relationships that used to develop through shared work are now mediated entirely through asynchronous digital tools.

•  Emotional or relational complexity is being avoided rather than navigated, because the friction of addressing it feels too high.

•  People are completing tasks faster but understanding the work less deeply.

Write down your two most significant erosion points. Be specific about the role, the process, and the capability at risk.

Step Four: Design One Protection (One Hour)

For one of your erosion points, design a concrete change to how the work is structured that protects the human capability at risk. Keep it specific and implementable. Examples:

•  Restore a human review step to a decision that has been fully automated, not to slow the process but to keep judgment in practice.

•  Redesign a recurring meeting that has become a status update into a working session where people think together in real time.

•  Create a standing space, a regular conversation, a peer group, a coaching relationship, where emotional and relational complexity can be named and worked through rather than suppressed.

•  Establish a norm that AI-generated work product is always reviewed and meaningfully edited by a human before it goes out, not as a quality check but as a practice of engagement.

Write down what you will change, who it affects, and how you will know if it is working.

Step Five: Run it for Four Weeks and Evaluate

Implement the change and observe. Each week, note:

•  Is the capability being exercised more than it was before?

•  What resistance have you encountered, and where is it coming from?

•  What would have been lost if you had not made this change?

At the end of four weeks, ask: what is one more protection worth adding? The goal is not a single intervention but a practice of asking, regularly and deliberately, what we are designing for when we design work.

Reflection prompt: Think about the people on your team who are most valued right now. What makes them irreplaceable? How much of that is technical, and how much of it is something else: the way they read a room, the quality of their judgment in hard moments, the trust they have built over time? Now ask: Is your organization designed to develop those qualities in the next generation of people coming up, or is it designed to extract technical output and hope the rest takes care of itself? What is one thing you would change if you took that question seriously?
