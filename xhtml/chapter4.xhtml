CHAPTER 4 WHAT THE DATA SAYS ABOUT LEADING THROUGH FRICTION

Every story, at its core, is about change. Leadership is not so different. At its core, leadership is how we respond to things changing and rarely is change emotionally neutral. What changes at work, more than anything else, is how people feel. And how people feel, more than anything else, determines what happens next. The question is what that emotional reality actually looks like in our organizations.

So, we set out to learn something about how people feel at work in America today. In partnership with a large data analytics firm which assembles representative panels, we launched the National Emotions Survey. We ultimately heard from 1,188 US-based working adults, across every generation, industry, and leadership level. What came back was not always what we expected; it was messier, more honest, and ultimately more useful.

Number 1. We Already Know Where We’re Going

Across every generation we surveyed, from Baby Boomers to Gen Z, roughly 60%a of respondents agreed that emotional intelligence will matter even more in the future of work. What makes this finding both hopeful and a little ironic is the context in which it emerged. We collected this data from a workforce that is, by many measures, struggling. Emotional drain is widespread, restrictive norms are alive and well, and generational tensions are bubbling right beneath the surface. And yet, at the same time, over half of workers look forward and see clearly: The path runs through emotional intelligence.

This tracks with what researchers and organizational leaders have been arguing for years. Daniel Goleman’s landmark work established that EQ competencies predict performance outcomes more reliably than IQ alone, particularly in leadership roles. More recently, as AI has displaced routine cognitive tasks, human emotional capabilities have only grown more strategically valuable. Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella put it plainly in a 2025 interview: “IQ has a place, but it’s not the only thing needed in the world. If you just have IQ without EQ, it’s just a waste of IQ.” He said this not as a soft sentiment but as a business argument; under his leadership, Microsoft’s market value grew from roughly $300 billion in 2014 to over $2.5 trillion by 2023.

Some organizational researchers have pushed back on what they call “EQ hype,” arguing that the construct’s predictive validity has been overstated, particularly in domains that require rigorous analytical judgment. Their concern is that elevating emotional skill above all else can inadvertently devalue technical competence and create organizations that feel good but execute poorly. It’s fair caution. No one wants a culture that mistakes emotional expressiveness for expertise. As Adam Grant has said, “If you are fixing my car or doing my taxes, I’d rather you didn’t pay too much attention to my emotions.” It’s a fair point, but high EQ isn’t about prioritizing emotions over outcomes, it’s about knowing when and how to prioritize them so you can be most effective.

Take airline pilots for example. A pilot needs profound technical skill. But the moments that separate a good flight from a catastrophic one—when systems fail, when weather turns, when human error enters—require the capacity to stay regulated, read a co-pilot’s hesitation, communicate with precision under pressure, and make judgment calls in real time. Consider Captain Chesley “Sully” Sullenberger, who in 2009 successfully ditched US Airways Flight 1549 on the Hudson River after both engines failed following a bird strike. With 155 lives on board and less than three minutes to act, his technical mastery alone didn’t save them. It was his ability to remain calm under extreme stress, trust his crew, and communicate clearly in chaos that made the difference. He later reflected that a lifetime of experience had not only prepared him to fly a plane, but to lead people through the unthinkable.

Sixty percent of American workers already know this. The question is whether the systems they work inside are ready to adapt.

Number 2. The “Emotion Is Weakness” Myth Is Officially Dying

Ask someone who grew up working in the 1970s or 80s about showing emotion at the office, and you’ll often hear a version of the same story. You kept it together, you pushed through, you didn’t give anyone a reason to think you couldn’t handle it. Feelings were personal, work was professional, and the two were not supposed to overlap.

That era produced a norm we’re still dismantling. Our survey found that 35% of working adults (more than one in three!) still agree that “emotions are a weakness at work.” That’s a meaningful number. But the story it tells is less about how many people hold this belief today and more about how many people used to, because the generational breakdown reveals something significant.

Among Baby Boomers and the Silent Generation, 19.8%b endorsed the emotions-as-weakness view. Among Gen X, that number jumps to 34.4%. Then Millennials come in at 43.3%, and Gen Z at 37.7%. The fact that Millennials hold this norm most strongly may be the most counterintuitive finding here. This is the generation that popularized the language of emotional intelligence, therapy culture, and “bringing your whole self to work,” and yet they endorse the emotions-as-weakness view at higher rates than any other cohort. One possible explanation: Millennials entered the workforce during the 2008 financial crisis and its aftermath, a period of intense job scarcity and professional precarity, when proving toughness and competitiveness may have felt like survival. They absorbed the progressive vocabulary of emotional openness while still operating inside systems that punished it. Gen X, often assumed to be the stoic holdouts, actually sits in the middle of the pack here. And notably, the oldest workers, Boomers and the Silent Generation, are the least likely to endorse the belief, which inverts the assumption that this norm is simply a relic of age.

The research supporting the death of this norm is extensive. A growing body of evidence shows that emotional suppression, the active effort to hide or mask feelings, is metabolically costly. James Gross’s work on emotion regulation demonstrated that expressive suppression is associated with decreased memory, increased physiological stress responses, and impaired social connection. In other words, the person who successfully “hides” their emotion at work is doing expensive, invisible labor.

There’s also a leadership effectiveness argument for not viewing emotion as weakness. Research synthesizing evidence on leader emotional display shows that leaders who allow appropriate emotional expression within reasonable norms tend to generate higher team performance, stronger cohesion, and better information sharing. The “stone-faced boss” model actively degrades team function.

And yet,c the critique of emotional openness isn’t entirely without merit. Research on emotional labor has shown that pressured authenticity, the kind where workers are told to “be themselves” while operating under norms that only reward certain kinds of emotional expression, can be just as exhausting as suppression. Demanding that people perform emotional openness in an environment that hasn’t made it safe is a new form of the old problem.

Which is why we find the 35% figure instructive. The myth is dying, but slowly, unevenly, and non-linearly. The cure for “emotions are weakness” is not a mandatory feelings circle. It’s the slow, deliberate, structural redesign of systems that have for decades rewarded people for the work of hiding themselves.

Number 3. Leaders Write the Emotional Rule Book

The numbers here tell two stories at once. First: 56% of respondents agree that leaders feel more pressure to hide their emotions at work than other employees do. And leaders themselves feel this most acutely, with 66% of leaders agreeing compared to 47% of non-leaders. Second: 53.6% of respondents agree that when leaders do hide their emotions, it creates more problems than it solves. Read those two findings together, and a painful bind comes into focus. Leaders feel the most pressure to suppress, and the people around them pay the highest price when they do.

This matters because leaders don’t just model behavior, they author it for everyone else. The research on psychological safety is unambiguous on this point. Leader behavior is the single strongest predictor of whether team members feel safe raising concerns, admitting mistakes, or expressing ideas that might not land well. Not team composition. Not company values. Not HR policy. The leader. When a leader is emotionally closed—flat affect in hard conversations, deflection when things get personal, performance of certainty they don’t feel—the system around them reads that signal and adjusts. People learn, quickly and without being told, that openness is risky here and they suppress accordingly. And suppression is costly; lower information flow, slower error correction, reduced creativity, and higher turnover among people who value authenticity. The leader who believes they are simply “staying professional” is, in practice, running an emotional tax on everyone in the room.

When you take a deeper look at the Satya Nadella story at Microsoft, it makes this visible at scale. When Nadella took over in 2014, Microsoft had a culture described by many insiders as paranoid and competitive, one in which emotional openness was routinely punished and political self-protection was rewarded. Nadella made a specific, deliberate decision to model a different emotional posture. He drew openly on lessons from raising his son Zain, who has cerebral palsy. He invited a mindfulness teacher to senior leadership team meetings. He institutionalized Carol Dweck’s growth mindset framework, which explicitly embraces the emotional experience of failure as information rather than indictment. Within a decade, Microsoft’s culture ranking and market value had both transformed dramatically. You cannot separate those two facts.

We will say that research distinguishes between authentic emotional display, expressing genuine states in service of connection and clarity, and strategic emotional display, where leaders perform emotions they believe will be effective. There is also the cautionary tale of Hollis Harris, former CEO of Continental Airlines, who in 1991 sent a memo to 42,000 employees telling them to “pray for the future of the company.” He was fired the next day. Vulnerability without competence, without context, and without the trust to hold the weight of it is not good leadership.

The point is not that leaders should be expressing emotion freely, all of the time, and in all situations. It’s that leaders should understand they are never emotionally neutral. Every time a leader shuts down, deflects, or performs invulnerability, they are writing a rule. And that rule (whether intentional or not) shapes what thousands of people decide is safe to feel.

Number 4. Enlightened but Exhausted: The Cost of Progress

This is the finding that crept up on us. And it’s the one we suspect will resonate most if you’ve managed anyone under forty in the past few years.

On a 1-to-5 scale of emotional drain at work, Baby Boomers and the Silent Generation average 2.13. Gen X averages 2.65. Millennials come in at 2.88. And Gen Z reports the highest drain of any cohort at 3.00, though only modestly higher than Millennials. The more meaningful gap is the generational one: Boomers and Silents report nearly a full point less drain than the youngest workers in our sample.

The pattern beneath the drain numbers is what makes this finding land. The cleanest version of the story isn’t Gen Z standing alone, it’s a broader generational shift, with younger cohorts paying higher emotional costs while simultaneously endorsing more open emotional norms. Consider the tension: 43% of Millennials and 38% of Gen Z agree that showing emotions at work is seen as a sign of weakness, compared with only 20% of Boomers and Silents. And more than half of both younger cohorts—56% of Millennials and 54% of Gen Z—say that leaders hiding their emotions creates more problems than it solves, compared to 47% of Boomers. Younger workers arrive with more emotionally progressive values than any previous generation, and they are paying the highest emotional toll for the gap between those values and the workplaces they’ve actually inherited.

The phenomenon at work is a “norms-conditions mismatch.” When your personal values are emotionally open, but you walk into a workplace that still operates on the old rules, the gap between your expectations and your reality becomes a source of constant friction. You’re doing your job and managing the dissonance between how you believe you should be allowed to act and how the system is requiring you to perform.

The external data corroborates what we found. A 2024 UKG survey found that 83% of Gen Z frontline workers report feeling burned out, higher than any other working group. U.S. research from Eagle Hill Consulting found that 54% of Gen Z report burnout, compared to 29% of Boomers. Gen Z workers are also reaching peak burnout at age 25, roughly 17 years earlier than the national average of 42.

Arianna Huffington gave this paradox a face years before Gen Z entered the workforce. On April 6, 2007, two years into building the Huffington Post into one of the most-read news sites in the world, Huffington collapsed at her desk from exhaustion and sleep deprivation. She broke her cheekbone on the way down and woke up in a pool of blood. She had been working 18-hour days, skipping sleep, and running on the fumes of what she later called a “collective delusion,” the belief that burnout is the necessary price of success. She was, by every external measure, winning. The Huffington Post was growing at an extraordinary pace. TIME magazine had named her one of the 100 most influential people in the world. And none of it had signaled that her internal systems were about to give out.

What followed were weeks of tests, doctor after doctor, searching for a medical explanation for what turned out to be a structural problem. Her diagnosis, as she later described it, was simply burnout, a term that in 2007 was not yet in common use. She went on to write two books on the subject, leave The Huffington Post, and found Thrive Global, an organization dedicated to the idea that the way work is currently designed is making people sick. Her collapse, she said, was the best thing that ever happened to her because it forced her to ask a question her board or executive team had never asked: By what sane definition was this success?

Some researchers push back on the framing of younger workers as uniquely vulnerable, noting that earlier generations also reported higher stress during career formation and that today’s tools for measuring and discussing mental health may simply make more visible what earlier generations suppressed without naming. That could very well be true. But our data points to something structural: The friction younger workers feel between what they believe work should be and what it actually is doesn’t belong to Gen Z alone. It’s the defining condition of an entire generation of workers who were handed a new value system without a new set of workplaces to go with it. We think that’s a design problem that belongs to all of us.

Number 5. Younger Workers Are the Most Emotionally Open

The generational pattern in our data is consistent: Younger cohorts are more likely to perceive their workplace as accepting of emotional expression (52.5% of Gen Z and 51.3% of Millennials, compared with 36.9% of Boomers and 44.7% of Gen X), more likely to say leaders hiding their emotions creates problems (54.1% of Gen Z and 56.3% of Millennials, compared with 47.0% of Boomers and 54.6% of Gen X), and more likely to feel comfortable when colleagues express emotion at work (72.1% of Gen Z and 73.5% of Millennials, compared with 67.5% of Boomers and 68.5% of Gen X). On all three measures, Millennials and Gen Z together represent the leading edge of a genuine culture shift in what workers believe a professional environment should feel like.

This suggests a different collective value system, one that is, by the evidence, healthier. The research consistently shows that workplaces with more emotionally open norms produce better outcomes; such as stronger psychological safety, higher engagement, lower turnover, and better creative performance.

What produces their exhaustion, then, is not the values themselves; it’s the gap between those values and the systems they work within. Across our four systemic-norm indicators, the average American workplace lands right in the middle of the 1-to-5 scale, meaning the average American workplace is neither strongly restrictive nor strongly emotionally supportive. Many are mixed, sending contradictory signals. When you believe one thing and are required to perform another, the management of that gap is its own form of work.

Even among the generation most convinced this conversation should be safe, 37.7% of Gen Z workers say their workplace still treats showing emotions as a sign of weakness. When you are the generation most likely to believe emotional openness belongs at work, and you are still walking into organizations that haven’t caught up, the friction is constant and cumulative.

There’s a broader labor history parallel here. When women entered professional workplaces in greater numbers in the 1970s and 80s, they encountered systems built entirely around assumptions about how men would work, think, and communicate. The resulting friction, having to adapt constantly to an environment designed without you in mind, was itself a form of invisible labor. What Gen Z is experiencing has structural similarities. They have arrived in workplaces that were built, in large part, by generations with different emotional norms, and the cost of navigating that mismatch is falling disproportionately on them.

Let us be clear: The friction is not one-directional. The SHRM Q1 2025 Civility Index found that about one in three employees say age or generational differences contributed to incivility they witnessed or experienced at work. Older workers can find younger colleagues’ emotional expectations demanding or performative, while younger workers can find older norms stifling or dishonest. Neither generation is wrong, exactly; they’re speaking different emotional languages in the same room.

The solution isn’t necessarily for one generation to give in to the other. But organizations would do well to ask which direction the system needs to move—and to take seriously what the friction itself is signaling.

Number 6. EQ Is Your Personal Battery Saver

If the data we collected had one simple prescription to offer, it would be to build your emotional intelligence. Not because it will make you more likable, not because it’s a nice thing to have, but because it is directly, measurably associated with lower emotional depletion at work.

Our regression analysis found that one EQ skill in particular, confidence in managing negative emotions, is significantly associated with lower emotional drain.d And the broader picture shows EQ functioning as a buffer exactly where it matters most. In a world where nearly half of workers report feeling regularly drained from managing their emotions, EQ functions as a form of internal insulation. It does not eliminate the friction, but it reduces how costly it becomes.

The workforce, as a whole, is not emotionally incompetent. Average EQ scores in our sample sit around 3.7 out of 5, a reasonably healthy baseline. Most people have some capacity for emotional awareness. The gap isn’t typically between those who have EQ and those who don’t; it’s between those whose EQ skills are strong enough to buffer the friction of their particular environment, and those whose aren’t.

Higher EQ isn’t magic or luck of personality; it’s a functional skillset. Self-awareness means you can name what you’re feeling early enough to do something about it, rather than discovering it only after it’s already shaped your behavior. Self-management means you can adjust your emotional response in real time rather than having it run away with you. Social awareness means you can accurately read others’ emotional states rather than being surprised by them. Relationship management means you can navigate conflict, give feedback, and repair ruptures without those interactions becoming their own additional drain.

Daniel Goleman described these competencies not as character traits you either have or don’t, but as learnable skills, practiced over time, degraded under stress, rebuilt through deliberate effort. The person who has practiced emotional regulation for a decade will, all things being equal, find a hard conversation less depleting than the person encountering it for the first time.

EQ may not be not a silver bullet, but in a workplace full of friction, it is a very useful shield. And right now, most of us are walking into friction-heavy environments with less of a shield than we need.

Number 7. You Can’t “Out-EQ” a Broken System

There is a genre of self-improvement advice that places the entire burden of workplace wellness on the individual. Meditate more. Sleep better. Develop resilience. Practice mindfulness. Build your EQ. As if the problem is always a gap in the person and never a gap in the system.

Our regression models showed that after controlling for EQ behaviors, systemic emotional norms remain powerful independent predictors of individual drain. Specifically, the norm that “emotions are a weakness at work” is associated with higher draine even when holding EQ constant. This means: Two equally emotionally intelligent people, one working in a culture that treats emotions as data and one working in a culture that treats them as weakness, will not experience the same level of drain. The culture matters, independent of the person’s skill.

This is an uncomfortable message for organizations that invest in individual development programs while leaving cultural norms untouched. We will say it again, as great as EQ is, it isn’t magic. You can (and should!) send every employee to EQ training.f You can offer people mindfulness apps and therapeutic services. But if the cultural air in your organization treats emotional expression as unprofessional, weak, or risky, those individual investments will be partially offset by structural drag.

The parallel in public health is useful here. Individual behavior—things like diet, exercise, and stress management—matters enormously for health outcomes. But individual behavior operates inside environmental systems, and those systems can override individual effort. A person eating a balanced diet in a food desert is still at a disadvantage compared to an equally motivated person with easy access to healthy food. The environment shapes the outcome independent of the individual’s choices.

Some might argue that individual agency is more powerful than structural analysis suggests, that high-EQ individuals can and do reshape their environments through the quality of their relationships and communication. This is true. Culture is not a prison, but it is a powerful gravitational field and pretending that individual skill can float free of it is wishful thinking if you are trying to build a healthy, sustainable culture.

As previously mentioned, systemic emotional norms average about 3.3 out of 5, suggesting many workplaces are still emotionally restrictive or mixed in their signals. When workers cannot reliably predict whether showing emotion will be received as authentic or as unprofessional, they default to suppression.

The implication for leaders then is: Your EQ development program, however excellent, is facing a headwind if your culture is emotionally restrictive. Individuals can’t outperform structural problems, at least not in a sustainable way. EQ skills help, and cultural change is also required.

Number 8. EQ Is a Shield, Not a Cure

If Number 7 is the realistic ceiling on what EQ can do, Number 8 is the floor it provides. Because even in the most emotionally punishing environments, emotional intelligence changes the equation. Just not in the way most people expect.

In workplaces where leaders are expected to keep their emotions hidden, lower EQ is associated with sharply higher drain. That is, if you work in a high-friction environment where the cultural rule is that leaders conceal their feelings, having low EQ means your emotional drain rises steeply with the friction. Higher EQ flattens that curve, and significantly. The increase in drain is still there, but it is shallower. EQ does not remove the friction, but it acts as a buffer to reduce how painful that friction feels.

This is the shield metaphor made precise. A shield doesn’t stop the battle, but it can protect you against what the battle could do to you.

The paradox embedded in this finding is worth considering: We also found that in strongly punitive emotional cultures, higher EQ is associated with slightly greater strain compared to lower EQ. This seems counterintuitive, but higher emotional awareness, in a culture that punishes emotion, means you notice the punishment more acutely. You see the norm operating. You recognize what’s happening to others and to yourself. That awareness is, simultaneously, what allows you to navigate more skillfully and what makes the navigation more conscious and more costly.

Research on emotional labor makes a similar point. Hochschild distinguished between surface acting (faking an emotion you don’t feel) and deep acting (genuinely transforming your internal state). Surface acting is associated with burnout and emotional exhaustion, while deep acting is less so. Higher EQ tends to enable more deep acting, but deep acting is still labor. The advantage is not zero effort, but it’s more efficient effort.

Interaction effects can be complex, but if you want to protect your people from emotional drain, the highest-leverage move is not more EQ training in a punitive culture, it is reducing the punitiveness of the culture. The two are not in competition. They’re both necessary. But if you must sequence them, address the system before demanding more from the individual.

Number 9. Leadership Is the Management of Emotional Energy

What does all of this add up to? Leadership is not primarily a strategic function or even a cognitive function. At its core, leadership is the shaping and directing of emotional energy in a system. When that energy is managed well, when emotions are treated as data rather than disruption, when leaders model the vulnerability they want their teams to be able to access, when the gap between personal values and system norms is small enough to navigate without constant cost, work becomes something people can sustain. When it isn’t, friction compounds into fatigue and that fatigue becomes inherent in the organization.

The person sitting across from you who seems emotionally depleted, checked out, or brittle may not completely lack resilience; they may just be someone with reasonable emotional skills operating inside an environment that is making emotional work unreasonably costly.

But that environment can change. It changes when leaders understand that their emotional behavior is not a private matter but a public broadcast. It changes when organizations stop treating emotional drain as an individual character flaw and start treating it as a signal about how the system is running. It changes when we stop only asking people to manage their feelings better and start asking our systems to make feelings less costly to have.

Leading is managing friction. Not because leadership should be hard all the time, but because leading means working inside the emotional reality of people—their hopes, fears, pride, belonging, resentment, loyalty, and exhaustion. There is no route around that fact; there is only a path through it.

The good news (and it is genuinely good news), is that we already know the way.

A Note Before You Turn the Page

The findings in this section are intended to orient you, because we need some orientation here.

In our National Emotions Survey, only a quarter of respondents said emotions were openly discussed and expressed growing up. The remaining three-quarters said emotions:

•  Were not talked about

•  Were seen as weakness

•  Were discouraged or minimized

•  Were acknowledged but not deeply explored

Most of us weren’t taught about emotions in our homes; we weren’t taught about them in school,g and then we went off to work where we were taught nothing at best or told to leave our emotions at the door at worst.

The world of work is not going to become less emotionally demanding. The rise of AI, ongoing organizational volatility, generational transition, and the deepening complexity of hybrid and remote work are all, in their own ways, increasing the emotional demands of leadership. The next decade will not reward those who manage feelings least; it will reward those who manage them best.

Part 2 of this book gives you tools for doing exactly that. But before you get there, consider this: The findings suggest that the people in your organization are paying an emotional price that often goes unacknowledged. The most powerful thing you can do as a leader is make that price a little lower, not necessarily by eliminating friction, but by helping people understand what it’s telling them. If we change the narrative around friction, it stops being the enemy and can become the thing that tells you where the real work is.
