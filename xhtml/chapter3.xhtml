CHAPTER 3 A FIELD GUIDE TO THE SCIENCE OF FRICTION

One of the hardest parts of writing a book is simply believing that it’s possible to write a book.

You may have an inkling of an idea, a compelling insight, or a way of describing something useful that others find interesting. Then you sit down to write and realize just how much needs to be done. The heft of the task sits featureless in front of you in the form of a white page and a blinking cursor. It’s no wonder that any number of potential writers lose their nerve, go nutty, or simply decide to work on the project later. This is writer’s block or what might, in other circumstances, be called procrastination. This is too much friction.

Thinking about procrastination in the context of friction provides some insight into where procrastination comes from and how it functions. Procrastination creates distance between you and a project that may be causing emotional distress. The subconscious logic is something like this thing is making me uncomfortable, and I will be more comfortable if I steer clear of this thing. Of course, we know intuitively that steering clear of the thing is only a temporary solution and often one that makes our inevitable encounter with the offending task that much more unpleasant.

Why Avoiding Hard Things Makes Them Harder

In our coaching work, we encounter this kind of circumstance all the time when talking to leaders about giving feedback.

The hardest part of giving feedback is almost always the difficult emotions that come up in being direct with someone else, having to say things that might make you (or them) uncomfortable, or the apprehension about the nature of the other person’s emotional response. And so, the easiest thing to do is to avoid the task. In fact, it’s as if this avoidance has been institutionalized into many of our organizations in the form of annual reviews. Having all participated in annual reviews, we know they don’t accomplish much other than providing commentary on a year’s worth of work. And yet, we also know from extensive research that annual reviews don’t work. Those who receive more frequent feedback (described as process feedback, rather than outcome feedback) are more motivated, engaged, productive, and less likely to turnover. Furthermore, when feedback is a more regular occurrence, it reduces the fear response in the feedback recipient, and allows them to process the information and potentially act on the insights.

So why do we persist in our annual-reviewing ways? Because when we try to change our approach, we focus on the mechanics of giving feedback rather than the emotions that underpin the action. The same is true of delegation, as we’ve described, and a number of other skills. If you only address the mechanics of the problem, you will inevitably revert to the prior way of doing things because the emotional physics will take over and push you back to the way of operating that requires the least amount of subconscious emotional energy.

The good news is, there is a raft of psychological research out there that helps us understand the dynamics of friction in a way that allows us manage it more effectively. If we imagine a chart defined by two axes—Intensity of Friction (Low to High) and Source of Friction (Internal vs. External)—we can map theories into four distinct “zones” of human experience. We think of this as the ‘Friction Field.’

The Friction Field

Here’s the thing about friction: We’ve tended to think of it like an enemy. Smooth is good. Easy is efficient. Friction only slows us down. But that’s only half the story.

Low friction may feel good in the moment. It’s the path of least resistance, the cognitive equivalent of coasting downhill, and sometimes that’s exactly what you need; when you’re performing at your best, when you’re executing what you already know how to do. When you’re in flow, the friction disappears not necessarily because the task is easy, but because your capability is matched to the challenge.

High friction, on the other hand, feels like resistance. It’s the struggle of learning something new, the discomfort of working at (or beyond) the edge of your abilities, or the difficulties that force you to think harder. It’s uncomfortable. It’s annoying, it slows you down, but that’s precisely why it can be useful. High friction isn’t always a bug; sometimes it’s a feature of growth. The art is in learning when each serves you.

•  Low Friction is for execution—allowing you to flow through tasks you have already mastered.

•  High Friction is for acquisition—providing the energy required to forge new skills.

There’s another dimension to this: Friction doesn’t just vary in intensity; it also varies in location.

Sometimes the friction is external. It’s in the task itself, the obstacles in your environment, or the challenges the world throws at you. Other times, the friction is internal. It’s in your head; it’s your beliefs about your own limitations. Unnamed and unmanaged emotions that knock you off kilter. The anxiety before a presentation. The self-doubt that creeps in when something gets really hard. This friction may be invisible to others, but you feel it acutely.

Sometimes these two dimensions align, and sometimes they don’t. You can face enormous external challenges and feel no internal friction at all. Or you can face minimal external obstacles but experience crushing internal resistance.

Understanding this distinction matters because it changes how you approach difficulty. When you’re struggling, you might ask: Where is the friction, and what is it telling me?

You’re Performing

There is something mesmerizing in watching someone move with zero drag, those magnetic moments when a person’s skill and attention align so perfectly that they seem to move with no resistance through a task. Think of the chef’s knife effortlessly chopping vegetables, the guitarist gliding through an improvised solo, or the engineer who starts tinkering with a problem and looks up only to realize they’ve worked clear through dinner and into the evening.

There’s a state of consciousness where time disappears, self-doubt dissolves, and you become completely absorbed in what you’re doing.

Athletes call it being “in the zone.” Artists talk about losing themselves in their work. Psychologists call it “flow”—and it might be the closest thing we have to a formula for peak performance and deep satisfaction in work.

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, the Hungarian-American psychologist who discovered flow, first observed it by studying people who did things purely for the love of doing them. Rock climbers. Chess players. Dancers. Surgeons. He found they all described a similar experience: intense focus, a merging of action and awareness, and a sense that the activity was somehow effortlessly unfolding even as they pushed themselves to their limits.

What Creates This State?

Flow is a precise balance between challenge and skill. When you’re doing something too easy for your abilities, you may get bored. When you’re attempting something beyond your capabilities, you may experience anxiety. But when challenge and skill are matched, other barriers fall away and you enter the flow channel.

Research shows that flow isn’t just subjectively rewarding; there is also a moderate but reliable relationship between flow states and performance outcomes in sports, gaming, and academic contexts. People who experience more flow tend to achieve better results. Interestingly, personality matters here too. People high in openness to experience find their way into flow more naturally, perhaps because they seek out the novel, stimulating challenges that create the conditions for it.

What Flow Tells You About Difficulty

And yet, in the context of friction, flow is a paradox. When you’re working at the edge of your abilities, the task you’re working on is objectively difficult. But internally the experience is one of alignment and ease. The friction disappears.

This tells you something important about challenges. Difficult does not inherently mean high friction. When challenge and capability are aligned, a challenge can create almost no internal friction. Instead of grinding in difficulty or folding into anxiety, you may experience a sense of self-forgetfulness.

Flow shows you what’s possible when your attention, your ability, and a task align, friction vanishes, and you can perform almost effortlessly. But what determines whether you even put yourself in position to experience flow? What makes you follow your curiosity and seek out the kinds of challenges where flow is possible? The answer lies not in your circumstances but in your beliefs.

You’re Wrestling with Identity

Here are a few questions worth sitting with:

•  Do you appreciate it when people—parents, coaches, teachers, and bosses—give you feedback?

•  Do you get angry when you get feedback about your performance?

•  Do you think the harder you work at something the better you will be?

•  Do you believe you can change how intelligent you are?

The answer to those questions may matter more than you might think. Psychologist Carol Dweck helps us to understand that people carry around implicit beliefs about intelligence and talent, and these beliefs shape nearly everything about how they approach difficulty. Those with what she calls a “fixed mindset” believe they’re either naturally good at something or not. Intelligence is innate, talent is predetermined, and there’s not much anyone can do about it.

Others have a “growth mindset.” They believe abilities develop through effort and learning. That they’re not born smart but become smart through practice and persistence. That they’re not born talented but can build talent through deliberate effort.

The implications of these beliefs can be quite consequential. When someone with a fixed mindset encounters difficulty, they see it as evidence of their limitations, while someone with a growth mindset might see that same difficulty as an invitation to redouble their efforts. The struggle isn’t a signal to quit; it’s the mechanism of improvement.

Mindset matters, but it’s not magic. You can’t simply decide to believe in growth and expect transformation. Changing how you think about your capabilities requires real, targeted internal work. Growth mindset research first emerged in education, and when the concept took hold in the United States, schools everywhere implemented programs to try to instill a growth mindset in students.

Years later, when researchers took a hard look at whether these interventions worked, the results were complicated. A comprehensive analysis of 63 studies involving nearly 98,000 students found the effects on grades positive, but small. At the same time, another research team found that growth-mindset interventions were modest for academics but more substantial for mental health, especially when they were targeted at those who already hold fixed mindsets, and who are in critical transition periods when they may be questioning their abilities.

When the World Engineers Out the Struggle

The core of a growth mindset is the belief that you can change and that struggle is a sign that you’re learning, not that you’re limited. Like muscles that only grow under strain, our capabilities expand when we lean into difficulty. But we are increasingly living in an era where that tension is being engineered out of our daily lives. We don’t live in a vacuum, and we don’t live in a world without the internet. We are building a world that treats friction as a defect to be corrected. If growth requires strain, we are effectively designing an environment of intellectual and emotional atrophy. And this raises an uncomfortable question: What happens when we can offload the struggle onto our machines? We may find that in our quest to make life easier, we are dismantling the very mechanisms we need to grow.

You’re Looking for Levers

Not too long ago, if you wanted to go on a road trip, you had to look at a map. You had to know the name of the highways you’d be taking, consider the best ways to drive from highway to highway, and pay attention to mile markers and exit signs. But why navigate by map (or by memory) when GPS can guide you turn-by-turn?

Why remember your grocery list when you can write it down? Why do mental math when your phone has a calculator?

We’ve become masters at offloading our thinking onto the tools around us and particularly the computers in our pockets. In many ways, it’s made us remarkably capable, and in important ways it has limited our capability.

“Cognitive offloading” is the use of external resources to reduce the mental effort required for a task. Make no mistake, this is a fundamental human capability, one we’ve been using since we first started drawing on cave walls or tying knots in string to keep track of information. Our brains weren’t designed to hold everything internally. In fact, we evolved to strategically use the environment as an extension of itself.

In many contexts, this is brilliant. Offloading routine tasks frees up mental energy for higher-level thinking. Relying on calendar alerts helps some of us ensure that we make it to any meetings at all. But in the age of AI, we’re beginning to learn that we can offload too much, too easily, onto tools that are too powerful.

Studies on AI usage and brain activity are beginning to show notably negative relationships between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking. Students using ChatGPT to write essays have demonstrated significantly lower cognitive engagement in the task than students writing without AI, and they have less recall and investment in what they turned in.

The concern is what researchers call “cognitive debt.” Shortcuts today that compound into deficits tomorrow. When we let AI solve our problems or structure our essays, we’re skipping the thinking that builds problem-solving ability.

The Case for Staying in the Struggle

But here’s what makes it complicated: Strategic offloading, paired with metacognitive awareness, can enhance learning. When students are explicitly taught to think about when and how they use AI—to remain conscious of what they’re offloading and why—AI becomes a tool for augmentation rather than replacement.

So, we have tools that eliminate friction, and we have a culture that celebrates efficiency and ease. But wait. If removing friction undermines learning—if cognitive offloading atrophies the very capabilities we’re trying to improve—how do we push back?

One place to look for insight is educational psychology.

You’re Making Good Friction

What if the approaches we take to learn new material—the methods that feel like they’re working well—are often the ones that are least effective?

This is the paradox at the heart of what memory researcher Robert Bjork has called “desirable difficulties,” a principle that turned conventional wisdom about learning upside down.

When you were in school, you likely adopted one (or all) of these study methods: You reread your notes. You reviewed material over and over in the same order. You crammed the night before the exam. These approaches seem to make good sense, they feel intuitive and you gain a sense of progress as you do them. At the time, they can create a sense of fluency, the feeling that the information is really sticking. But that feeling is misleading.

What Bjork discovered is that easy practice predicts poor retention. If it feels like there’s little friction in your study method, it’s probably too easy to be effective. Difficulty during practice—the right kind of difficulty—predicts strong, durable learning.

The techniques that have been shown to work include spacing practice over time instead of doing it all at once (no cramming), intermixing problems from different subject areas instead of focusing on one subject and then another sequentially (mix and match), testing your knowledge instead of just reviewing (test yourself), and practicing in various conditions instead of keeping everything constant (change it up). If only we had known this when we were in school …

But there’s a catch: These methods make learning feel harder. They turn up the friction. When you space practice sessions, you must work to retrieve information you’ve partially forgotten. When you mix problem types, you can’t settle into a comfortable groove while you’re studying. Testing yourself to study can seem like the educational version of putting the cart before the horse—it genuinely feels as if you’re testing yourself before you’ve reviewed the material. But that difficulty is the point. It enables deeper processing, making your brain encode and consolidate information rather than just recognize it as familiar.

The challenge here is deeper than it seems. Students actively resist these methods, even when they’re told they work better, because the experience is uncomfortable.

Desirable Difficulties at Work

The same things happen in the workplace all the time. Here are a few examples:

•  Having a hard conversation instead of letting an issue fester: It feels worse in the moment. It’s awkward. It’s avoidable (at least temporarily). But the friction of that conversation is what clears the air, builds the relationship, and prevents bigger problems from developing. Those who consistently avoid difficult conversations don’t just fail to resolve issues, they fail to develop the very capability that makes them better at resolving challenging issues in the future.

•  Sitting with discomfort instead of immediately jumping to fix or explain: When someone highlights a problem, the ambitious-overachiever tendency is to jump in and solve it immediately. Or when someone criticizes you, your instinct (like most people’s) may be to defend yourself. But the pause—that uncomfortable space where you can listen instead of react—is where emotional intelligence truly develops. You can’t build that capacity by avoiding the feeling.

•  Asking for help when you’re struggling: It’s uncomfortable to admit when we’re unsure or don’t know something. In some circumstances, it feels as if revealing uncertainty or asking for support is an existential failure. It’s often much easier to fake confidence and figure it out alone, despite how much energy and effort it may waste. But the friction of vulnerability is what builds trust, creates psychological safety, and models the behavior you want from your team.

Is the lesson here not to trust your feelings about what’s working? That might be a hard case to make in a book about learning to tune into your feelings with more intentionality, but desirable difficulties may give us a playbook for designing productive friction, for thinking about how we might opt into tasks that feel more challenging in the shorter-term in order to reap the benefit of the development they can provide in the longer-term.

Unfortunately, in the real world, you don’t always get to choose your challenges. Deadlines hit. Crises emerge. Pressure mounts. Stress finds you whether you’re ready or not. Why would you ever opt into making some things harder?
