CHAPTER 6 INTERPERSONAL FRICTION EXPERIMENTS

Much of leadership friction is relational. It works in the space between you and another person/other people: in conversations gone sideways, feedback landing wrong, a team that is stacked with talent but can’t quite make it work. Sometimes the friction isn’t yours to carry alone but you find yourself in the middle, equal parts witness and reluctant referee. Think of the two team members who can’t seem to get through a meeting without the tension becoming everyone’s problem. And sometimes what feels like a conflict with someone else has in fact been a conversation you’ve only been having with yourself; they just have no idea anything is wrong.

The experiments in this section work on that in-between space. They will not make difficult people easy or hard conversations comfortable, but they will give you more options for how you show up inside them, and more skill at reading what is happening between you and the people you work with and lead.

You don’t need to read this chapter from the beginning. If you landed here because someone on your team is in conflict and you need something you can use this week, start with Don’t Feed the Fire or The Two-Word Reset. If you’ve been avoiding a hard conversation, Set the Temperature with Your First Sentence is where to go. If the relationship you’re thinking about is one where trust has quietly eroded (and you’re not sure either of you has named it) Disagree Better or Show Love are the deeper end of the pool. The fifteen experiments below move roughly from quick interventions toward longer structural practices, but the right starting point is the one that matches the friction you’re actually carrying right now.

The Praise That Travels

Respectful Friction

Credit in Public, Correct in Private

Maggie: In early 2026, I took my Prius into the Toyota dealershipa for regular maintenance. I was sitting in waiting area, working (read: observing others and sometimes eavesdropping), killing time until they called my name, when I heard this:

“What is wrong with you? Are you awake yet? I just told you that. You always do this! Are you in interstellar land?!”

The response from the other person was muffled—a mumbled apology, barely audible. Six other customers heard the exchange. The two young women behind the desk heard it. Four or five other reps heard it. I heard it.

And I could not stop thinking about everything wrong with what had just happened.

The questions were not really questions. “What is wrong with you?” and “Are you awake yet?” are attacks disguised as punctuation. Real feedback invites dialogue; this was a monologue with question marks. The language was all judgment and no behavior; nothing observable, nothing specific, nothing a person could actually act on. And then there was “you always do this,” which is the moment feedback stops being correction and becomes character assassination. People can change behavior, they cannot un-be a person. By the time it was over, the person on the receiving end had no idea what success looked like. They only knew they had failed, in front of an audience, with no path forward.

I do not know what that employee did wrong. Maybe it was significant. Maybe it was the tenth time, or the hundredth. But the moment you deliver feedback that way, you become the bigger problem, and whatever the other person did ceases to matter to anyone watching. The message was real, the conditions were absent, and the person walked away smaller, not better.

This is why the principle of crediting in public and correcting in private is a design decision about conditions. Feedback delivered without the right conditions does not land as intended. In a psychologically safe environment, people tend to be less defensive and focus on accomplishing team goals rather than protecting themselves. But psychological safety is not a default state. It has to be built, and it is extraordinarily easy to destroy, especially when feedback crosses lines of identity, power, or surprise.

Research suggests that criticism delivered without care for tone and context feels like a threat to the person receiving it, regardless of whether the criticism is accurate. Public correction makes it worse by triggering shame, which research identifies as one of the most destructive emotions in the workplace, because it makes people want to hide, pull back, or push back. Praise, by contrast, works best when it is specific, visible, and tied to something the person actually did.

Recognition is what makes hard conversations possible; it funds the account that correction later draws from. The data on this is consistent across sources, and we examine it in depth in Show Love and The Praise That Travels. What matters here is the specific mechanism. When employees feel seen, they can absorb difficult feedback at measurably higher rates. This isn’t about being nice just to be nice. Recognition is the precondition for the feedback this experiment is designed to help you give.

The Experiment: Practice the CORE Model

Duration: Two weeks

Time required: 10 minutes of preparation, one real feedback conversation (time will fluctuate)

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Get more comfortable with feedback so you can give and get it more consistently

The CORE feedback model offers a four-part framework for structuring feedback conversations, so they are specific, observable, and forward-facing rather than personal, vague, or punitive. CORE stands for Context, Observation, Result, Expectations.

•  Context sets the stage: it ensures everyone is focused on the same issue or event and provides the opportunity to be as specific as possible—when did this happen, where, who was involved.

•  Observation describes what you saw or heard, anchored to behavior rather than interpretation.

•  Result explains the direct impact; what you thought, what you observed in others, what the effect on the work was.

•  Expectations clarify what you need going forward, whether that is a behavior change or more of the same effective behavior.

The model works equally well for praise and correction. The discipline it requires, specificity, observation over inference, impact over intent, is the same in both directions.

Step One: Choose Your Feedback

Identify one piece of feedback you have been holding, either praise you have not yet delivered specifically enough, or a correction you have been avoiding or have delivered poorly. Write it down in its current form, as you have been carrying it in your head. Be honest about what it sounds like before you shape it.

Step Two: Run It Through CORE

Rewrite the feedback using each of the four elements:

Context—What is the specific situation you are referring to? When did it happen? Who was there? What was the setting?

Observation—What did you see or hear? Strip out interpretation and inference. Describe only what a camera would have captured.

Result—What was the impact? On you, on others, on the work, on the team? Be concrete. Avoid generalizations.

Expectations—What do you need going forward? For praise: What would you like to see more of, and why does it matter? For correction: What specific change are you asking for, and what does success look like?

Step Three: Check the Conditions

Before you deliver the feedback, ask yourself three questions:

•  Is this person in a state where they can receive this?

•  Have I chosen a private setting for correction, or a visible one for praise?

•  Am I walking in as a partner in this person’s growth, or as the keeper of a grievance?

If the answer to any of these gives you pause, address the condition before delivering the feedback. The best-structured feedback in the world does not survive poor conditions.

Step Four: Deliver and Debrief

Have the conversation. Afterward, reflect briefly:

•  Did the CORE structure help me stay specific and behavioral rather than personal?

•  How did the other person respond?

•  What did I notice about how it landed?

•  What would I adjust next time?

We commonly hear from leaders that most feedbackb (positive and corrective alike) has been living in their heads in a form that is too vague, too interpretive, or too emotionally loaded to be genuinely useful to the person receiving it. The CORE structure does not remove the difficulty of feedback; it doesn’t get easier, but it gets better.

Don’t Feed the Fire

There’s a moment most leaders recognize in hindsight.

Someone challenges you in front of the team. A vendor drops the ball. A peer takes credit. A direct report says something that lands like a slap. You may physically stay in the meeting, but a different system takes the wheel. Your jaw tightens, your voice sharpens, your face goes still, you start talking faster, or colder, or louder. You might even say the “right” words, but the room can hear something else, too. That’s the moment this experiment is for.

“Image management” can sound like performance (read: faking or politics). But in real leadership, what you are working towards is harm prevention. When emotions spike, your expression (think tone, pace, posture, and timing) becomes a force multiplier. It can steady the room or set it on edge. And because authority amplifies everything, your signals carry extra weight.

Why does this happen so fast? Because your brain is trying to protect you. Under acute stress, prefrontal systems involved in judgment, inhibition, and flexible thinking become less effective, while threat-detection systems become more influential. This means you are more likely to react than respond and less able to do the subtle social work that leadership requires.

What you show in that moment becomes information. Research on emotional contagion tells us moods spread through groups and shape cooperation, conflict, and performance. Sociologists call this “affective presence”—the pattern of feelings people reliably experience after interacting with you. You can have good intentions and still leave damage behind if your signal is sloppy under pressure. And when others read your frustration as contempt or instability, it changes what they do next.

The instinct is to clamp down, but unfortunately, suppression has real costs. Experiments show it increases physiological strain, and people sense the mismatch anyway. The tight smile, the clipped tone, the blank face. One paper memorably calls suppression the “second-hand smoke” of emotion regulation.

What works better is creating a small buffer between feeling and impact.

We’ve talked about “affect labeling” (putting feelings into words).c Naming the feeling internally (e.g., “I am irritated”, “I am embarrassed”, “I feel threatened”, etc.) can reduce its intensity enough to choose a cleaner response. So can lowering physical arousal. When people become physiologically flooded, their ability to listen and problem-solve drops sharply. In leadership, flooding looks like talking over people, going rigid, being overly sarcastic, or retreating into silence. A few simple resets help: Take a sip of water, take a longer exhale and empty your lungs, unclench your jaw or your hands. And resist the urge to vent. Evidence on catharsis suggests that “getting it out” often keeps arousal high and makes hostility worse, not better.

So, what does “appropriate image management” look like in the middle of interpersonal tension?

Start with one non-negotiable: I will not make this worse. Not “I will win.” Not “I will be liked.” Just: I will not injure trust while I’m in this elevated state.

Buy 10 seconds. Use the resets above. Let your eyes widen slightly, narrowed eyes read as threat. Ten seconds can prevent a tone you’ll spend days cleaning up.

Label and meta-communicate. This is where leaders get brave in a grounded way. You might say:

•  “I’m noticing I’m frustrated, give me a minute.”

•  “I’m feeling defensive; let me make sure I’m hearing you.”

•  “I care about this, so I am going to choose my words carefully.”

This regulates you and reassures others that you’re not about to swing.

Lead with curiosity. Under threat, the mind wants a story—they disrespected me, they’re incompetent, this is political. Curiosity interrupts that reflex. Ask for specifics. Reflect what you heard. Separate impact from intent.

Repair fast if you slip. Appropriate image management includes accountability. If your tone lands hard, don’t defend it. Name it: “That came out rude. Let me try again.” People don’t need perfection. They need evidence you’re safe to work with when it’s tense.

Over time, this becomes part of your reputation. Not “never emotional,” but steady under pressure. Used well, you’ll still be direct, you’ll still set limits, you’ll still deliver hard messages. You’ll just do it without turning the room into collateral damage. And in an interpersonal pinch, that’s the difference between a hard conversation that strengthens a relationship and a hard conversation that creates a fracture.

You don’t need to be perfect, you need to be safe.d

The Experiment: The Signal Plan

Duration: One week

Time required: 5–10 minutes of preparation before a high-stakes interaction

Materials: Nothing required

Goal: Build emotional self-regulation and interpersonal trust by replacing subconscious physical “tells” with a conscious, practiced response during high-stakes moments.

Step One: Identify Your “Leaks” (3 Minutes)

Think of the last time you got activated with someone at work. Write the first three things your body did (pick from: faster speech, quieter/colder voice, raised eyebrows, narrowed eyes, tight smile, clipped answers, long silence, sarcasm, looking away, interrupting, over-explaining). These are your tells.

Step Two: Decide Your “Default Signal” (2 Minutes)

Choose one outward posture you want to be true when you’re hot—something observable.

Examples:

•  “Slower voice, fewer words.”

•  “Open hands on the table.”

•  “Ask one question before I state my view.”

•  “Neutral face + warm tone.”

Step Three: Write Your One-Sentence Meta-Script (2 Minutes)

Make it sound like you. Keep it short. Pick one:

•  “I’m getting frustrated, give me a minute to respond well.”

•  “I want to be fair here. Let me slow down.”

•  “I’m feeling reactive. Can we take five and come back?”

Step Four: Build an “If-Then” (2 Minutes)

“If I notice [my leak], then I will [my default signal] and say [my meta-script].”

Example: “If I notice I’m talking fast, then I will exhale, slow my voice, and say, ‘I’m getting frustrated, give me a minute to respond well.’ ”

Step Five: Use It Once This Week (1 Minute + The Real Moment)

Don’t wait for a big blow-up. Use it in a small tense moment: a pushback email, a meeting interruption, a misunderstanding.

Afterward (30 seconds): jot two notes:

1.  What changed in me when I used it?

2.  What changed in them?

Rush to Curiosity, Not Advice

How curious are you about other people? Take a second and really think about it.

Most people believe they are more curious about others than they truly are. They ask questions, but they are asking to confirm, not to discover. In our effort to understand one another, it’s easy to walk into rooms with the story already partially written. Then, our questions become a way of gathering evidence to support what we think rather than listening to learn something new.

Psychologists call this confirmation bias or the tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information in ways that support our prior beliefs, while discounting what contradicts them. Research on cognitive bias in organizational decision-making finds that confirmation bias is among the most pervasive and consequential of all the distortions leaders bring to their work, reinforcing existing assumptions and propagating flawed interpretations throughout teams and strategy.

Our brains are predictive organs; they conserve energy by pattern-matching at speed, generating explanations for behavior before we have finished observing it. Many of us experience this as intuition. More often, this is assumption wearing the clothes of understanding. And the cost, in organizations, is enormous. Decisions made about people and situations based on narratives that were never tested, conversations that ended before they began, or problems that compounded because no one asked the question that would have changed everything.

Tony Robbins, in his January 2026 appearance on The Diary of a CEO, returned to a framework he has taught for decades: that every human decision, including behavior we cannot explain or behavior that frustrates us, flows through three sequential decisions:

1.  What we focus on

2.  The meaning we assign to what we see

3.  What we do in response to that meaning

The story, he argues, is the invisible variable. Two people can witness the exact same event—a stranger at a door on Thanksgiving, say—and can construct entirely different meanings, entirely different emotions, and entirely different lives built on the divergence.

Most leaders, most of the time, are running stories about the people they lead. When someone pushes back on a decision, the story is: They are being difficult. When someone goes quiet in a meeting, the story is: They are disengaged. When someone misses a deadline, the story is: They don’t care. Each of these stories triggers a response, that response triggers a counter-response, and by the time anyone pauses to check whether the story was true, the dynamic feels fixed.

The alternative is not to become story-free, because unfortunately, that is not how human cognition works.e The alternative is to develop the discipline of pausing the story long enough to ask what is actually at stake for the person in front of you.

This is the core insight at the heart of Box of Crayons, the learning and development company founded by Michael Bungay Stanier and now led by CEO Dr. Shannon Minifie and VP of Product Dr. Margo Gouley. Under their leadership, Box of Crayons has scaled coach-like curiosity as a teachable, organizational skill, the idea that curiosity is not a personality trait you either have or don’t, but a practice that can be trained, reinforced, and built into how leaders operate every day. Stanier’s central argument, developed across a decade of training and explained in his bestselling book The Coaching Habit, is that the single most useful shift a leader can make is to stay curious a little longer and rush to advice and action a little more slowly. Research from Harvard Business School supports exactly why this is hard: Most people in conversation spend far more time talking than asking, and they systematically underestimate the benefits of questions, the information they would learn, the trust they would build, and the rapport they would create by simply asking rather than telling. Even people who intend to ask questions end up defaulting to advice. The reflex is that strong, and the organizational culture that rewards leaders for having answers makes it stronger still.

Let us be very clear: Advice is not wrong. What is worth naming, though, is the friction that makes curiosity so difficult to sustain. The pull toward closure is strongest when the situation is most charged. When we are frustrated with someone, when a conversation has history, when we are certain we already understand, that is when the story feels most true and the question feels least necessary. Staying curious a little longer is simple in principle and genuinely hard in practice. Advice given before understanding, however, is almost always aimed at the wrong target. Most of the time, Stanier argues, what people bring to a conversation is not the real challenge—it is the presenting challenge. The real one is underneath, and it only surfaces when someone asks.

Marc Brackett, founding director of the Yale Center for Emotional Intelligence, frames this differently but arrives at the same place. In his research on emotional intelligence and his book Permission to Feel, Brackett describes what he calls becoming an “emotion scientist,” approaching your own and others’ emotional experiences with genuine curiosity rather than judgment, the way a scientist approaches data rather than a verdict. His model—RULER: Recognize, Understand, Label, Express, Regulate—builds the skill of understanding as distinct from recognizing. Most people can read that someone is upset. Far fewer stop to ask what is driving the upset, what it means to that person, what need is underneath it. The quality of the question changes what becomes possible in the conversation.

The question that unlocks this is simple, and it has three versions depending on context:

•  What is this person protecting?

•  What is this person fearing?

•  What is this person trying to achieve?

These are not questions to ask out loud, necessarily; they are questions to ask yourself, before or during the conversation, as a way of interrupting the story and opening the inquiry.

Francesca Gino, a behavioral scientist at Harvard Business School, has found in extensive research across thousands of employees and organizations that curiosity—genuinely practiced—reduces conflict, improves decision-making, and increases innovation. Only 24% of employees report feeling curious in their jobs regularly, and 70% say they face barriers to asking more questions at work. The organizations that actively cultivate it outperform those that don’t, not because curiosity is a soft skill, but because it is the mechanism by which leaders come to understand the problems they are trying to solve—including the problem of the human being sitting across from them.

The pause is the practice. Before you decide what someone’s behavior means, before you respond, before you run the meeting or deliver the feedback or make the call—stop. Ask yourself what story you are running. Then ask what is at stake for them. The answers will often surprise you. And the conversation that follows will be different from the one you were planning to have.

Experiment #1: The 48-Hour Curiosity Tally

Duration: 48 hours

Time required: 10 seconds after each conversation

Materials: A notes app, a tally counter app, or a piece of paper divided into two columns: Yes and No

Goal: Find out how often curiosity operates in your conversations.

This experiment asks you to measure one thing across every conversation you have for two days.

Step One: The Only Question

At the end of every conversation, every single one, including the brief ones—ask yourself:

Did I learn something about this person that I did not know before?

Not about the project or situation but the person. What they are carrying, what they care about, what they are worried about, what they are hoping for. Something that would not have been in whatever story you were running about them before the conversation started.

Answer yes or no. Mark your tally.

Step Two: What You Are Measuring

You are not measuring whether you asked good questions or whether the conversation went well or whether you were kind or effective or present. You are measuring one thing: whether curiosity was operating or whether it was just something you intended to practice without quite getting around to it.

The yes/no binary is deliberate. It resists the temptation to grade yourself on effort or intention. Either you learned something new about the person or you did not. Either the conversation moved your understanding of them or it confirmed what you already thought. The tally does not care how busy you were or how short the conversation was.

Step Three: At the End of 48 Hours

Look at your ratio. Do not judge it, just look at it.

Our guess is that most people who run this experiment will be surprised by how low the yes count is, particularly in the conversations that felt most productive: the meetings where a lot got decided, the check-ins that moved quickly, the conversations with people you know well and think you understand.

Those are often the lowest-scoring conversations, because familiarity and efficiency are the enemies of curiosity. When we think we know someone, we stop asking. When a conversation has a clear purpose, we optimize for the purpose and skip the person.

Then look at the conversations that scored yes. What was different about them? What question got asked or what silence got held, that opened something up?

Experiment #2: Pause the Story Before One Conversation

Duration: One week

Time required: 5 minutes of preparation, one real conversation

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Catch the story you’re running about someone before it runs the conversation for you.

Step One: Name the Story You Are Running

Choose one person you are currently frustrated with, concerned about, or approaching with a fixed interpretation of their behavior. It might be a direct report who seems disengaged, a peer who has been difficult in meetings, or a manager whose decisions you cannot understand.

Before any conversation with this person this week, write down in two or three sentences the story you are currently running about them:

What do I believe is true about why they are doing what they are doing?

Write it plainly, without softening it; this is not a document you are sharing. The story is the thing you need to see before you can set it down.

Step Two: Ask the Three Stake Questions

With the story written, turn to the three questions and answer each one as honestly as you can from the information you have:

•  What is this person protecting?

•  What is this person fearing?

•  What is this person trying to achieve?

You may not know the answers. If you cannot answer these questions with confidence, you are running a story based on incomplete data. The gap between what you wrote in Step One and what you can answer in Step Two is where you get to work.

Step Three: Enter the Conversation Differently

Go into the conversation with one goal that is different from your default: not to explain, not to persuade, not to correct, but to find out something you do not currently know about what is at stake for this person.

Prepare one question in advance. It should be specific enough to require a real answer and open enough that you cannot predict what the answer will be. Some examples:

•  What’s been the hardest part of this for you?

•  What would need to be true for this to feel workable to you?

•  What’s the piece of this I might be missing?

•  What are you most concerned about?

Ask the question and then stop talking.

Step Four: Debrief

After the conversation, return to what you wrote in Step One and ask:

•  Was the story I was running accurate?

•  What did I learn that I did not know before?

•  What would I have done differently if I had led with this question from the start?

What we tend to find in using this is not that the story was entirely wrong; it is that it was incomplete. People are rarely doing what they appear to be doing for the reasons we assume. The colleague who pushes back on every initiative is usually protecting something. The direct report who seems checked out is usually carrying something. The question does not fix the situation, but it puts you in a fundamentally different relationship to it, one where you are working on the real problem rather than the one you projected.

Set the Temperature with Your First Sentence

A team leader ran a small experiment in their 1:1s to better understand how feedback impacted her employees. She had a genuine, positive piece of feedback for each person—specific, earned, and meant to land well. But she changed the setup, opening her feedback with a phrase most of us associate with bad news: “Before we dive in, I need to give you some brutally honest feedback.”

She was testing a simple question: What does one loaded word do to a person’s body and attention, even when the message that follows is praise?

•  One teammate felt her stomach drop and her heart rate jump (she confirmed her heart rate was still 135 beats per minute (BPM), four minutes after the feedback).

•  Another got rigid. Still, but not calm.

•  One braced like he was about to take a punch.

•  A few wanted to “just get to it.”

•  One person heard “brutal” so loudly he barely registered the praise that followed.

•  And one (because of deep trust, he said) took it as intimacy: “I thought, we’re beyond roles right now; we’re just two humans telling the truth.”

That’s the thing about language at work: Your words are never only information; they are also a social signal.

They communicate:

•  Is this safe or dangerous?

•  Am I respected or reduced?

•  Are we on the same team or opposing teams?

•  Do I belong here?

And people tend to answer those questions before they ever process your content.

Why The First Sentence Matters So Much

Your language is one of the fastest ways to raise or lower perceived interpersonal risk—which matters because, as Edmondson’s research on psychological safety makes clear, when people feel unsafe they go quiet, and silence is expensive. Your language is one of the fastest ways to raise or lower perceived interpersonal risk.

That’s where “brutally honest” gets interesting. The word brutal doesn’t just mean “direct.” It carries threat, pain, and domination; it tells the listener’s body to brace.

This is similar to what medicine calls a nocebo effect, when negative expectations, often shaped by suggestion and language, worsen symptoms or increase distress. In organizations, we do a social version of nocebo all the time:

•  “This is going to be rough.”

•  “Don’t take this the wrong way.”

•  “I’m just being honest.”

•  “No offense, but …”

These phrases prime people to brace for impact.

What Sports Psychology Gets Right About Language

In sports, language is treated like equipment. Because in high-arousal moments, the brain can’t handle long instructions. It needs simple cues.

That’s why self-talk interventions in sport psychology often focus on short, intentional phrases—instructional (“eyes up,” “follow through”) or motivational (“you’ve got this,” “next play”). Meta-analytic evidence suggests self-talk training can improve performance, with meaningful effects.

Leadership conversations are also performance moments. When you start feedback with “brutally honest,” you’re giving the brain a cue for danger. If you start with a cue like “I’m going to be clear because I care about your growth,” you’re giving a different cue: connection + direction.

A Better Frame for Honest Feedback: High Standards + Belief

There’s a beautiful, evidence-backed alternative to “brace yourself.”

In research on “wise feedback,” teachers increased students’ willingness to revise (and improved outcomes) by pairing critique with two signals: high standards and confidence in the person’s ability to meet them.

“I’m giving you this feedback because I have high expectations and I know you can reach them.”

Notice what that does:

•  It anchors belonging: I care enough about you to take the time to do this.

•  It lowers threat: Because I have high expectations.

•  It tells the nervous system: You’re safe enough to grow.

•  It keeps standards high: I know you can reach them.

Practical Language Upgrades That Build Trust

Here are a few swaps that preserve honesty and reduce unnecessary threat:

1.  Replace your typical “brace yourself” statement with a precision word.

Try: clear, direct, candid, specific, straightforward.

“Can I be really specific about one thing that will make you even stronger?”

2.  Put intent before impact.

“I’m sharing this because I want you to succeed here.”

3.  Describe behavior, not character.

Instead of “You were unprofessional,” try “In that meeting, you interrupted twice while she was answering.”

4.  Ask permission (it creates agency).

“Is now a good time for feedback or should we do it after your deadline?”

5.  Offer a path.

“Here’s what ‘great’ looks like next time …”

The Experiment: The First-Sentence Rewrite Activity

Duration: One week

Time required: 2–3 minutes of preparation per conversation

Materials: Nothing required

Goal: Practice writing the first sentence of a hard conversation intentionally so you can try to set the emotional temperature at the outset.

For one week, run this micro-practice before any potentially tense conversation (feedback, conflict, performance, a tough email).

1.  Write your first sentence as you normally would.

Example: “I need to give you some brutally honest feedback.”

2.  Label what it might signal to the other person’s nervous system:

Threat? Shame? Belonging? Partnership? Ambush?

3.  Rewrite the first sentence using this template:

“I’m going to be [clear/specific/direct], because I [care about your growth / want you to win / want us to work well together].”

Examples:

•  “I’m going to be really specific, because I want you to have the impact you’re capable of.”

•  “I have high expectations for this role, and I’m confident you can meet them. Can I share what would get you there faster?”

•  “This might feel uncomfortable, and I’m bringing it up because I respect you and I don’t want it to get in your way.”

4.  After the conversation, reflect on:

•  What changed in their body language?

•  What changed in my body?

•  Did the message land faster?

•  Did trust go up, down, or stay the same?

The Two-Word Reset

You don’t need a catastrophic blow-up to damage a relationship at work. Occasionally, we say things too directly. We overstate something to make sure the full message has been delivered. Or we simply respond more sharply to others than we mean to. If you lead long enough, you will do this, a lot more than you may like to admit. Not because you’re a bad person, but because leadership is an attention sport, and attention is not a steady resource. You get tired, you get flooded, you misread tone. You carry stress from one room to the next. And in the exact moments when your role demands the most care, your nervous system is often doing the opposite: protecting you.

“I’m sorry” in leadership is a repair tool. Used well, it can stop small mistakes from turning into long-standing damage. Used poorly (or avoided entirely) it can turn a single misstep into a story people tell themselves about you: “She doesn’t listen.” “He doesn’t care.” “It’s not safe to be honest here.”

Attachment research has a blunt lesson: Closeness isn’t built by never missing; it’s built by what happens after the miss. Relationships, whether parent to child, partner to partner, leader to team, are a constant rhythm of attunement, mis-attunement, and repair. When repair doesn’t happen, people adapt by protecting themselves: withholding, complying, avoiding, or counterattacking.

Dr. Dan Siegel’s work in interpersonal neurobiology puts a frame around why this matters at work. He emphasizes that minds are shaped in relationship, and that integration—linking what we feel, think, and do—thrives in environments where people experience attunement and responsiveness. In plain language: When people sense you’re willing to see the impact you had and take responsibility, their system can settle. They can come back online. And then you can solve the actual problem together. Let us be incredibly clear here: We don’t believe work is a therapy session or that your team is your family. But your team members still bring a nervous system to work and so do you. And in moments of interpersonal friction, that biology shows up fast.

You can see a version of this in neuroscience, too. Studies of social exclusion show that being shut out or dismissed engages brain regions involved in distress and pain, including the anterior cingulate cortex. When you snap, roll your eyes, or cut someone off, you may not mean “you don’t matter,” but their brain may hear it that way. A clean apology is one of the fastest ways to reverse that signal: I see you. You matter. We’re still on the same side.

Here’s the catch: Most workplace apologies are either too small or too self-protective.

•  Too small looks like: “Sorry if you felt that way,” (Translation: That’s your issue.) “Sorry, but …,” (Translation: Let me defend myself.) or the classic drive-by, “My bad,” delivered while you keep walking. (Translation: Please don’t make this inconvenient.) These don’t repair because they make the other person carry the reality of what happened alone.

•  Too self-protective looks like: an explanation disguised as an apology. “I’m sorry I raised my voice, but you were being impossible.” Or a performance of guilt that forces the other person to comfort you. Or a request for forgiveness that turns the spotlight back onto what you need.

Research on apologies and trust repair is helpful here because it makes this less mysterious. In studies that look at what changes people’s perceptions after a trust violation, the most valued elements are taking responsibility and offering to repair the harm, often more than simply expressing regret. And the least helpful element? Asking for forgiveness. Not because forgiveness is bad, but because it can pressure the other person to give you relief before they’ve had a chance to understand the impact.

What does an “I’m sorry” look like when it’s doing its real job?

Think of it as five moves, in this order:

1.  Name the impact. Not your intention. Impact. “I cut you off twice in that meeting.” “I raised my voice.” “I criticized you in front of the group.” Precision is respect.

2.  Take responsibility. No report out on your own stress. No “but.” “That was on me.” This is the move that signals safety fastest.

3.  Express genuine regret. Keep it simple. “I’m sorry.” Not dramatic, not performative—just clear.

4.  Make a repair offer. Repair is the part most leaders skip. It might be practical (“I’m going to correct that in the next meeting”), relational (“Can we take two minutes so I can understand what landed badly?”), or restorative (“What would help here?”). Experiments that measure emotional and physiological recovery after harm suggest that apology helps, and restitution or concrete repair can amplify the effect.

5.  Commit to a change you can keep. “Next time I feel myself escalating, I’m going to pause and ask one question before responding.” Or: “I’m going to circle back with you before I share feedback publicly.”

Notice what isn’t on the list:

•  Defending your character

•  Making the other person agree you’re a good leader

•  Getting the relationship back to “normal” in the next 30 seconds

This is leadership in the part of the job we usually want to hide. Unfortunately, this is tricky because not everyone experiences apology the same way.

•  Attachment patterns matter. People who lean avoidant (often highly competent, self-reliant, and allergic to vulnerability) tend to offer less complete and more defensive apologies when they’ve hurt someone. If that sounds like you, it doesn’t mean you don’t care. It means your system is trying to protect you from feeling exposed. The work is learning a new association: Apology is steadiness.

•  Culture matters, too. In some contexts, an apology is taken as an admission of blame; in others, it’s a way of expressing regret and preserving harmony without necessarily assigning fault. The practical takeaway: Don’t treat apology as a one-size script. Treat it as a signal of respect and responsibility that you tailor to the meaning the other person is likely to hear.

•  Power matters. Goffman described apologies as a form of “remedial” social work, an act that helps restore the moral order after a breach. In organizations, the person with more power has more capacity to breach, and more obligation to repair. When you apologize cleanly, you don’t shrink your authority. You make your authority safer to be around.

In the interpersonal friction moments, when your attention slips, your tone hardens, or your words land with more force than you intended, “I’m sorry” is one of the only tools that can rewind the emotional tape before it becomes a fixed memory. It says: I’m not above impact, I’m not too busy to repair, and I choose this relationship over my pride.

The Experiment: The Repair Rehearsal

Duration: One week

Time required: 5 minutes to debrief after a repair conversation

Materials: Nothing required

Goal: Practice this now, so “I’m sorry” is available to you later, especially when you’re tired, embarrassed, or defensive.

Step One: Pick Your Repeat Rupture

Write one sentence that starts with:

•  “When I’m under pressure, I tend to …”

Examples: interrupt, get terse, go silent, correct people publicly, multitask while someone’s talking, yell.

Step Two: Write Three Apology Version (10 Seconds / 60 Seconds / 10 Minutes)

You’re building muscle memory.

The 10-second reset (in the moment):

“I’m sorry—I just [behavior]. That wasn’t okay. Let me try that again.”

The 60-second repair (soon after):

“I want to name something: I [behavior] in [context]. I’m sorry. That likely landed as [impact guess]. What did it land like for you? And what would help repair this?”

The 10-minute repair (bigger miss):

“I’ve been thinking about [specific moment]. I’m sorry for [behavior] and for the impact it had. I’m not going to explain it away. Here’s what I’m going to do now to repair: [concrete action]. And here’s what I’m changing going forward: [specific commitment]. Is there anything I’m missing?”

Step Three: Rehearse the Deliver Like a Leader, Not a Defendant

Before you speak, do two slow exhales and drop your shoulders. Your job is to send “safe enough” through your tone, pacing, and face, not just your words.

Step Four: Add One Question

Choose one:

•  “What did that land like for you?”

•  “What would help here?”

Then listen without correcting their experience.

Step Five: Close the Loop Within a Week

Follow up: “I said I would do X. I did it. Is there anything else you need from me on this?”

This is where trust starts to come back.

Empathy That Doesn’t Burn You Out

Few words in the leadership lexicon have been more celebrated, more misunderstood, and more exhausted than empathy. It has appeared on competency frameworks, 360-degree feedback forms, and corporate values walls with such frequency that it has started to lose its shape entirely. Everyone agrees it matters. Far fewer people agree on what it is, and that gap, it turns out, is doing a significant amount of damage.

Let us start with what empathy is not.

•  It is not sympathy. Sympathy is concern for someone’s suffering from the outside, feeling sorry for someone while remaining distinct from their experience. Sympathy is “I care about your suffering,” empathy is “I feel your suffering,” and compassion is “I want to relieve your suffering.” These distinctions matter because they produce different behaviors, different neurological responses, and different outcomes for the person offering them. Most people who believe they are practicing empathy are in fact offering sympathy, a caring observation from a safe distance. That is not nothing, but it is also not empathy.

•  Empathy is also not agreement. Understanding how someone feels does not require endorsing their position or abandoning your own. Leaders often resist empathy because they fear that to understand is to concede, which it is not.

•  Empathy is not fixing. The impulse to solve or advise the moment you sense distress is one of the most common empathy-defeating moves in leadership. The person speaking does not always need resolution. They do need to feel that their experience has registered.

Now to what empathy is, and why the distinction between its two forms changes everything.

Research consistently identifies two dimensions of empathy. Affective empathy is feeling another’s emotions as your own. Cognitive empathy is understanding the feelings of others using situational information and the perspectives of others. Most people default to one or the other, and both defaults carry costs. Pure affective empathy, sustained over time, leads directly to depletion. Chronic depletion of dopamine from repeated episodes of empathic distress is what leads to burnout, characterized as emotional exhaustion, withdrawal, depersonalization, and a decreased sense of personal accomplishment. This is the origin of what is commonly called compassion fatigue, though the science is clear that it is empathy that fatigues, not compassion. Leaders who absorb their team’s distress without boundary or distance are burning themselves out in a way that ultimately renders them less able to help anyone.

Cognitive empathy, by contrast, is the form most underutilized in leadership contexts, and the most actionable. In engineering and design, it functions as the backbone of human-centered practice: understanding the thoughts, feelings, and perspectives of another individual by putting oneself in their position. Cognitive empathy requires deliberate effort to step outside your own frame of reference and inhabit someone else’s, not permanently, not self-erasingly, but long enough to understand what they are experiencing.

Research using dual-brain EEG hyperscanning—measuring the brain activity of two people simultaneously—has found that the extent to which two brains align during information processing is central to collaborative memory encoding. When people are genuinely present with each other, their neural activity synchronizes in measurable ways. Empathy, at its most fundamental level, is a physiological event, one that requires actual presence, attention, and the kind of disciplined perspective-taking that does not happen accidentally.

What most organizations call empathy training produces neither of these things. It produces nodding or the performance of listening. It produces the phrase “that sounds really hard” delivered without curiosity about what, specifically, is hard, or why, or what the person needs.

You do not need to feel everything your team feels to lead them well but you do need to understand it.

The Experiment: Deliberate Perspective-Taking

Duration: One week, applied to real interactions

Time required: 5–10 minutes of preparation per conversation

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Build the habit of entering high-stakes conversations with the other person’s frame of reference already in view.

This experiment targets cognitive empathy specifically, the practiced, deliberate act of stepping into another person’s frame of reference before or during a high-stakes conversation. It is designed to build the muscle that most leaders have left largely undeveloped.

Step One: The Setup

Identify three conversations you will have this week that carry some emotional or relational weight. These might be a difficult one-on-one, a performance conversation, a team discussion where friction exists, or even a conversation with a peer where you sense misalignment. Write them down.

For each conversation, before it happens, spend five minutes answering the following questions in writing:

•  What do I know—factually, not interpretively—about what this person is dealing with right now?

•  What pressures, constraints, or concerns are most likely shaping how they are entering this conversation?

•  What do I want from this conversation?

•  What might they want, which could be different? What assumption am I most likely to bring in that could close down my ability to hear something new?

This is a structured interruption of the default tendency to enter conversations with your own frame already fully loaded. Writing it down matters because it slows the process enough to make it real.

Step Two: During the Conversation

Once in the conversation, hold one practice rule: Before you respond to anything emotionally significant that the other person says, pause and ask one clarifying question rather than offering your reaction, interpretation, or solution.

The question does not need to be sophisticated. Some of the most effective ones are simple:

•  “Can you say more about that?”

•  “What’s been the hardest part of this for you?”

•  “What would be most useful from me right now?”

The pause and the question serve the same function: They create a brief interruption between what you hear and what you do with it. That interruption is where cognitive empathy lives.

Step Three: After Each Conversation

Write two sentences in response to each of the following:

•  What did I learn about this person’s experience that I did not know before the conversation?

•  Where did I feel the pull to fix, advise, or redirect—and what did I do with that pull?

Here is what we think you might notice. You might discover that you knew far less about what your colleagues were experiencing than you assumed, which is uncomfortable but useful. Or you discover that asking one more question before responding changed the quality of the conversation in ways that neither directive advice nor emotional mirroring would have produced.

Cognitive empathy asks you to understand better than you do now, without necessarily feeling everything, without burning yourself down in the process. For most leaders, in most situations, this is the more useful form of empathy.

Mantra Minute

Before entering a room where a difficult interaction is waiting, be it a hard conversation, a person in pain, or a colleague on the edge, there is a practice worth stealing from the world of performance psychology, especially because it takes 60 seconds. You don’t need equipment or training or an audience. It is simply this: Pause, choose one word that captures who this person is to you at their core, and hold it. A single word that reorients your identity in relation to theirs before the friction of the moment has a chance to take over. We call it the mantra minute.

The idea shares its DNA with Amy Cuddy’s now-famous 2012 research on power posing, the finding that what you do in the two minutes before a high-stakes interaction shapes how you show up inside it. The hormonal findings of Cuddy’s original study have been disputed and have not been consistently replicated, but what has held up across subsequent research is that brief pre-interaction rituals change your subjective experience of what follows, and that change in experience influences how you behave.

The research underneath this is more robust than the power pose literature. Claude Steele’s foundational work on self-affirmation theory, developed across decades of research at Stanford, demonstrated that briefly reflecting on a core value reduces defensiveness, opens perspective, and makes people more capable of responding adaptively to threat rather than reactively. Self-affirmation interventions bring about a more expansive view of the self and its resources, weakening the implications of a threat for personal integrity. What the mantra minute does is extend this inward: Instead of affirming who you are, you briefly affirm who this person is to you, and in doing so, you widen the frame through which you will encounter them.

This matters most in exactly the situations where it is hardest to do.

Greg Campbell is the kind of person who talks about his wife the way most people only talk about their heroes. He references Keisha in meetings, in passing conversations, in the way he frames what matters to him at work and at home. It is not a performance, it is the natural overflow of someone who has made another person central to how he moves through the world. Which is why it landed with particular weight when he shared, with characteristic honesty, that Keisha had been sick and that he was not a great caregiver, struggling to fully embody that role. The irritability, the demands, the regression that even the most stoic people exhibit when they feel terrible, it got to him over time, and he lost the thread of who he was to her.

When he shared this, his coach suggested he try the mantra minute. Before going into Keisha’s room, before sitting with her during a hard day, just pause. Hold one word. For Greg, the word was Queen. Not because Keisha always behaved like one when she was sick. Because she was one to him. And the word had the power to reorient him before the behavior had a chance to pull him somewhere else.

This is the core of the practice. People can be extraordinarily difficult to lead, to care for. They regress. They complain. They are ungrateful in ways that would be unthinkable on a good day. And if you let yourself respond to the behavior, it is easy to slide into resentment before you even notice it happening. The internal monologue becomes: Get over yourself or you are the most annoying person in the room right now. The behavior becomes the whole story, and the person disappears inside it.

The mantra interrupts this. It’s less about pretending the friction isn’t real and asks you to hold something truer and more durable than the moment, the identity of the person beneath the behavior, and to let that anchor how you respond, rather than letting the behavior itself set the terms.

The research on implementation intentions, the practice of specifying in advance how you will respond in a given situation, shows that pre-committing to a response dramatically increases the likelihood that you will do it under pressure. When people form a concrete intention before entering a challenging situation, they are significantly more likely to act in accordance with their values when the moment arrives, rather than defaulting to reactive behavior. The mantra minute is a form of implementation intention: It pre-commits you, relationally, to who you are to this person, before the moment demands a response you have not prepared for.

Greg’s story about Keisha is not really a story about caregiving. It is a story about how easy it is to lose the relationship inside the moment, and how a single word can give you back the relationship when the moment tries to take it from you.

The Experiment: Build Your Mantra Library

Duration: One week of reflection, ongoing practice

Time required: 20 minutes to build the library, 60 seconds per interaction to use it.

Materials: A notebook, journal, or notes app

Goal: This experiment asks you to identify the people in your life (at or outside of work) for whom a mantra would be most useful and to build a small personal library you can draw from before high-friction interactions.

Step One: Identify Your High-Friction Relationships

Think about the people you interact with regularly where one of the following is true:

The relationship carries enough history, care, or importance that you want to show up well, but the interactions are sometimes difficult. Or the person is currently in a hard season (e.g., sick, grieving, overwhelmed, difficult, etc.) and their behavior does not always reflect who they are at their best. Or you have a pattern with this person of responding reactively rather than intentionally, and you know the relationship deserves better.

List three to five people. These can be people you love, people you lead, people you care for, or people you simply want to be better with.

Step Two: Find the Word

For each person on your list, ask yourself one question: Who is this person to me, at their core, in the fullest sense of who they are?

Then find a single word that holds that truth.

Some examples of what this can look like in practice:

•  A colleague who is brilliant but prickly: Pioneer

•  A parent in decline who has become demanding: Giant

•  A direct report going through a hard time: Fighter

•  A partner who is overwhelmed and short-tempered: Home

Write it down beside each name.

Step Three: Deploy It

In the coming week, before at least one interaction with someone from your list, especially an interaction you are dreading, stop. Take 60 seconds. Say the word to yourself. Not once, but several times, slowly, until you can feel it orienting you toward the person rather than the situation. Then walk in.

Step Four: Reflect

After the interaction, write briefly:

•  Did the word change how I showed up?

•  Where did I feel the pull to react to the behavior rather than respond to the person, and did the mantra help me hold the line?

•  What did the person need from me today that I was able to give because I had grounded myself first?

•  Does the word still feel right, or does this person need a different one?

We have found that the library itself is useful. Using this tool in the moment before friction peaks is incredibly helpful to protect relationships. And, having thought clearly, in advance and outside the heat of the moment, about who each person is to you provides a clarity that will be there when things aren’t as hard and something you can reach for to deepen the relationship.

Believe Hard, Push Harder

Most feedback fails before it is finished being delivered. Not because the content is wrong, but because of how the brain receives it.

When feedback targets behavior, what someone did, in a specific situation, with a measurable impact, it is more processable. The person receiving it can examine it, respond to it, decide what to do with it. But when feedback drifts toward personality, who someone is, how they “always” are, what kind of person they fundamentally seem to be, something neurologically different happens. The brain registers identity threat, the amygdala activates, defensive responses flood in. Feedback that attacks identity destroys the psychological safety in the very moment it most needs to be present.

This is the distinction that separates feedback that changes behavior from feedback that damages relationships. Hard on the problem. Soft on the person. The problem is the behavior, the pattern, the decision, the output. The person is the full, complex human being whose growth depends on being able to hear what the problem is, which becomes impossible when they are busy defending who they are.

The research on coaching effectiveness offers a parallel insight that initially seems contradictory. A well-supported principle in the executive coaching literature is the idea of coaching the person, not the problem. Coaching is a complex dynamic change process rather than a linear input-output mechanism, where relational coaching processes significantly impact outcomes. At first pass, this seems to argue against what we just said. If feedback should target behavior, not the person, why does effective coaching focus on the person, not the problem?

The answer is that these two ideas are operating on different levels. Coaching the person means orienting your work toward growth, identity, and long-term development, not just solving the immediate presenting problem. Hard on the problem means keeping your feedback specific, behavioral, and non-punitive rather than personal and damning. These are actually complementary. The best coaching holds both at once: deep investment in the person’s potential, rigorous focus on the specific behavior that needs to change.

In March 2026, a sideline interaction between University of Maryland coach Brenda Frese and junior guard Oluchi Okananwa went viral with more than 23 million views, misread by many as a coach overstepping. Frese, nose to nose with Okananwa, pointing at her with a finger, screamed: “I believe in you, but you’ve got to want this moment.” What looked like aggression was something more precise. Okananwa had been struggling. She missed free throws, a travel, a missed layup, and Maryland was down in the third quarter of an NCAA tournament game. Frese was not attacking who Okananwa was. She was pressing hard on the specific problem in front of them, while simultaneously affirming everything she believed about the person.

Okananwa went back out and scored 21 points. After the game she said: “Coach understands I’m a competitor at heart.… I love to be coached hard, and that’s what she does with me every single day. Really what that was, was a regroup moment for myself, and her telling me she believed in me.”

Frese herself said, “You can’t have those conversations if you don’t have a relationship with them. The best of the best, the elite of the elite want to be coached hard.”

What made that intervention work was its architecture. The feedback was not “you’re not a winner” or “you’re not good enough.” It was “you’ve got to want this moment.” Behavioral. Specific. Rooted in belief rather than judgment. Hard on the problem. Soft on the person.

This is the discipline. Not softening the feedback until it disappears. Not delivering it so gently that the message never lands. The discipline is precision: knowing the difference between challenging someone’s behavior and attacking their identity, and staying firmly on the right side of that line, even when the stakes are high and the moment is urgent.

The Experiment: Know Your Person, Know Your Defaults

Duration: Two weeks

Time required: 20 minutes for the audit, 5–10 minutes per feedback conversation Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: This experiment combines a relationship audit—do you know the people you are coaching well enough to challenge them effectively—with a self-awareness practice around your own feedback defaults to close the gap between the feedback you intend to give and the feedback that lands effectively.

Part One: The Relationship Audit (Week One)

For each direct report or person you regularly coach, answer the following honestly in writing:

•  What are this person’s stated goals—not just their job description, but what they have told you they want for themselves?

•  How do they prefer to receive hard feedback? Do I know this from conversation, or am I assuming?

•  When I have challenged them in the past, how did they respond? Did it land as intended?

•  On a scale of 1–10, how much does this person trust that my feedback comes from investment in them rather than frustration or judgment?

•  Is there feedback I have been avoiding with this person? If so, what is the real reason I have not delivered it?

Be honest about the gaps. A low score on trust does not mean the relationship is broken. It means there is work to do before the high-stakes conversation. Frese spent 24 years building the kind of relationships that allowed a moment like that sideline exchange to land as belief rather than attack.

Part Two: Feedback Default Audit (Week One, Continued)

Reflect on the last three pieces of significant feedback you delivered—positive or corrective. For each one, ask:

•  Was my feedback about a behavior or a pattern of behavior—or did it slide into characterizing the person?

•  Did I anchor it to a specific situation, observable action, and concrete impact—or was it general and interpretive?

•  If the person received it defensively, is it possible the framing triggered identity threat rather than behavioral reflection?

Most people discover one of two defaults. They either pull punches until the message evaporates, or they drift into characterizations—“you always,” “you tend to,” “the problem with you is”—that land as verdicts rather than observations. Neither produces the outcome you want. Knowing your default is the first step to interrupting it.

Part Three: One Conversation, Restructured (Week Two)

Choose one piece of feedback you identified in the audit, something you have been avoiding, or something you have delivered before without it landing. Structure it using the CORE model from Credit in Public, Correct in Private, but before you deliver it, add one element that CORE alone does not make explicit and that the Frese and Okananwa story makes impossible to ignore. Belief. Name what you believe this person is capable of, and make clear that the challenge comes from investment, not frustration. That single addition is what separates feedback that lands as an attack from feedback that lands as support.

This is backed by the same research we explored earlier in this chapter.f Yeager and colleagues,g in a 2014 article, Breaking the cycle of mistrust: Wise interventions to provide critical feedback across the racial divide, showed that a relatively simple statement can help someone absorb hard feedback: “I am giving you these comments because I have very high expectations; I am confident you can reach them.” Nineteen words can change whether someone can really internalize your feedback.

The most common discovery in this experiment is that leaders who believe they know their people well often discover they have been making assumptions about how feedback lands rather than testing those assumptions. The second realization (seen in so many of our own coaching engagements) is that the feedback they have been avoiding is often the feedback the person most needs and, if the relationship is strong, is most ready to receive.

Listen for What’s at Stake

When someone tells you you’re not listening, they rarely mean your technique. Eye contact, paraphrasing, asking questions, those matter, but they’re not what people are talking about.

They’re talking about the experience of being with you. The hard reality in a fragmented and hard-to-get-your-attention environment is that listening isn’t neutral; it makes a statement about attention, respect, and safety. When someone feels heard, they don’t just feel understood. They feel seen, as in, “You are taking me seriously.” That experience has a name in relationship research: perceived partner responsiveness. It’s the sense that the other person gets you, cares about you, and is responding to you (not just to the situation). High-quality listening is one of the clearest pathways into that feeling.

The problem is that most listening gets hijacked before the other person finishes their second sentence. You can feel it in your own body. They start talking and your mind starts building. A counterpoint. A fix. A story of your own. A mental checklist of what you’ll say when it’s your turn. Sometimes what you build is generous, be it advice, reassurance, or a solution. Sometimes it’s defensive: your case, your evidence, your closing argument. Either way, the attention leaves the other person and snaps back to you.

This is the part of listening that doesn’t feel like a failure while you’re doing it. It feels like competence. It feels like speed. It feels like “I’m engaged.”

But the other person experiences it differently. They experience it as pressure, like they have to get to the point fast before you take the floor or judgment, like their story is being graded. Or worse still, they experience it as loneliness, like they’re talking at you, not with you.

There’s a useful way to describe what’s happening in these moments, and it’s simple enough to remember when you’re tired. Most of us listen on one channel at a time. We listen for facts, or we listen for feelings, or we listen for values. Facts are the easiest channel for many leaders because facts feel safe and actionable.h Feelings and values are messier. They require you to slow down and tolerate ambiguity. But they’re also where the meaning lives.

A conversation that’s “about” a missed deadline is often about reliability. A conversation that’s “about” a reorg is often about dignity. A conversation that’s “about” a tone in an email is often about belonging. Your content may be the facts, but your stakes are feelings and values.

If you want a quick test of whether you’re listening deeply, ask yourself, What do they care about that might be threatened here? What are they trying not to lose? If you can’t answer, you’re probably still listening only for the facts.

In a study comparing different response styles in initial conversations, people who received active listening responses felt more understood than those who received advice or simple acknowledgments. That result matters because feeling understood is one of the quickest ways to lower defensiveness. Not because the issue disappears, but because the person no longer has to fight to be taken seriously before they can work on the issue with you. That’s the practical promise of better listening: It changes the emotional math in the room. It turns “me versus you” into “us versus the problem,” which is almost always what you wanted in the first place.

Of course, none of this means you must turn every conversation into a therapy session. The point isn’t to drown in the friction of feelings. The point is to notice that what looks like a disagreement about content is often a disagreement about meaning. When you speak to meaning, the conversation becomes easier to move.

This is also why “helpfulness” is such a common listening failure. Advice has its place and reassurance has its place, but in the early minutes of a charged conversation, advice can land as a quiet dismissal: I already know what this is. I already know what you should do. I don’t need to understand more. And that can be true even when you’re right and even when your advice is good.

A more relationship-preserving approach is to earn the right to problem-solve by first showing that you understand the stakes with accurate reflection: “It sounds like this put you in a tough spot,” or “It sounds like you’re worried this will happen again,” or “It sounds like what matters most is fairness.” The simplest version is: Here’s what I think this cost you.

When people feel that kind of understanding, something else tends to happen. They tell you more truth, with less performance. They stop sharpening their story into a weapon and start shaping it into information. This is one of the downstream effects that shows up across the listening and responsiveness literature: High-quality listening helps create the conditions where people open up, feel cared for, and connect more constructively.

There’s another modern complication here that has nothing to do with your character and everything to do with your environment: your phone.

If you’ve ever tried to talk to someone while their attention is half on a screen, you know what it feels like. Even if they say “keep going,” you speed up. You edit. You skip the vulnerable part. You land on something safer. Research on devices in face-to-face conversations has found that the mere presence of a phone can reduce feelings of closeness and conversation quality, especially when the topic is personally meaningful. That said, the evidence isn’t perfectly consistent. Some replication work suggests the effect may be smaller or more context-dependent than the original studies implied. But you don’t need a statistic to recognize the lived and practical truth. A phone on the table can feel like an exit sign to the other person.

And when the phone is actually used—when someone is “phubbed,” phone-snubbed—relationship research links that pattern with more conflict about phone use and lower relationship satisfaction. It creates a small, repeated experience of being less important than whatever is happening elsewhere.

If you put all of this together, listening becomes less like a communication skill and more like a relationship stance. It is one of the primary ways we signal, “You matter here.” And that signal either lowers the temperature between us or raises it.

So here’s the aim: In the moments when friction shows up, don’t listen for the point. Listen for what’s at stake.

The Experiment: Stakes Listening

Duration: One week

Time required: 5 minutes of preparation, one real conversation

Materials: A notebook divided into three columns (Facts, Feelings, Values)

Goal: Practice listening for facts, feelings, and values so the other person feels understood before you move to solutions.

Use this for any conversation where you feel yourself getting reactive: feedback, tension with a peer, a partner replaying something that happened, a direct report bringing you a problem, a customer escalations call.

Step One: Pick the Conversation

Who is the conversation with?

What is the topic on the surface (one sentence)?

Why does it matter (one sentence)?

Step Two: Set a One-Line Intention

Write the sentence you want to be true when the conversation ends:

“By the end, I want them to feel ________.”

(Examples: respected, clear on the expectations, understood, taken seriously, not alone, safer telling me the truth.)

Step Three: Take Three-Channel Notes

As they talk, capture at least one line in each column:

Facts (what happened; who; when; outcomes)

Feelings (what this feels like; tone; emotional cues)

Values (what matters; what they’re protecting; what they won’t lose)

If you finish the conversation with a full Facts column and empty Feelings/Values columns, that’s your data: You listened for content, not stakes.

Step Four: Reflect Back in Three Sentences

Say, in your own words:

1.  “What I’m hearing is …” (facts)

2.  “And it sounds like this has felt …” (feelings)

3.  “And what matters most to you here is …” (values)

Then ask: “Did I get that right?”

Step Five: Ask One Stakes Question

Pick one:

•  “What’s the part of this you don’t want me to miss?”

•  “What are you most worried will happen if we don’t address this?”

•  “What would ‘better’ look like from your side?”

Step Six: Only Then, Solve

If the conversation needs action, move into it after the reflection. If you’re tempted to jump to advice early, reflect on that temptation and what may drive it (e.g., Do you love being the person who gives advice? Are you trying to get this off your to-do list? Are you trying to get out of discomfort? Or something else?).

Build the Felt Experience

A symphony orchestra is one of the most structurally unequal organizations that exists. There is a single conductor whose authority is essentially absolute. There are principal players and back-row players with formally different status. There are instruments that carry melody and instruments that carry support. There is no voting, no consensus, no flat hierarchy. And yet the research on what makes orchestras function and really sustain excellence across decades keeps returning to the same finding: the quality of the interpersonal bonds between musicians, and the felt sense that each person’s presence matters to the whole, is among the strongest predictors of cohesion, resilience, and collective output. Researchers studying music ensembles have found that collaborative music-making is, at its core, a model for something deeply human: the way people attune to each other, adjust in real time, and create something together that none of them could produce alone. When the connection between players breaks down, everyone hears it immediately. Leaders in organizational settings rarely get that kind of real-time feedback; the mechanism is the same.

What orchestras have always known, and what organizations are only beginning to name, is the difference between psychological safety and psychological togetherness. They are not the same thing and conflating them has cost us something important.

Amy Edmondson’s work on psychological safety is rigorous, the evidence is compelling, and the concept has given leaders a concrete goal to create an environment where people can speak up, take risks, and make mistakes without fear of punishment or embarrassment. That is not a small thing. The absence of psychological safety is genuinely costly. But psychological safety describes the floor, the minimum condition for participation. What it does not fully describe is what most people hunger for, which is not merely the absence of fear but the presence of something: the felt sense that the people around them know them, care about them, and would notice if they were gone. That is togetherness. Unfortunately, building one does not automatically build the other.

Deloitte’s 2020 Global Human Capital Trends research, drawn from nearly 9,000 respondents across 119 countries, found that belonging ranked as the second-highest organizational priority globally. But the more important finding was structural: Deloitte identified three distinct pathways that produce felt belonging, comfort, connection, and contribution, and found that most organizations only reach the first two. Comfort means feeling respected and treated fairly. Connection means having relationships with colleagues. Contribution means seeing, viscerally, how your individual presence makes a meaningful difference to the people and outcomes around you. It is that third level, contribution, that 63% of respondents identified as the mechanism by which belonging drives performance, and it is the level most leaders never deliberately design for.

The gap matters because contribution-based belonging is not produced by policies, perks, or even by psychological safety initiatives. It is produced by the quality of the interpersonal experience; by whether people feel genuinely known to one another, not just professionally familiar. This is what the orchestra gets right by necessity. Every musician in an ensemble knows, in the most immediate sensory way possible, that their presence changes what is happening, that if they stop playing, the whole changes. They attune to the conductor and also to each other, in real time, adjusting constantly to what the people around them are doing. Research on music ensemble performance describes this as “participatory sense-making,” the process by which people come to understand each other and the world by participating in each other’s behavior and intentions. In organizational life, this is rarely designed in. It happens accidentally, if at all.

This is the ambition of psychological togetherness as a leadership practice is to build the felt experience that they matter to one another, especially across tension, difference, or change. A team can have strong psychological safety and still feel like a collection of individual contributors who happen to share a calendar. Togetherness is different. It is the condition in which people feel that they are, in some meaningful sense, playing the same piece, and that what each person does ripples outward and is received by the others.

The same mechanism operates in less extreme contexts. Deloitte’s 2024 research found that organizations making meaningful progress on human sustainability, of which belonging is a central component, are nearly twice as likely to achieve their desired business outcomes.

The leader’s role in all of this is not to manufacture warmth or engineer intimacy. It is more precise than that. It is to create the structural conditions in which people encounter one another as full human beings—and to do so deliberately, repeatedly, and especially when the pressure to skip it is highest. Togetherness is built in the small moments of genuine attention, in the questions that treat someone’s inner experience as worth knowing, and in the acknowledgment that a person’s presence changes things. This is something to consider before things get hard, so that when it does, they don’t break.

The conductor cannot make an orchestra play together by instruction alone. What they can do is create the conditions in which every musician, no matter where they sit, knows they are part of something that requires them specifically.

The Experiment: The Team Design Canvas

Duration: One meeting, 60–90 minutes

Time required: 30 minutes of preparation for the leader, 60–90 minutes with the team Materials: A shared document, whiteboard, or digital canvas; one section per person

Goal: This experiment uses a structured team exercise to surface what people need from one another.

What This Is Not

Before describing the exercise, it is worth naming what it is not. It is not a personality assessment, a strengths inventory, or a team-building icebreaker. It does not require vulnerability theater or enforced emotional disclosure. It is a design exercise that treats the question of how people need to work together as a legitimate organizational design problem, worth the same rigor and intentionality as any other.

Step One: Prepare the Canvas

Before the session, create a shared document or whiteboard with one section for each team member, including yourself. Each section has four prompts. Send these to participants at least 24 hours in advance so people can reflect rather than react:

1.  What I bring that people might not know about or fully use

2.  What I need to do my best work that I rarely ask for

3.  What signals that something is wrong for me, and what I usually do when that happens

4.  What it looks like when I am most fully in it

Step Two: Each Person Fills Their Section

Give people 24–48 hours to complete their section privately. Frame this as a design contribution; they are helping the team understand how to work well with them, not performing openness for its own sake. The prompts are deliberately functional. Even people who are guarded about emotional disclosure can usually answer the question “what do I need to do my best work?”

Step Three: Run the Session

In the session itself, each person shares what they are willing to put into the room. The facilitator’s role (which can be the leader, or someone else) is not to interpret or respond to each person’s contribution, but to ask one follow-up question after each person shares:

Is there anything here that would be useful for us to hold for you when things get hard?

This question does the structural work of the exercise. It moves the conversation from information-sharing to active commitment; from “here is what I am like” to “here is what I am asking you to notice and hold for me.”

Step Four: Capture What the Team Is Committing To

At the end of the session, document two or three things the team has agreed to notice and hold for one another.

For example:

•  When Raj goes quiet, we check in rather than assume everything is fine.

•  When Yesenia says they need more context before deciding, we treat that as signal-gathering, not delay.

•  When Joan says they’re good, we know to ask one more time.

The most useful moment in this exercise is what they add when asked the follow-up question. That is the information the team most needs, and it is almost always the information no one has ever asked for. Also watch for is who hesitates before the follow-up question. Hesitation is often the signal that the person is deciding whether the room is safe enough to say the real thing. How the room receives that hesitation tells you a great deal about where the team’s togetherness currently lives.

Disagree Better

On March 27, 2022, roughly 16 million people watched Will Smith walk onto the Oscars stage and slap Chris Rock across the face. The moment went everywhere, replayed, analyzed, memed, condemned. What most of the commentary missed, however, was what the moment illustrated about conflict: not that Will Smith was a bad person, but that he was a person whose emotional system had overridden his rational one before he had processed what he was feeling or what outcome he was after. He moved directly from trigger to action, skipping every step in between. That is the anatomy of unproductive conflict, not evil, not stupidity, but the collapse of the gap between feeling and response.

In his first public interview after the incident, Smith told Trevor Noah: “That was a rage that had been bottled for a really long time.… Hurt people hurt people.” Later, in an Instagram video, he said: “I was fogged out by that point. There is no part of me that thinks that was the right way to behave in that moment.” What Smith’s reflection reveals, and what he could not access in the moment itself, is that the slap was not really about Chris Rock. It was the eruption of something much larger, something that had been building long before the joke landed.

Psychologist John Gottman calls this state “emotional flooding,” the point at which the sympathetic nervous system’s stress response overtakes the brain’s capacity for rational thought. Gottman found that when heart rate exceeds 100 beats per minute during conflict, productive communication becomes physiologically impossible. The prefrontal cortex, the region responsible for reasoning, perspective-taking, and impulse control, effectively goes offline.

Most of us will never slap anyone at the Oscars. But most of us, in some form, have walked into a disagreement already flooded, already defending a position before the other person has finished speaking, already composing our rebuttal while they are still mid-sentence, already treating the conversation as a contest to be won rather than a problem to be understood. We may do it more quietly than Will Smith did, but the mechanism is the same.

The research on what makes conflict productive is consistent across fields: The quality of listening precedes everything else. And most people, most of the time, are not actually listening. They are waiting for a gap, for a weakness, for their turn.

Gary Friedman, co-founder of the Center for Understanding in Conflict and a Harvard Law School collaborator, spent four decades developing a model of mediation built on a deceptively simple insight: Understanding must precede resolution. In his Understanding-Based Model of Mediation, Friedman and his colleague Jack Himmelstein identified what they call Looping—a four-step practice designed to demonstrate, in a way the other person can feel, that you are genuinely trying to understand them.

It works as follows:

•  Step One: Listen for what is being said. What is the person most upset about, what emotions are threaded through their words, what metaphors and strong language are they using? These are the signals of what actually matters to them.

•  Step Two: Paraphrase in your own language. Not a verbatim repeat, which can feel like mimicry, but a genuine attempt to translate their meaning into your words. The effort itself demonstrates that you were actively trying to understand, not just waiting for your turn.

•  Step Three: Check if you got it right. “Did I get that right?” This is the hinge of the whole practice. Almost always, the person will correct you, add something, or clarify, and in doing so, they reveal more. The “loop of understanding” is used to see if, and demonstrate that, you understood them. People often misunderstand the things other people say, especially when they disagree.

•  Step Four: “Tell me more.” The impulse (and it is a powerful one) is to ask plot questions, offer a parallel story from your own experience, or jump to advice. Resist all of it. Before you do any of that, you must prove to the person that you are trying to understand them, not because you will necessarily agree with what emerges, but because understanding is the precondition for anything productive that follows.

The paradox at the heart of Looping is that it feels counterintuitive in exactly the moments it is most needed. When we are flooded, when the stakes feel high, when we deeply disagree, that is precisely when the instinct to listen collapses and the instinct to win takes over. Looping is a forcing function for the opposite movement: into curiosity, into the understory of what the other person is carrying, into the slow and difficult work of making someone feel genuinely heard.

Listening is not passivity or agreement. A leader who Loops is not surrendering their position, they are building the conditions under which their position can be heard in return. You cannot skip the understanding and get to resolution. The only path to productive conflict runs directly through the other person’s experience of being genuinely known.

Will Smith eventually understood this. “I had to humble down,” he said, “and realize that I’m a flawed human.” The work of productive conflict is the same humbling, of the certainty that our version of events is the whole story.

The Experiment: One Conversation, Looped

Duration: One week

Time required: One conversation, 30–60 minutes

Materials: A notebook to prepare and debrief

Goal: This experiment asks you to choose one conversation this week where you know disagreement is present and practice looping deliberately from start to finish, to practice making the other person feel genuinely heard before you ask them to hear you.

Step One: Choose the Conversation

Pick a real disagreement or tension you have been carrying. Not the highest-stakes conflict you can think of—choose something real but navigable. A friction with a colleague. A recurring tension with a team member. Something you have been avoiding or have already handled poorly once.

Before the conversation, write down two things:

1.  What is my goal for this conversation—not what I want to win, but what I want to understand?

2.  What do I already believe this person thinks or feels—and how certain am I that I am right?

The second question is the more important one. Your assumptions about what the other person means are the primary obstacle to hearing them. Name them before you walk in so you can catch them when they interfere.

Step Two: Run the Loop

In the conversation, commit to the following sequence—in this order, without skipping steps:

Listen. When the other person speaks, track what they are saying and how they are saying it. What words carry the most weight? Where does their tone shift? What are they most animated or most careful about? What are they not quite saying?

•  Paraphrase. When they have finished, or reached a natural pause, reflect back what you heard in your own words. Be specific. Not “I hear you saying you’re frustrated” but “It sounds like what bothered you most was not being consulted before the decision was made, is that right?”

•  Check. “Did I get that right?” Then stop talking and listen to their correction or confirmation. Do not add your response or explain yourself. Just receive what they offer.

•  Invite more. “Tell me more about that.” Or: “What was that like for you?” Or simply: “What else?” Do this at least once before you offer anything from your own perspective.

Only after you have completed the full loopi should you begin to share your own view.

Step Three: Debrief

After the conversation, write briefly in response to these questions:

•  What did you learn about this person’s experience that you did not know before?

•  Where did you feel the pull to skip the loop and defend yourself or offer your own view? What triggered it?

•  Did the conversation feel different than your typical pattern in disagreement? What changed—or did not?

Show Love

There is a manager we know—brilliant, results-oriented, deeply respected—who once told us, with complete sincerity, that he did not think it was his job to care about his people. His job, he said, was to get results and that caring was someone else’s department.

We hear versions of this more often than you might expect. Not always stated so plainly, but present in the way some leaders run their one-on-ones (or the way they cancel them five minutes prior), in the distance they keep, in the belief, often unconscious, that professionalism and warmth are in tension with each other.

They are not. But the confusion is understandable, and it has a psychological foundation worth naming.

Will Schutz, a Harvard scientist who developed the FIRO-B assessment in the late 1950s through his work at the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory, identified three fundamental interpersonal needs that drive human behavior: inclusion, control, and affection. The need for affection refers to the emotional connections between people and the extent to which individuals seek to establish relationships with others, particularly one-on-one relationships. Critically, Schutz found that these needs vary significantly across individuals. Some people have a low need for affection. Invitations to join the group may seem obligatory, being singled out may feel negative, and collaboration may feel like a time waster. This is not coldness. It is a different interpersonal wiring, and it is entirely possible to be a leader with low affection needs who nonetheless leads with genuine care.

The distinction that matters is this: You do not need to be personal, but you do need to be personable. Care is not the same as closeness. It does not require knowing someone’s childhood or their weekend plans. It requires knowing that they exist as a full human being, and treating them accordingly.

Brené Brown’s research on daring leadership arrived at this conclusion with striking force. After years of studying what separates courageous leaders from armored ones, her data returned a finding she described as solid as a rock: You must care for and be connected with the people you lead. When she first presented this finding at a high-level Air Force base, working with fighter pilots and squadron commanders, the response was not what she expected. She incorrectly assumed her data wouldn’t be well received. Her Air Force contact told her they took that idea to the next level; if you didn’t feel affection for someone, you weren’t allowed to lead them.

Gallup’s employee engagement survey captures this dynamic through one of its most debated questions: Q10: “I have a best friend at work.” It consistently ranks among the most mocked items on the survey, and among the most predictive. Globally, three in ten employees strongly agree they have a best friend at work. By moving that ratio to six in ten, organizations could realize 28% fewer safety incidents, 5% higher customer engagement scores and 10% higher profit. The “best friend” framing is not asking managers to manufacture intimacy. It is a proxy for trust, indicating the extent to which trusting relationships exist within a team.

What does intentional investment in those relationships look like? On the Fixable podcast, hosted by Harvard Business School professor Frances Frei and leadership expert Anne Morriss, actors Kate Moennig and Leisha Hailey, best known as Shane and Alice on The L Word, and best friends in real life, described how the showrunner began building storylines directly around their real-world friendship. The creative output deepened because the human connection was genuine and deliberately prioritized. The lesson for leaders is not to cast their teams like a television show, but to recognize that real relationships, when protected and invested in, generate something that no org chart or strategy document can produce.

What Organizations Can Do When Q10 Scores Are Low

Care is not a personality type, it is a practice. And like any practice, it can be built, even by people for whom warmth does not come naturally, and even by organizations that have historically left this work entirely to chance. Care is also structural, and organizations that treat it as an optional cultural comfort will find, eventually, that it was load-bearing all along.

Because leaders arrive at this strategy from very different starting places, we offer two experiments, one for those who already believe in the value of care and want to deepen how they express it, and one for those who are more skeptical and need a lower-stakes point of entry.

Experiment #1: For Leaders Who Already Believe and Want to Deepen the Practice

Duration: 30 days

Time required: 10 minutes per week upfront, plus small daily micro-acts

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: You already know care matters. The question this experiment asks is: How specifically and intentionally are you demonstrating it?

Week One: The Knowledge Audit

For each person on your team, write down what you know about them as a human being. Not their performance, not their role, but them. What do they care about outside of work? What motivates them? What are they proud of? What are they working through?

Then ask honestly: Where are the gaps? Who do you know well, and who remains, for all practical purposes, a stranger? The gaps are your map.

Weeks Two Through Four: Intentional Acts of Care

Each week, choose two people from your gap list and do one small, specific thing that demonstrates you see them. This does not need to be dramatic. It might be:

•  Asking a genuine follow-up question about something they mentioned last week

•  Noticing and naming something they did that went unrecognized

•  Sending a brief, specific note of appreciation

•  Asking “What do you need from me right now?” and staying quiet long enough to hear the answer

At the end of thirty days, return to your knowledge audit. What changed? What do you now know that you did not before? Who showed up differently, or did you?

Experiment #2: For Leaders Who Are Skeptical, Start Smaller Than You Think

Duration: Two weeks

Time required: 5 minutes per day

Materials: Nothing required

Goal: If the idea of “showing love” at work makes you uncomfortable, that is useful information. It is not a reason to stop reading, but a reason to start carefully. This experiment does not ask you to change your personality, share your feelings, or become someone you are not. It asks only for curiosity.

The Practice

For two weeks, once per day, ask one person on your team a question. It does not have to be personal. It does not have to go anywhere. Try to find a question you are genuinely curious to hear the answer to. Some examples:

•  “How was your weekend?”—and then listen.

•  “What are you working on that you’re excited about?”

•  “Is there anything getting in your way right now that I could help remove?”

That is the entire experiment. One question. One genuine moment of attention per day.

The Reflection

At the end of two weeks, notice three things:

Did anything surprise you about what you learned?

Did any of your working relationships feel, even slightly, different?

What did it cost you (e.g., time, discomfort, energy, etc.) to do this?

The goal of this experiment is not conversion. It is data. Most skeptical leaders who run it honestly discover two things: The cost was lower than they expected, and the return, in trust, in information, in team responsiveness, was higher. Care, it turns out, is a performance variable, and you do not have to feel it warmly to practice it well.

See the Real Work

Most leaders miss talent because they’re moving quickly. The calendar is stacked. The meeting starts late and ends late. Someone pings you with “quick question” and you answer it like it’s a mosquito, swat, keep walking. As we talked about previously, you have a whole team in front of you, doing a hundred small things right, and the moments that break through your attention tend to be the ones that go wrong.

Which is a problem, because talent rarely announces itself with a trumpet.

•  Maybe it shows up as an offhand comment in a meeting that makes the whole plan clearer.

•  It could show up in the way one person can calm a tense customer without sounding like a script.

•  It shows up in the analyst who can take messy information and tell the story in three sentences.

•  It shows up in the new hire who somehow gets people talking (really talking) on day four.

None of those are always in the job description. And they’re easy to overlook if your leadership model is “manage tasks” instead of “develop people.”

There’s a quiet shift happening in good leadership: from being the person with the answers to being the person who can see (not in the mystical way, in the practical way). Seeing patterns, effort, progress, and what someone does when they’re not performing their role but revealing their strengths.

Anthropologists have long argued that humans are built to track competence and value in one another—who’s good at what, who can teach, who can be relied on. That’s part of how groups survive. Status isn’t only taken through dominance; it’s often given through prestige, freely conferred respect for someone’s skill or contribution. When leaders name what someone does well, they’re not just being “nice.” They’re participating in an ancient social system that tells the group: This matters; this person matters.

And our brains seem to treat it that way. Social approval and reputation light up reward circuitry in the striatum, in ways that overlap with how we process other rewards. On the flip side, being ignored or excluded can register as “social pain,” with overlap in neural systems that respond to physical pain. “Being seen” physically lands in the body.

Catching people using their superpowers is specific, observant, it names a strength in action, and it often shows up in the margins: the thing someone does between tasks, or the way they do the task that no one asked them to do.

If you want a research-backed reason to care about small moments like this, zoom out to motivation. Self-determination theory argues that people thrive when three psychological needs are supported: competence, autonomy, and relatedness. A leader who notices strengths feeds competence (“I see what you can do”), supports autonomy (“I trust you to use it”), and deepens relatedness (“you matter here”). That combination is the real performance multiplier.

It also changes how people perform over time. Teresa Amabile and Steven Kramer’s work on inner work life shows that “small wins”—tiny experiences of progress—can have outsized effects on motivation, creativity, and engagement. When you catch a superpower in the act, you’re often highlighting a small win the person didn’t even register as a win. You’re making progress visible.

And then there’s the relationship side of it. In close relationships, one of the most consistent findings is that it’s not only how we respond when things go wrong, but also how we respond when things go right. Research on “capitalization” finds that when someone shares good news, the listener’s active-constructive response (enthusiastic, specific, engaged) is linked with better relationship outcomes. Work relationships aren’t marriages, but they are relationships. People bring good news to work in tiny forms: “I figured out a better way,” “The client finally said yes,” “I got the data to make sense.” A leader who responds actively and specifically builds trust faster than one who nods and moves on.

So why don’t we do it more? It asks for attention, first. The kind you have when you’re actually looking for patterns. It asks for emotional comfort, too. Some leaders are uneasy with positive emotion because it feels like a loss of edge, or because praise was rare in their own upbringing and they learned to equate silence with approval. And it asks for generosity. If I can name your strength, I might be admitting I don’t have it in the same way, and that can quietly threaten a leader who thinks they need to be the smartest person in the room.

But the bigger cost is what happens when people aren’t seen. In fast-moving environments, what gets reinforced is what gets measured, and what gets measured is often what’s easiest to count. That creates a gap between the “formal work” and the “real work.” The formal work is the deliverable. The real work is how people carry each other through pressure, how they translate, how they anticipate, how they repair a mistake, how they teach others without being asked. When you never name the real work, people assume it doesn’t count. That’s where interpersonal friction starts to grow, not as a dramatic conflict, but as a slow leak of goodwill.

Jobs are changing too fast for job descriptions to keep up. Research on job crafting describes how employees actively shape their roles—tasks, relationships, even the meaning of the work—often in ways managers don’t fully see. Sometimes that crafting is exactly where someone’s future value lives. The person who “helps everyone onboard” might be your next enablement lead. The person who can translate technical detail into plain language might be your next product marketer. The person who always spots downstream risk might be your next ops leader.

If leadership is partly talent scout, then your job isn’t only to evaluate performance, it’s to discover potential that hides outside titles. (Even Harvard Business Review has argued that modern talent spotting is shifting away from fixed competencies toward potential and adaptability because the work keeps changing.)

Again, your attention is contagious. As we explored in Don’t Feed the Fire, the emotional stance you bring into a room spreads, and that works in both directions. When a leader is only scanning for problems, the team feels it. When a leader is scanning for strengths in motion, people loosen, they take more healthy risks, and often offer more of themselves.

The strategy, then, is simple and demanding. Slow down enough to notice the signal, then name it with care.

Not “you’re amazing.” More like: “When you did X, I noticed Y, and it changed Z.”

That kind of feedback does three things at once. It reinforces the behavior. It teaches the person what they’re good at in a way they can repeat. And it teaches the team what this group values.

Over time, it also gives you a richer map of your people. You stop thinking of them as roles (“the analyst,” “the PM,” “the assistant”) and start thinking of them as strengths portfolios. That’s how you build careers and organizations, not just quarterly performance.

And if you want a final, very practical reason: Recognition is rare. In a large Gallup/Workhuman analysis, only about one in four employees strongly agreed they’d received recognition or praise for good work in the last week. That’s not an “engagement problem.” That’s an attention problem. The good news is you don’t need a budget to fix it. You need eyes.

Exercise: The Superpower Scout Log (7 days)

Duration: 7 days

Time required: 5 minutes daily

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Train your attention to spot strengths in motion—especially the ones outside formal roles—and turn them into development.

Step One: Pick Your Lens (2 Minutes)

For the next week, you’re not looking for “who’s the best.” You’re looking for signals. Use this list as a prompt:

•  Clarifies complexity fast

•  Calms tension without avoiding truth

•  Anticipates downstream risk

•  Builds trust quickly with strangers

•  Makes others better (teaches, coaches, connects)

•  Spots patterns in messy info

•  Moves work forward quietly (removes blockers, bridges teams)

Step Two: Capture Three “Strength Sightings” Per Day (5 Minutes)

At the end of each day, write three quick entries. Use this format:

•  Moment (facts only): What did I see/hear?

•  Superpower (name it): What strength was operating?

•  Impact (who/what changed): What got better because of it?

•  Where else could this matter?: What bigger work could use this?

Example:

•  Moment: “In the client call, Maya summarized the disagreement in 20 seconds and everyone nodded.”

•  Superpower: “Sense-making under pressure.”

•  Impact: “Lowered heat; sped decision.”

•  Elsewhere: “She could lead the next cross-functional alignment meeting.”

Step Three: Deliver One “See It. Say It. Shape It.” Message Per Day (60 Seconds)

Send a Slack, text, or hallway note using this script:

“I noticed [specific action]. That looked like [strength]. It mattered because [impact]. I’d love to see you use that more on [future opportunity].”

This is recognition plus development. It tells them what to repeat.

Step Four: End the Week with One Talent-Scout Conversation (15 Minutes)

Pick the person whose superpower showed up most clearly—especially if it’s outside their job description—and ask:

1.  “When do you feel most useful at work?”

2.  “What kinds of problems do you naturally run toward?”

3.  “What work drains you faster than it should?”

4.  “If we made 10–15% of your next month more strengths-shaped, where would you put it?”

Close with one concrete experiment (two weeks max): a small project, a meeting to lead, a mentoring role, or a cross-functional task that lets the strength work harder.

Step Five: Reflection (Write This):

•  “Who on my team is currently under-seen?”

•  “What strengths am I rewarding by default (speed, compliance, polish) … and what strengths might I be ignoring (repair, translation, courage, steadiness)?”

•  “If I acted like a talent scout for one hour a week, who would grow fastest?”

The Praise That Travels

If you want to hear the most honest and specific positive feedback in an organization, go to the retirement party.

It’s usually the first time people say the quiet part out loud. You were the steady one. You made this place better. I don’t know how we would’ve gotten through that launch without you. Everyone nods, half relieved, half regretful, relieved because the truth is finally being said, regretful because it took a departure to make it speakable.

Most leaders I meet aren’t stingy with appreciation, they’re just late with it.

They notice the extra care someone put into a client call. They feel grateful when a teammate stays calm under pressure. They see the quiet person who fixes problems before anyone else realizes they exist. And then … the moment passes. There’s always another meeting, another email, another fire that looks more urgent than a moment of gratitude. The appreciation stays in your head, unspoken, like a draft you never send.

That’s how positive feedback becomes something we reserve for major milestones; at best, the performance review, the promotion announcement, the farewell lunch, the retirement party, at its worst, a funeral. We act like the only appropriate time to name someone’s impact is when they’re halfway out the door. It’s an odd pattern when you say it out loud: We wait until someone is leaving to tell them why we liked having them here.j

There are a few reasons this happens, and none of them are “because you’re a bad leader.” One is simply human attention. Negative information is stickier than positive information. We’re wired to scan for threat, error, and what might go wrong, and that bias shows up everywhere, including how we talk to other people. The research version of this is unfortunate: “bad is stronger than good” across a wide range of psychological phenomena. So even if you have ten good observations about someone’s week and one concern, the concern is more likely to get airtime.

Another reason is cultural. Many workplaces treat praise as a reward you have to ration, as if it’s a scarce resource that loses value if you use it too often. Some leaders inherited a model where you only speak when something needs fixing. We might assume people “already know” they’re valued, especially if they’re competent, so silence is in and of itself a compliment: If you don’t hear from me, it means you’re doing fine. Unfortunately, humans are funny, and we use our big brains to make up wild stories when there is a lack of data.

This is one of those interpersonal friction points that doesn’t look like conflict, it looks like professionalism or efficiency. It looks like “I’m not a gushy person.”

Yet the cost accumulates: People disengage, they stop taking initiative, they stop bringing you the messy early drafts that you need if you want to lead well.

A third reason is more personal, and it’s the one that creates the interpersonal friction worth working with: Some people are uneasy with positive emotion. Praise can feel intimate or exposing. It can even feel risky, like you’re giving someone power, or lowering standards, or setting a tone you won’t be able to keep up. If you grew up around restrained affectionk (or built your career on being the unflappable one), offering warm, direct appreciation may feel strangely hard, even when it’s true.

There’s also the fear of getting it wrong. If I praise you for something you didn’t value, will it feel like I don’t understand you? If I praise you in public and you hate attention, will it land like a punishment? (Some peer-to-peer recognition systems backfire for exactly that reason: What people imagine as motivating can register as awkward, political, or even diminishing, depending on context and culture.)

What the evidence suggests is not that praise should be constant, grand, or theatrical. It’s that specific, believable recognition changes how people feel about themselves and about the relationship. In one well-known set of studies, being thanked made people more likely to help again, in part because it increased their sense of social worth and efficacy. Recognition also functions as reinforcement. Across organizational behavior studies, recognition interventions are associated with meaningful improvements in performance, especially when paired with clear feedback.

And beyond the behavior change angle, there’s a relationship angle that matters just as much. Responding well to the good stuff is one of the ways trust gets built, a finding we examine more fully in Be a Superpower Scout, where the research on capitalization shows exactly what an active, specific response produces in work relationships.

Now for the part most leaders miss: Some of the most powerful positive feedback is the kind that gets said when the person isn’t in the room.

We usually call that gossip, and we usually mean it as an insult. But anthropologists and social psychologists have long pointed out that “talking about people who aren’t present” is also one of the ways groups create shared norms and social cohesion. And organizational research increasingly distinguishes negative workplace gossip from positive workplace gossip, the latter being things like acknowledging someone’s contribution, skill, or integrity when they’re not there. Studies have linked positive workplace gossip to outcomes such as engagement, loyalty, and even innovative behavior, depending on trust and context.

This matters because reputations are built in absent rooms.

When you praise someone to their face, you’re giving them a moment. When you praise them to other people, you’re giving them a standing. You’re shaping what the team believes is valued. You’re making it easier for that person to be trusted, staffed on meaningful work, invited into influence.

There’s even neuroscience that helps explain why this lands so deeply: Social reward (especially a “good reputation”) activates reward-related brain areas that overlap with those activated by money. Status and belonging are experienced in the brain as reward.

So when you say, in a meeting where she isn’t present, “I want to name what Maya did here. She kept the client calm and she protected our team,” you’re doing more than being nice. You’re reinforcing values and distributing social safety.

If you want to make this real, the shift isn’t “give more compliments.” It’s:

•  Smaller: Short, specific acknowledgments that don’t require a big speech

•  Sooner: Close to the moment, not weeks later

•  More portable: Said in rooms the person isn’t in, credibly, concretely, and without exaggeration

The point is to tell the truth out loud, in real time, while it can still do its job.

A useful test: If your praise could apply to anyone, it won’t land for anyone. “Great job” is cotton candy. “You were the first person to slow the room down and name what was at stake” is a mirror.

And because some people have a complicated relationship with praise, you can make it easier to receive by giving it a clean structure:

•  What you saw (observable)

•  Why it mattered (impact)

•  What it says about them (character/strength)

•  Where you want more of it (future)

That structure helps you stay grounded and helps the other person trust that you mean it. It also keeps positive feedback from becoming a once-a-year event.

Final note: If you’re the kind of person who doesn’t need praise, that may be true for you.l But leadership isn’t only about what you need or how you operate, it’s about what the relationship needs. The real loss is that leaders miss chances to strengthen relationships while people are still here to receive it, while it can still increase trust, effort, and connection, instead of becoming a speech at the exit. Sometimes the smallest act of care is adding a little friction to your own habits: pausing, naming the good, and letting it be seen.

The Experiment: Write a Toast for Your Team

Duration: One week

Time required: 5–10 minutes daily

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: This is not a speech you have to deliver. Think of it as a practice that trains your attention and gives you language you can use so you can be more consistent in providing valuable, positive feedback weekly, maybe even daily!m

Step One: Choose Three People

Pick:

1.  Someone who carries a lot quietly

2.  Someone who recently grew (even if imperfectly)

3.  Someone you rely on but rarely name

Step Two: Use this Template to Write a 90-Second Toast for Each

1.  The moment I’m thinking of:

Name a specific scene (meeting, project, client moment, tough week).

2.  What the person did (observable):

No adjectives without evidence. Describe behavior.

3.  The impact (on people/work):

What changed because of them?

4.  What it reveals about them:

A character strength: steadiness, courage, care, judgment, patience, initiative.

5.  What I hope they keep doing:

A simple future-facing line.

•  In the room: Share one sentence directly with them this week (Slack/Teams, email, or face-to-face).

Respectful Friction

In the 2013 film World War Z, Gerry Lane, a retired United Nations investigator played by Brad Pitt, arrives in Jerusalem to understand how Israel managed to build a wall around the entire city before the zombie pandemic consumed the rest of the world. He meets Jurgen Warmbrunn, a high-ranking Mossad official, who explains the decision that made it possible. When Israeli intelligence intercepted a message about soldiers fighting the walking dead, ten analysts reviewed it. Nine dismissed it as absurd. But because nine agreed, the tenth was institutionally required to dissent, to take the threat seriously, investigate it independently, and plan for the possibility that the other nine were wrong.

That structural requirement to disagree is what saved Jerusalem (at least for a while).

The concept is fictional in its precise form, but it is grounded in a real practice. Israeli intelligence has long maintained mechanisms designed to surface contrarian assessments, not because dissent is comfortable, but because consensus, unchecked, is dangerous.n The practice has a name as old as the Catholic Church: devil’s advocate. And the research on why it matters is both consistent and underutilized.

Psychologist Irving Janis coined the term groupthink in 1972 to describe what happens when cohesive groups prioritize harmony over accuracy—when the desire to agree overrides the discipline of thinking well. Janis identified eight symptoms of groupthink, among them self-censorship of deviations from group consensus, a shared illusion of unanimity, and direct pressure on any member who dissents. The conditions that make groupthink most likely to occur are precisely the conditions many leaders work hard to create: tight-knit teams, strong shared culture, high trust, and low conflict. The very things that make a team feel good together can make them collectively worse at making decisions.

This is the paradox at the heart of respectful friction. Groupthink results in decisions of low quality and a group’s inability to explore more alternatives. And the devil’s advocacy technique has the potential to enhance decision quality and creativity, as well as avoid groupthink, especially when the devil’s advocate practices the Socratic method, asking open-ended critique questions rather than simply declaring opposing opinions. The key word in that finding is practiced. Dissent that is assigned, structured, and normalized lands differently than dissent that erupts spontaneously. The tenth person is a role, one the group has agreed in advance to honor.

The implications for leadership are direct. Teams that are high in agreeableness, deeply aligned in values, or composed of people who genuinely like one another are not immune to bad decisions, they are more vulnerable to them. Pressure to reach consensus can lead to groupthink and to the suppression of relevant data. The friction that feels rude in a harmonious team is often the friction that is most necessary. The question is not whether to introduce it, but how to do so in a way that the team can receive it. Psychological safety, which we examine in depth in Build the Felt Experience, is the condition that makes structured dissent possible. Without it, the tenth person role becomes performative more than anything else.

Respectful friction is a design choice. It names disagreement as a feature, not a bug. It removes the social cost of being the one who pushes back, because the role has been agreed to in advance. And it creates the conditions for teams to be good to work with and genuinely good at the work.

The Experiment: Institutionalize the Tenth Person

Duration: One month, applied to real decisions

Time required: 10–15 minutes per meeting where a significant decision is being made

Materials: A rotating assignment and a simple set of questions

Goal: Build structured dissent into how your team makes decisions.

The Setup

As a team, agree to adopt a rotating tenth man role for any meeting where a significant decision is being made. The role rotates so that no single person becomes the permanent skeptic, and so that every team member develops the muscle of structured dissent.

Before the next such meeting, name the tenth person for the session. Everyone in the room should know who holds the role and what it requires.

The Role

The tenth person’s job is not to be contrarian for its own sake, and not to torpedo decisions already well-reasoned. The role is to ensure that the group has genuinely examined the decision rather than arrived at it through the path of least resistance. The tenth asks:

•  What are we not considering?

•  What would have to be true for this to be a bad decision?

•  Who is not in this room whose perspective we are missing?

•  Are we agreeing because this is the best option, or because it is the most comfortable one?

The tenth person does not need to believe the counterargument. The role is to make the argument anyway, and to make it seriously, with evidence where possible, so the group is genuinely tested.

The Debrief

After the decision is made, spend five minutes as a group reflecting on three questions:

•  Did the tenth man surface anything we had not considered?

•  Did the process change the decision, or strengthen our confidence in it?

•  Was the friction respectful, and if not, why? What would have made it more so?

One interesting side effect of doing this well over time is that the team usually gets better at disagreeing without it feeling personal. When dissent is structurally expected, it stops being an act of disloyalty and becomes an act of care. The tenth person is the one making sure the wall gets built.o
