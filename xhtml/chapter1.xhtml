CHAPTER 1 THE PROBLEM WITH PHYSICS

I don’t mind restrictions / or if you’re blacking out the friction / it’s just an escape / it’s overrated anyways.

—Ben Gibbard, Death Cab for Cutie, “Blacking Out the Friction,” 2001

In 1941, John Gillespie Magee Jr. was training as a fighter pilot in the Royal Canadian Air Force. Stationed north of London in Wales, he was learning to fly spitfires to fight in WWII.

It was August, unusually wet and cool that year, but one could imagine a day with a break in the weather, the sun shining, the sky filled with towering cumulus; Magee set out on a high-altitude test flight in his quick, agile plane, and when he landed, filled with the creative spirit, he put pen to paper:

Oh! I have slipped the surly bondsa of Earth

And danced the skies on laughter-silvered wings

Sunward I’ve climbed, and joined the tumbling mirth

of sun-split clouds,—and done a hundred things

You have not dreamed of—wheeled and soared and swung

High in the sunlit silence.

This first stanza of the poem, “High Flight,” completed in September of the same year shortly before Magee was killed in a training accident, has since spread through our culture. From books to TV shows to movies, it is still the official poem of the Royal Air Force, the Royal Canadian Air Force, and is ritually recited by first class cadets (seniors) at the United States Air Force Academy.

There is something about this poem that speaks to human nature and our desire to soar, to “slip the surly bonds of Earth.” This desire is nothing new—from the ancient myth of Icarus flying too close to the sun, to Elphaba’s solo, “Defying Gravity,” in the smash hit musical and 2024 movie adaptation Wicked.

We are desperate to be unbound by the laws of physics, to transcend our limitations, to be relieved of the forces that hold us back and slow us down. The sky—limitless, open, weightless—feels like the opposite of this constraint. We may be intellectually aware that resistance is necessary, yet like Magee, Icarus, and Elphaba, we are drawn to experiences that feel free of it. We must live within real constraints, yet we are continually seduced by frictionlessness.

Reducing Friction

This is a noble aim, isn’t it? After all, friction is everywhere, and it’s a drag.

Sure, there’s the resistance you learned about in grade school, the kind that keeps your coffee cup from sliding off your desk, or that gives your shoe a firm grip on a step. And then there’s the interpersonal and bureaucratic kind we associate with human behavior and getting things done.

Think of the DMV—a classic high-friction environment—where the lines are long, the forms are confusing, and it can feel as if it’s deliberately designed to frustrate you. Or consider a simpler example: Not long ago, the grocery in Ross’s neighborhood went through a changeover from a “Greenwise” (a Publix specialty store) to a standard Publix. The process involved updating the food stock and reconfiguring the aisles to the standard Publix layout. No longer could people come, grab the two or three things they needed and zip out. Pallets were scattered throughout the store where restocking was taking place, and shelves remained empty during switchovers. Customers were seen to be standing looking around, asking employees for guidance, trying to reorient themselves amid their everyday habit, now doused with friction.

This was a minor inconvenience of course, and yet the change was notable. You don’t appreciate how much mental energy your brain conserves during the day until the friction level increases and you realize you’ve been running on autopilot. These are small moments, but they add up. They’re the moments where you feel the difference between life gliding along and life grinding a bit more than it needs to.

In some ways it feels like reducing friction is the primary aim of modern life. We want fewer lines, fewer forms, fewer clicks, fewer steps between desire and satisfaction. We want to move from point A to point B—with our groceries, our packages, our decisions, our careers—with minimal resistance between desire and fulfillment.

This drive toward frictionlessness has become the organizing principle of entire industries, not least the technology sector clustered in and around Silicon Valley. As journalist Meghan O’Gieblyn describes it:

The endgame was the same for everyone: […] A world freed of decision-making, the unnecessary friction of human behavior, where everything—whittled down to the fastest, simplest, sleekest version of itself—could be optimized, prioritized, monetized, and controlled.

Many of the apps and services we use remove so much friction that consuming products and managing relationships can feel like nothing more than pushing buttons while sitting on the couch. You can order food to your door with a click, set up a date with a swipe. Meetings that once required hours of travel can now happen anywhere with a screen and a WiFi connection. Research that once required hours of digging and reading can be delivered almost at will with a search. Artificial intelligence (AI), particularly large language models, is the latest and most extreme expression of this trend. With a prompt, an AI chatbot can draft an email, summarize a meeting, create a strategy document, or write an essay in seconds.

The Effect of Frictionlessness

In theory, frictionless productivity is a boon for the worker and the leader. With the thinking machine ever at the ready, we no longer need to feel the friction involved in getting more done, more quickly. If you’re a leader, you can maybe get by with a leaner team or can increase your expectations for what can be accomplished. You can imagine a world in which you spend less time on bureaucratic nonsense and more time on the work that matters.

And yet, as we have all experienced (and research has shown), offloading too much of a task to AI causes a measurable reduction in brain activity; it makes it harder to recall or explain what has been accomplished and (unsurprisingly) decreases feelings of ownership over what’s been produced.

On several occasions, we attempted to use AI to help think through and organize the contents of this book. It seemed like such a simple task. Just drop in a few key concepts we want to talk about—friction, emotion, leadership—prompt the sense of the style we’re after, and let it rip. Each time AI produced compelling-enough ideas. Topics and chapter titles that fit together nicely and seemed to flow with a predictable rhythm from one to the next. And yet, each time we tried to use AI to help reduce some of the friction of co-authoring, it felt like we lost our way. We got chapter titles that sounded interesting but didn’t mean anything. The more we tried to leverage AI to help, the more the writing resisted us (or perhaps we were just resisting the writing).

We kept bumping up against the same realization: The struggle was the point. The disagreements, the false starts, the moments of wrestling with a sentence, the discomfort of not quite knowing what we think yet, the writing was the thinking. When we tried to use AI to help, the friction we relied on to do meaningful work was gone, and eliminating it didn’t make the work easier, it made the work vanish. Ben Gibbard saw it clearly enough to put it in a song: Blacking out the friction is just an escape, and it’s overrated anyways. We kept having to learn that the hard way.

Which raises the question: Should eliminating friction be the chief aim of our working lives? Or do we need to be more precise about what kind of friction we’re talking about and which parts of it we might need?

Emotional Friction

One could hardly argue against the benefit of reducing the time it takes to renew a driver’s license, because it’s a hassle to do it and making it faster and easier is only gain. But the kind of friction that seems to linger the longest and hurt the most is not waiting in line at the DMV; it’s the emotional kind.

We’re talking about what it feels like to keep moving through your day while your thoughts, feelings, and relationships push back on you. We’re talking about the knot in your stomach before a hard conversation, the spike of irritation when someone talks over you, the quiet resentment when you stay late three nights in a row and no one seems to notice. We’re talking about the low-grade dread on Sunday night when your mind starts scrolling through the week ahead. We’re talking about the drag you feel when you stare at a blank slide, open your inbox instead, and suddenly it’s thirty minutes later.

We’re not trying to draw a hard line between “physical/logistical friction” and “emotional friction,” because in real life they constantly feed each other. A clunky system can create emotional friction. Emotional friction can slow people down and make simple tasks feel burdensome. A broken process creates agitation and cynicism, a leader’s avoidance stretches timelines and increases rework, whereas a small dose of structure or ritual—a deliberate logistical friction—can calm people down and help them focus. Reducing physical friction (clearer process, better tools) often reduces emotional friction (less dread, more agency); and emotionally intelligent leadership often smooths the path for physical work to get done. Frictions stack and loop.

The mistake we’ve made, especially in an age that prioritizes speed and ease over everything else, isn’t that we confused these kinds of friction, it’s that we’ve treated all friction as the same problem to wipe out. If something feels harder, slower, or heavier, we assume it’s problematic and our instinct is to streamline, automate, or offload it, without questioning what we might be trading away.

Some friction is a feature, not a bug: the thoughtful pause that keeps you from saying the thing you’ll regret, the uncomfortable question that surfaces a real risk, the hard conversation that resets a team. By sliding past the parts of our work that may be most demanding in service of making it easier or quicker, we are often sliding past the most essential parts, the places where diligent thought or deep conversation may reveal the most insight, generate the most connection, or provide the most meaning.

So, our stance isn’t “friction is bad” or “friction is good”; it’s that friction is powerful, and it’s already everywhere in your life and leadership whether you like it or not.

The real work is to notice it, name it, and try to interact with it more directly:

•  Where should we remove friction so people can do their best work?

•  Where should we expect it and stay steady through it?

•  And where might we even design a bit of friction in, on purpose, because that’s where meaning, learning, and trust are built.

Leadership as Emotional Work

This is a reality we have both experienced many times in our work with clients and thought it would be useful to share a concrete example of what this looks like.

Ross: A few years ago, I was on site with a client facilitating a leadership development program. It was morning and the group was gathered around a large wooden conference table in the executive boardroom, coffees still steaming. We had assembled to talk about delegation. In fact, the plan was to spend half a day talking about delegation. I’ll be honest, even writing that sentence makes it seem boring to me. Delegation? For half a day?!

I was serving as the “leadership expert” in this program, and it is perfectly reasonable to sort delegation into the leadership bucket; it is, after all, a required skill when leading a team well. And yet, I felt slightly out of my depth on the topic. I knew about delegation, I could describe it, talk about the key elements that make it effective, reference the relevant research, but the truth is that my professional life up to then had been largely organized around work that involved not delegating. Most of my work—executive leadership coaching—consists of me operating as a solo practitioner. Although I could bring book knowledge to the table, I knew I’d be doing so in a room filled with corporate managers—de facto experts in the day-to-day process of delegating. So, instead of charging in with a slide deck full of how-to’s, I kicked off the session by inviting them into my predicament.

“I’m not convinced there’s anything I could tell you today that will change what you know about delegation,” I said. “You wouldn’t be in this room if you didn’t know what it was and how to do it with some level of proficiency. Now, that doesn’t mean it’s easy to do. In fact, I’m certain it can be quite challenging. But that’s a question I’d like to pose to you—what is it that makes delegating hard?”

They left the boardroom to discuss in small groups. As I walked around to check on them, the conversations were notably engaging. The topic had generated real energy as leaders from different parts of the company commiserated on the challenge of delegation and the real barriers—structural and emotional—that made delegating hard. They distilled their conversations into lists written with colorful markers on poster-sized sticky notes, and when we came back together as a large group, they posted them around the room and had a conversation.

Most of the delegation barriers were not entirely surprising: Delegating simply makes it harder to get something done, something that you already know how to do, quickly and accurately. It takes time to train others, to manage delegated tasks alongside your own workload, and to navigate the preferences you may have for how something should be done. And this is to say nothing of whether you have clear authority to delegate.

But here’s the barrier that stood out most: not wanting to delegate out of “selfishness.” When I asked for clarification, we got into a conversation about identity; we talked about the difficulty of letting go of tasks and skills that have helped you succeed in the past, and we talked about the feelings of loss associated with the change.

“We have a word for this feeling,” I offered, “though we rarely use it when talking about our work, it’s grief.”

The tone of the conversation shifted. We spoke less about the tasks involved in delegating—things like defining the objective, clarifying authority, agreeing on a timeline—and more about feelings and emotions that frequently accompany these kinds of tasks, like uncertainty, fear, and excitement. Yes, there are technical challenges to delegating effectively; yes, it requires training and skills. But frequently, the stickiest barriers are emotional. Delegation often means surrendering control, adapting to imperfection, and trusting others. It requires grappling with and adapting to a new working identity that may involve less task-oriented work and different measures of what it means (and feels like) to be productive or to contribute.

We closed the session by talking about how the underlying challenge of leadership, even more fundamental than the knowledge, skills, and competencies that we typically talk about, is learning to take on new challenges, to let go of old ways of thinking, and to continually adapt to new circumstances.

This delegation conversation is a case-in-point of a larger dynamic. Helping a leader understand and manage emotions is a different kind of “skill-building.” It’s learning to interpret the emotional friction they feel as data about what’s important and what needs attention—helping them change the way they understand themselves and their work, and how their work impacts others.

Now, let’s zoom out from this single session …

The Everyday Life of Leading a Team

Leadership usually gets measured in numbers: revenue, margin, headcount, turnover, quality scores. If you look at most performance reviews for senior leaders, that’s what you see. Maybe there’s a section at the bottom for “people” or “culture,” but it tends to come after the financials and the project updates.

We want to you to consider the task of leading from a different angle by making a simple claim: Emotions are not background noise to our work. They are the material we’re working with every day whether we ask for it or not. Strategy, decisions, and performance all move through emotional channels: fear and trust, urgency and apathy, pride and resentment, hope and fatigue. Leadership lives in that traffic.

If that sounds like a stretch to you, you’re not alone. Most leaders in organizations are held to account for numbers, not feelings. And yet you can see it in the first five minutes of almost any meeting.

A leader walks into the room already irritated. People feel it before a single word. Shoulders tighten. Jokes die halfway out of someone’s mouth. When that leader speaks, every comment lands with extra weight. Fewer people take risks. Someone with a concern stays quiet. The conversation stays narrower than it needed to be. On paper, it looks like a solid hour of work on the project. In the room, it’s a team squeezing itself smaller to avoid getting hit.

Now change the scene.

Same leader, same pressure. This time they open with, “I know tensions are high right now. Before we get into the numbers, I want to hear what’s got you worried about this project.” People may not suddenly kick back and relax, but there’s a small shift. A few honest sentences. A risk someone had been sitting on comes into view. The plan that emerges is a little sharper, a little more grounded in reality.

Nothing changed in the business, but the emotional conditions changed in the room.

When you pay attention to how results happen, emotions are hard to ignore. Teams doing complex work need to admit errors, ask questions, and say out loud when something is going sideways. That only happens when people feel safe. That feeling is built in small moments: how you respond to bad news, whether you mock or respect a challenge, whether you roll your eyes or stay curious.

Teams that feel safe surface risk faster. They test and adjust. They recover from errors instead of hiding them. On charts, that shows up as fewer mistakes, faster learning, and better quality. Underneath those numbers is emotional work: how people feel in your presence, in your meetings, when they see your name on their calendar or email.

When people quit, they talk about how they felt: ignored, dismissed, taken for granted, overloaded, alone. They rarely say, “My boss didn’t understand our pricing model.” They say things like, “I felt invisible,” “I was constantly on edge,” “I didn’t trust them,” “It felt like no one had my back.” Those emotions drive whether people stick with an organization, how much effort they give, and how much of their brain they bring into their work.

Results are the mandate of leadership, but emotion is the medium through which leadership succeeds or fails.

Betting on Friction

When Philip Lorish opened Commerce Street Books in 2025, his independent bookstore in Charlottesville, VA, he was most concerned about the emotional barriers that consumers face in an ever-faster-moving economy. In an interview around the time of opening he was asked about the biggest challenges of running a brick-and-mortar bookstore today and said:

“Friction. People are used to getting whatever they want immediately, and reading takes time and practice. We’re not just competing with Amazon, we’re competing with streaming, social media, the whole culture of convenience. If we only try to compete on ease and price, we’ll lose. But if we can create a place people want to be—a place that offers human connection, surprise, and beauty—then we have a shot. I like to say we’re not just selling books; we’re selling the conditions in which books can change your life.”

Lorish’s bet wasn’t just a business model, it was an experience he wanted to create for his employees and customers. He was choosing to lead them toward certain kinds of friction rather than away from all of it. His vision was that if he could turn up the friction of book buying—not by making it hard to buy a book, but by reintroducing an emotional connection to the idea of reading—that he’d be able to provide a superior experience.

He offers a sense of community and human connection to his customers, in effect making the argument that the experience of in-person browsing outweighs the ease of online searching. And he’s banking that friction itself—the slowness, the serendipity, the social interaction—is where value lives.

This example serves as an object lesson. It’s a concrete instance of our argument: that some of the friction we’ve been trying to eliminate is in fact the condition for the work and change we say we want. To design for certain kinds of friction, rather than to reflexively eliminate as much as possible, is precisely what we see missing in many organizations today. Leaders default to optimization, not because they’re wrong to value efficiency and productivity, but because they frequently haven’t considered what gets lost in the process of optimizing. Which raises deeper questions for you:

•  What does friction feel like in your own work?

•  Where is it wasted effort and nonsense?

•  Where is it a signal that something important is at stake?

What “Good” and “Bad” Friction Gets Wrong

We are not the first people to write a book about friction. The most prominent recent entry in that conversation is The Friction Project by Stanford professors of organizational behavior Robert Sutton and Huggy Rao, published in 2024. It is a careful, research-rich book. We recommend it, particularly if you are a leader trying to understand the operational and structural costs that unnecessary friction imposes on people and organizations. Their central argument is that the best leaders are “friction fixers,” people who make the right things easy and the wrong things hard. They describe years of research across hundreds of organizations and document, compellingly, how friction accumulates in working life the way clutter does (think: rules, red tape, pointless meetings, cascading approvals) until its weight becomes the dominant feature of how work feels.

We agree with much of this and we’ve seen it ourselves, as employees in organizations and through our work with organizational clients. Organizational friction that has nothing to teach you is absolutely worth fixing.

But here is where we see things differently.

Sutton and Rao organize their framework around a distinction between “bad friction” (the kind that slows people down, demoralizes teams, and should be removed) and “good friction” (the kind that forces genuine deliberation, slows down unethical decisions, or protects people’s time). A large majority of the work is about identifying and eliminating bad friction. Good friction is acknowledged, but it arrives mostly as a footnote to the main argument.

We noticed something else. In the index of The Friction Project, emotionb does not appear as a category. We find this remarkable, not as a criticism of Sutton and Rao, who were writing a different book for a different purpose, but as an indication of how much further the conversation needs to go. When we look at the friction that most consistently shapes whether leaders succeed or fail, whether teams trust or fracture, whether organizations adapt or fossilize, it almost always involves emotions, and not naming it makes it seem as though we have no control over changing it.

We also diverge on the good/bad framework itself. It’s useful as a diagnostic—is this friction serving us or costing us?—but we think the binary misses something important. Our argument is that friction is neither good nor bad, it is data about what is happening inside you, between you and another person, or inside the system you’re operating within. Like all data, its value depends on what you can read from it and what you do with it. Friction that feels bad may be the most important signal you have. If you categorize that friction as “bad” and move to reduce it, you may be solving the wrong problem and treating a symptom rather than understanding a deeper root issue.

What Sutton and Rao do well is in the structural domain, the processes, the policies, the organizational conditions that make certain behaviors easy or hard. We want to go somewhere different: the emotional domain. Not what conditions make the right behaviors easy? But what is this feeling trying to tell me, and what would it mean to actually listen to it?

That question gets answered by developing the capacity to notice what you’re feeling, understand where it’s coming from, and use that information rather than push past it in the name of efficiency. This is a different kind of friction work, and it is the work this book is designed to help you do.

Leadership Is Emotional Intelligence

Leadership always and only exists where there are other people. That’s because leadership is a social phenomenon. You can be brilliant, skilled, and visionary on your own, but until your decisions and behavior intersect with other human beings, you’re not leading, you’re just thinking.c

This is why we define leadership as emotional work. Not “emotional” in the sense of drama or sentimentality, but in the sense that it is always happening in an emotional field. Every decision, every conversation, every change initiative, every project plan lands in people’s nervous systems before it ever lands in a spreadsheet.

Simply, effective leadership isn’t just enabled by emotional intelligence, it is emotional intelligence in action. Strategy, judgment, technical skill, these things of course matter, but your ability to lead people—to turn plans into coordinated action with others—rises and falls on how you work with emotion: your own, theirs, and the emotions generated by the system you’re all creating and operating within.

Our emotions are always at work, and yet, we don’t tend to think of emotional intelligence as the substrate underneath other skills; we think of it as a distinct skill. In fact, we tend to think of it as the softest of “soft skills.”

The military is where the distinction between “hard” and “soft” skills first emerged. In technically focused engineering training, “hard skills” focused on the use of things made of metal, like weaponry, machinery or vehicles. Any skill that involved people or paper was “soft.”

Does that metaphor still serve us in professional life? The world we work in now is more relationally complex, more interdependent, and more emotionally demanding than the environments that those categories described. The irony is that “soft” skills are, in our experience working with thousands of executives and senior leaders, often the hardest to master, and the ones that determine whether the “hard” skills ever have their intended impact.

Ross: At an offsite with a group of senior leaders we spoke about the challenges of growing leadership capacity. We talked about taking on more responsibility, meeting larger challenges at a faster pace with bigger teams, all while maintaining the same calm and equanimity frequently expected of senior leaders (especially in Fortune 500-type organizations). Toward the end of a session about self-awareness, one executive raised his hand and asked: “I understand why it’s important to learn about this stuff and I can see how it will make me more effective. But when does this get easier … when will I feel like I have a handle on it?”

Here’s what I said: It never gets easier, but it always changes.

If the scale of your leadership mandate is growing, if the purview of your leadership is changing, then your work is becoming more complex and ambiguous, and your experiences of friction will keep changing shape. Just when you think you have a handle on everything, COVID will hit, or AI will explode, or new tariffs will require you to pivot, learn, and adapt.

We should expect this pattern! Leadership is not a simple task. In many ways, it has never been more challenging. You can thank hybrid, remote, and geographically dispersed teams making it harder than ever to connect and build culture, AI-powered technologies reshaping the way we work, and the presence of five (soon to be six) generations in the workplace approaching work in their own ways.

It can be tempting in the face of all this to retreat into “logic.” Emotions, the thinking goes, are messy and biased. If we want better decisions, we should focus on the data and strip the feeling out. The intention is good: less ego, less drama.

The science tells a more interesting story. People with damage to parts of the brain that handle emotion can still solve logic puzzles. They can weigh pros and cons. What they struggle with is choosing. They can’t decide what to eat, which job to accept, or what plan to back. They have an incredibly hard time being successful if they only have access to logic. Emotion is part of how we discern importance and urgency; it tells us, “This matters more than that.”

In real life organizations, leaders swim in partial information. Data is late, incomplete, or conflicted. The clock is ticking. There are trade-offs. You feel a knot in your stomach about one option before you can fully explain why. You sense resistance from the team even though no one has explicitly disagreed. Your own pulse picks up when someone challenges your favorite idea. All of that is emotional information.

Ignoring it doesn’t make you objective, it just sends those reactions underground. They transform into stubbornness, avoidance, or the subtle pull to listen more to the people who agree with you and less to the ones who don’t. The room reads your mood anyway. People adjust. They stop bringing you certain kinds of news. They protect you from discomfort instead of protecting the work from risk.

Treating emotions as part of the work looks very different. It looks and sounds like:

•  Noticing you’re getting defensive when someone questions your approach and, instead of shutting them down, naming it: “I can feel myself wanting to argue this. Let me hear the rest.”

•  In a heated conversation, slowing the pace on purpose, and asking, “What are we worried will happen if we choose this path?” You’re making space for fear instead of letting it steer from the back seat.

•  When you sense the team is numb or checked out, asking how they’re actually doing before you push through the next round of change.

These are acts of leadership. You are taking responsibility for the emotional conditions in the room so the work can get better. You’re making it more likely that the truth reaches you before the budget is blown or the customer leaves.

When you lead, you are always working with two layers at once. There is the visible work: the project plan, the numbers, the org chart, the project plan, the decision. And there is the emotional work: the fear that keeps people quiet, the pride that keeps a failing idea on life support, the loyalty that makes someone work late again, the quiet resentment that slows everything down.

Most leadership models spend a lot of time on the visible work and gesture vaguely at the emotional layer. Our aim is to flip that emphasis. We’ll still talk about decisions and strategies, but we’ll talk about them as emotional events, not just logical ones.

The core idea is simple: Emotions are not background noise to the work; they are the medium through which the work moves. They are the air your leadership breathes. If emotional intelligence is the engine of leadership, friction is the terrain it must traverse. To make that terrain more navigable, we’ll need a clearer map.

Types of Emotional Friction

Leaders tend to encounter friction in three main ways. You’ve already seen pieces of them in the stories we’ve told so far: the executives wrestling with delegation, the brick-and-mortar bookstore trying to bet on slowness in a one-click world.

Internal friction—what’s happening inside you: This is the tension of competing priorities, spiraling thoughts, self-doubt, and the stories you tell yourself about how you’re doing. It’s the tightening in your chest before a difficult conversation, the exhaustion that makes everything feel heavier than it is, the late-night rumination that keeps you half-awake. It’s your narratives, your body, and your private reactions no one else sees.

You use emotional intelligence here to:

•  Notice what you’re feeling and why, instead of running on autopilot.

•  Hold your impulses and reactions rather than acting them out.

•  Adjust your stories about yourself and others so they’re more accurate and less critical.

In other words, internal friction is the arena where self-awareness and self-management do most of their work.

Interpersonal friction—what’s happening between people: This is the conflict that won’t go away, the colleague you avoid, the team that never quite clicks. It’s the awkward silence after a blunt comment, the meeting after the meeting where people say what they really think, the trust that’s been eroded by broken commitments. It’s misaligned expectations, clashing communication styles, and unspoken resentments.

Emotional intelligence helps you:

•  Read the room and pick up on what others are feeling, not just what they’re saying.

•  Name issues directly and respectfully instead of letting them harden.

•  Repair after misunderstandings and rebuild trust when it frays.

Interpersonal friction is where social awareness and relationship management are constantly being tested.

Systemic friction—what’s happening around people: This is the friction built into the system itself: unclear roles, misaligned incentives, inconsistent leadership, policies that slow everything down, or a culture that rewards short-term wins over long-term health. It’s the meeting that has thirty people in it because no one is sure who genuinely has authority. It’s the initiative that dies quietly because another, louder priority shows up. It’s the sense that the system is asking people to do impossible things.

Emotional intelligence does not make systemic friction disappear. It doesn’t magically fix bad incentives or instantly cure chronic under-resourcing. What it can do is help leaders:

•  Recognize and name the emotional impact of the system on people.

•  Hold their own reactions (frustration, cynicism, helplessness) without going numb or exploding.

•  Build coalitions, influence decision-makers, and communicate in ways that make systemic change more likely, not less.

Here, emotional intelligence is less about “coping better” and more about seeing clearly where the system is creating unnecessary drag, naming it without shaming individuals, and using whatever power you have to change the conditions.

Seen this way, the three types of friction ask a new question: Where does my emotional intelligence get tested and why does it matter?

In other words, before we talk about emotional intelligence as a set of internal abilities, we want to talk about the places those abilities are tested: inside people, between people, and around people.

Our argument is straightforward:

1.  Leadership is emotional work.

2.  Emotional work shows up as friction—inside you, between you and others, and in the systems around you.

3.  Emotional intelligence is your ability to work with friction with intentionality.

Emotional Intelligence and Friction

Most emotional intelligence models describe a set of abilities: recognizing your own emotions, managing your reactions, understanding what others are feeling, and navigating relationships wisely. These abilities are often codified into four core skills: self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, and relationship management.

Any way you slice it, we’re talking about your capacity to notice, interpret, and work with emotion in real time.

The challenge is that this language doesn’t always land in everyday leadership conversations. In our research, we found that 74% of people weren’t raised in a house where emotions were openly discussed or expressed. Leaders don’t (usually) wake up saying, “I really need to improve my self-management today.” They wake up worrying about declining sales, a presentation that might go badly, a colleague who’s underperforming, a reorg that’s going to upset people, or a board that’s getting impatient. They wonder how to have a hard conversation without making it worse. They think about how to keep going when they’re exhausted, how to rally people after a setback, or how to stay open when they’d rather shut down. They feel like they’re pushing through molasses—facing the same conflicts again and again or getting worn down by a system that makes good work harder than it needs to be.

In these scenarios, understanding friction becomes useful.

For many leaders, “friction” may be a more intuitive entry point than “emotional regulation.” You don’t have to learn a new vocabulary to feel it. You know what it’s like when a decision that should be straightforward keeps stalling, even if you can’t explain why. You know what it’s like when a conversation that should be simple gets tangled. You know what it’s like when the system itself seems to resist the very outcomes it is intended to produce.

Friction is how most leaders feel the problem before they ever use our language for it.

When we talk about emotional intelligence, we’re talking about your capacity to notice where friction shows up, to interpret what it tells you, and to respond in ways that move the work forward without burning yourself or others out. This is the difference between being dragged by friction and using it as a guide.
