CHAPTER 2 WHEN AND WHERE DO WE ENCOUNTER FRICTION?

The classic legal drama A Few Good Men includes several excellent examples of emotional friction. Perhaps the most instructive scene is the film’s most famous, in which Colonel Jessep (Jack Nicholson) is on the stand and Lieutenant Kaffee (Tom Cruise) questions him with increasing directness and intensity.

Kaffee paces between the prosecutor’s table and the bench, hurling accusations of malfeasance at the Jessep amid the objections of the defense counsel and the judge. Jessep sits there with glowering intensity, while listening to a growing list of bald accusations until he can’t take it anymore.

KAFFEE

Colonel Jessep! Did you order the Code Red?!

JUDGE

You don’t have to answer that question.

JESSEP

I’ll answer the question. You want answers?

KAFFEE

I think I’m entitled to them.

JESSEP

You want answers?!

KAFFEE

I want the truth!

JESSEP

You can’t handle the truth!!

Has this ever happened to you? OK, not being yelled at on the witness stand (we hope) but tension escalating into outright conflict. Feeling poked and prodded until you come out and say exactly what you’re thinking for better or worse. It’s a common enough occurrence.

This scene is high drama, sure, but it’s also a great illustration of emotional friction bursting into the open. At his limit for being questioned in a manner he views as disrespectful and beneath his stature, Jessup fires back that classic line that kicks off his final speech and the climax of the movie.

The other thing that’s interesting about this scene is that it provides a striking illustration of where friction lives—in our minds and our bodies.

When we watch Jessup explode at Kaffee, we witness friction at three levels simultaneously. There’s the friction within Jessup himself—his wounded pride, resentment, rage, the physical energy building in his body. There’s the friction between the two men—the adversarial dynamic, the escalating accusations and defenses. And there’s the friction within the system—the military courtroom with its rules, hierarchies, and competing loyalties all colliding in that moment.

These three levels of friction are always at work, influencing and amplifying each other. To understand friction—and how to work with it effectively—we need to understand how it shows up at each level. Let’s start with what’s happening inside us.

How Emotions Emerge

The common conception of emotion, at least in Western cultures, is that it is made up of discrete feelings that act like occasional visitors in our minds. Love, anger, joy, and fear; they step in and out of our experiences like characters in Pixar’s Inside Out. We find ourselves in a circumstance where a boundary needs to be asserted, and Anger steps in to rev the engine and make our needs known to those around us. This is a fun image, but it is not representative of how our emotions work.

Modern neuroscience provides a more accurate view of where emotion comes from, what is known as the “constructionist” view. While your emotional systems are hard-wired, your body and mind create unique expressions of emotion in each moment. At any given instant, you experience your body as having a certain amount of energy—your “body budget”—and a generally good or bad feeling—positive or negative valence. So, broadly speaking, you can map your embodied experience as being relatively high or low energy, and relatively positive or negative.

For example, if you’re enjoying what you’re reading (fingers-crossed), you’re likely in the relatively low energy / good zone. How would you describe how you’re feeling at this very moment?

As you consider it, your body is providing some input, and your brain is applying some meaning. Mind you, this often happens quite seamlessly and somewhat subconsciously, but we want to make this process more conscious. Your mind is processing clues, evaluating, and providing labels to the feelings and sensations you’re experiencing as you live your life and interact with other people.

Think of your brain like a chef in the kitchen where the “ingredients” are your body’s sensations, environment, and your own psychological history. The “chef” constantly mixes these ingredients into stories—fear, excitement, surprise, curiosity—that guide what you notice, how you act, and what you say. This is why the same experiences and situations don’t always generate the same emotional responses, and why different people may experience different emotions in the same situation. One person may feel excited at the prospect of an afternoon spent reading, while another may feel intense dread.

This is emotion at the most fundamental level. Your brain scans your body and processes—slow heartbeat, general sense of ease—while noticing what’s happening around you—a quiet room, perhaps a low murmur in a coffee shop—and at the same time, it assimilates that information with your memories and lived experience to interpret all these inputs and allow you to arrive (again, usually subconsciously) at some word (or words) that describe(s) how you feel when you’re reading a nonfiction book about emotions.

A feeling of intrigue or curiosity or delight is not triggered in your brain; instead, your brain constructs a concept—let’s say curiosity in this case—based on the physical sensations in your body and the contextual information from your environment and your past. This experience may feel quite different if you were reading, say, a romantasy novel. Or, say you had a history in which the activity of reading had become closely intertwined with feelings of pressure and anxiety; in that case, you may have quite a different emotional encounter with this book.

Why This Matters More Than You Think

This may be starting to sound technical and a bit too “under the hood,” but understanding some of the basics of the neuroscience of emotion is useful for two reasons:

1.  You’re never emotionally neutral, not exactly, which means that in any given situation you can tune into your emotional-friction level and gain some insight into how you’re feeling and how your feelings may be shaping your thoughts; and

2.  Emotions direct the energy in your body. Emotions are impulses to act, which means that your emotions are not only coding and informing your thoughts, but they’re also driving your behavior. Understanding how that process works can give you the leverage needed to create change.

Friction Is Individual

In 1974, two psychologists, Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman, published a paper that would change how we understand decision-making. Their research showed that humans are not the rational decision-makers economists once imagined. We make all sorts of irrational choices as we go about our days. For example, we take bigger risks to avoid losses than to pursue gains; we assume things are more common if we can recall examples of that thing more quickly; we seek out information that confirms our views rather than challenges them; we consider our own behavior to be shaped by our environment and circumstances, and others to be shaped by their personal choices and actions. One of their classic studies, “the Linda Problem,” demonstrates this reality vividly.

In the study, participants read about a fictional character:

“Linda is 31 years old, single, outspoken and very bright. She majored in philosophy. As a student, she was deeply concerned with issues of discrimination and social justice, and also participated in anti-nuclear demonstrations.”

After reading that brief bio, they are then asked which description is more likely to be true:

1.  that Linda is a bank teller

2.  that Linda is a bank teller and is active in the feminist movement

What do you think? Which is more likely to be true of Linda?

Most people chose the second option, even though simple probability makes the first option more likely. There are simply more bank tellers than there are bank tellers who are active in the feminist movement. And yet people picked the second choice.

Studies like this highlight flaws in our reasoning. Probability can help us more accurately understand the world around us, and yet we tend not to consider probabilities when we make choices. Evaluations of Linda are shaped not only by our judgements (both rational and otherwise) but also by our social and emotional associations.

Our snap judgments create friction when they clash with reality. We have biases and stereotypes that shade how we interpret what goes on around us. Our minds work like this because our brains are simplification machines; it costs less energy to match our experiences and observations to preexisting patterns in our minds than it does to process each new experience on its own. That means we make errors in our judgments. We misinterpret. We overgeneralize. We move too quickly. We naturally operate on intuition—what Kahneman later called “thinking fast”—because it allows us to make instantaneous judgments and navigate the world with minimal effort. And so, we read that Linda is concerned about issues of discrimination and social justice and assume someone like that is likely active in the feminist movement.

The Hidden Upside of Irrational Feeling

But this is not a bad thing! Inevitably when we talk about these features of how our minds work, the next question is usually something like, “How do you combat these problems?” Yes, we want to reduce our biases and our errors in judgment; but by only aiming to short-circuit the brains’ fast, instinctive, and emotional-thinking system, we fail to recognize how prevalent emotions are in our cognition, and how useful they are.

We all have a constant emotional undercurrent whether we’re aware of it or not. Sometimes our emotional reactions to circumstances are easiest to identify—the surge of anger, the slog of some sudden sadness—but emotion is there even when it’s less prevalent, less readily nameable.

Emotions shape how we pay attention and how we decide; they shape our creative thinking, our health, and our relationships.

We tend to want to avoid negative emotions like fear, anger, anxiety, and hopelessness. Rightly so. But in only seeking to avoid or mitigate emotions like these, we fail to see the value they can bring. Marc Brackett, in his bestseller Permission to Feel, observes that negative emotions are constructive because they narrow attention and prepare us for action.

Ross: Not long ago, I was out climbing with my daughters and experienced this effect in acute detail.

We were scrambling and bouldering (climbing short walls without ropes) in a local boulder field in central Alabama. When we were making our way back towards the car, my older daughter started a climb that was too tall; a crack climb that started at a 45-degree angle for about 5 to 10 feet and then went straight up about another 10 or so feet. I can’t really remember how tall it was; but, suffice to say, it was tall and felt a lot taller. She climbed up a few times to where the angle changed and then would down climb to where I could help her off the rock.

Two times, three times, maybe four times she tried, testing new footholds, working her way up to where the crack went vertical, and then came back down. She paused for a bit, but said she wanted to give it one more try. I was convinced she didn’t have the strength left to advance past the point she’d already reached several times, so I let her give it a go. But when she reached a bit farther into the crack, found a foothold, and pressed her way higher than she’d gone before, I realized immediately I’d made a mistake. She was too high for me to comfortably spot her and likely too high to be able to climb back down. My heart rate spiked, my palms began to sweat, my vision narrowed; the only thing I could see were my daughter’s hands on sandstone. I thought, I prayed: She can make it to the top. But I was on high alert, waiting for one finger to slip.

That’s when she yelled that she was going to fall.

The flash of her jacket in the air, and then somehow I’ve caught her and am holding her under her arms, and she’s upright and is sitting on the ground, and we’re both crying, and I’m checking her for injuries only to find a minor scratch on her stomach.

Since this happened, I’ve told the story time after time, trying to process it, trying to mark out in my own mind the multitude of minor mistakes I made that led to a big, bad circumstance. But I’ve also been trying to process the emotions of the whole experience—excitement, apprehension, confidence, surprise, and fear. What I remember most is the 100-proof terror coursing through my veins when she got too high, which kept my eyes glued to her every move, waiting for a slip. When I consider that I caught her, I don’t consider it an ability that allowed me to do that, but a surge of emotion and adrenaline that allowed my body to react in that moment; that, and a heap of capital “G” Grace. It wasn’t what I was thinking in that moment that helped me perform, it was what I was feeling.

Take a more extreme example of a similar effect. In a New York Times interview, professional ultramarathoner and mountaineer Kilian Jornet described a traumatic expedition in which his mentor and friend, Stéphane Brosse, died when a snow cornice collapsed under him. Jornet considered the event as a pivotal moment that forced him to confront the devastating reality of risk and survivor’s guilt, leading to a period where he struggled with his mental health and his relationship with the mountains.

He describes the episode from an emotional standpoint:

We were almost finished and we were happy, just at the summit, enjoying. There were some birds flying around, and I remember we were smiling and laughing about where we were and how fun it was. And we were walking on the ridge and we didn’t notice that we were walking on a cornice.

We can all relate to that feeling of accomplishment and the positive rush of emotions that comes with it. And yet, when we consider affect as a source of important information, we can understand what feelings of happiness may have done to Jornet and Brosse’s attention in that moment. Feelings like happiness and enjoyment can signal to our brains that our environment is safe, which can cause our more analytical “threat detection” systems to relax. At the same time, positive emotions can also have the effect of shifting our focus toward the “bigger picture”—like the summit views, the birds flying around—which may have prevented Jornet and Brosse from noticing the details of the dangerous snow cornice beneath their feet.

It should be no surprise then that our emotions work similarly in our professional lives, where our affect shapes what we’re capable of and where we often mistakenly treat leadership and teamwork as purely logistical exercises. We act as if a team is just a collection of thinkers gathered to accomplish tasks. The fact that team members have feelings is ancillary to the almighty to-do list. But because we are feeling, social animals, a team is more like a biological ecosystem than a collection of individuals. Teamwork, and thereby leadership, operates as much on emotional infrastructure as on intellectual insight.

Unfortunately, most organizations traffic in a severely limited emotional range: professionalism, motivation, grit. You may encounter some occasional empathy or excitement if you’re lucky. The typical array of emotions on display at work would make the stoics proud.

But the real emotional landscape of the workplace is much more complex and much more intense. It is not as if we get in our cars to commute, walk into the office, and turn on our computers and the emotions just fall away. We continue feeling feelings both small and big and ride those waves throughout the day. Your mood and emotional state can play a large role in how and why you’re motivated, how and why you’re performing exceptionally well, or how and why you’re unable to get going and deliver the goods. Your emotional state can impact your ability to focus, your ability to interact well with others, and your ability to align with others around a common goal.

We don’t abandon our feeling selves when we clock in; our feelings are deeply embedded in our connections and experiences with others. This means our emotions don’t just exist inside us as private experiences; they serve as a shared language and social glue. They are the signals we send and receive to navigate our lives with others, making what’s personal inevitably communal.

We may not always be rational, but we’re always relational.

Friction Is Social

Our brains have mechanisms that allow emotional synchronization to happen naturally among individuals. “Mirror neurons” are specific brain cells that light up both when you do something (say, when you’re smiling) and when you watch someone else do the same thing. You know this experience. This is why yawning is contagious, why watching someone else laugh hysterically will probably make you laugh, why you might wince when someone stubs their toe, or why you might experience a sense of shared delight at a concert of your favorite band. Our brains are designed to take information from other people and have our experience shaped by it.

It happens in the other direction too. Have you been to a funeral in recent memory? Shared grief is an incredibly powerful emotion, and a profound example of the way that our emotions can serve as shared language. We don’t experience our emotions alone; we experience them in tandem, in relation, and in opposition to others. And this has enormous implications for leadership and teamwork. If you’ve ever walked into a meeting and instantly “felt the tension in the room,” you’ve experienced this in action. You didn’t calculate it rationally; you sensed it emotionally.

With one leadership team we’ve worked with, we’ve seen the operational impact of shared emotional experiences and friction that have gone unmanaged. Three equal partners have struggled to find ways to communicate and make decisions that didn’t lead to direct conflict. One partner views conflict as good and as a way of generating nuanced insight and new ideas; the second partner views it as useful, but frequently experiences it as an indictment of his own authority; and the third partner has this implicit assumption that all conflict is negative. So, when these leadership team meetings start to heat up, one partner leans in, one becomes defensive, and the other shuts down. Three different types of internal emotional friction work together to create a different type of group friction.

Observing the team in conversation, though, it’s interesting to see that they aren’t exactly locked in disagreement about what to do or how to do it. Nobody feels heard, so the fact that they are aligned on the outcomes becomes secondary to the desire to feel heard.

Why “Take the Emotion Out of It” Doesn’t Work

In one case, we spent time working with a team to clarify how each executive would define “effective collaboration.” We asked each to describe it individually, then report to the group. We heard strikingly similar responses from each, like: respect, transparent and direct communication, trust and reliability, support for one another. In the process, one executive offered that they should try to communicate, “without emotion.”

This is not an uncommon desire. “Can we take the emotion out of this conversation?” is among the many ways we talk about the fact that too much emotional friction grinds communication and productivity to a halt. But it is perhaps more useful to acknowledge and understand that it is no less possible to “take the emotion out” of our interactions than it is to talk without being conscious.

Hear this: “no emotion” is not the goal; “no emotion” is not a possibility. Viable goals include identifying the emotions that are present, understanding where they’re coming from and what kind of action they may be facilitating, managing individual emotions, and making changes that can reduce collective friction. The challenge isn’t that leaders have emotions. The problem is that emotions are always present, whether or not we acknowledge them. We can’t “turn them off” when we walk into a boardroom or log in to a Zoom meeting.

Consider the leadership team described above. They weren’t just experiencing individual frustration or interpersonal friction; they were caught in an emotional pattern that had become embedded in how they worked together. This pattern shaped every meeting, every decision, and every interaction. When emotional patterns become this consistent, they stop being just about individuals or relationships; they become part of the system itself.

In other words, they become culture.

Friction Is Structural

Think about how this might play out in a typical startup story. Let’s call this imaginary company Vertex, a small software firm founded by three people working out of a cramped office in Austin, TX. Vertex is six months old and just landed its first major client—a contract that could truly launch the company. The team is ecstatic. They go out for drinks. They share in the excitement, the relief, and the possibility.

Then, a few weeks later, disaster strikes. A bug in the software causes a data loss for the client. The client is furious and the founders are terrified.

What happens next will shape Vertex’s culture for years to come.

The three founders gather in their office on a Saturday morning. One suggests they call the client, explain what happened, and let them know they’re working to solve the problem and recover the data. Another argues they need to fix the bug first, then call the client. The third is just adamant that they need to figure out how this happened, so it never happens again. The friction in the group rises as the volume goes up.

But then something shifts. One founder says, “Look, I’m terrified, but this is what we wanted. We wanted clients like this, and we wanted to be able to help businesses like this. We built this thing together, and we can fix it together.” The friction is still there, but it’s now informed by a sense of direction, determination, and the solidarity that they can solve the problem together.

They work through the weekend. All three of them, plus two friends they bring in to help (who later become part of the growing team). They fix the bug, they recover the lost data, they document exactly what went wrong and how they’ve addressed it in a sustainable and scalable way, and they meet with the client first thing on Monday morning with both a solution and a plan to prevent it from happening again. The client is impressed, and the startup is off to the races.

This is an emotional event that creates a cultural pattern. The organizational psychologist Edgar Schein famously defined culture as patterns of shared behavior that are learned by a group as it solved collective problems and showed how the group then taught proven patterns to new group members. He described these patterns as shared basic assumptions, but they could also be described as patterns of shared emotional responses to certain types of problems. These responses become so automatic they tend to work below the level of conscious awareness, which is why culture is typically described as just “the way things are done around here.” You might say, the way things feel around here. Does it feel formal or informal, laid back or energetic, free-flowing or deliberate?

At Vertex, the pattern isn’t written down anywhere. But as the company grows, everyone knows it intuitively. When a new crisis emerges, everyone shifts into heroic effort mode. People cancel personal plans, weekend work becomes expected, and those who stay late and deliver are the ones who get promoted. This pattern gets reinforced because it has helped the group succeed in the past, so the implicit assumption is that it will help the group succeed in the future. And stepping outside of the pattern—leaving at 5 P.M. during a crisis or suggesting a more measured approach when everyone else is revving up—risks contravening a cultural norm and being seen as “not one of us.”

How Leaders Set the Emotional Tone (Whether They Mean To or Not)

Here’s the most important part to consider: Emotions are contagious, yes, but they’re not equally contagious in all directions, especially inside organizations.

When someone in a leadership position expresses an emotion, it doesn’t tend to stay put. Emotional reactions flow outward from positions of authority and influence. And consistent ways of responding to circumstances is how emotional patterns become cultural assumptions.

If a leader responds to criticism by talking louder, arguing, and becoming visibly agitated, team members will learn that they can’t push back without creating a battle, so they’ll stay quiet when they should be speaking up.

If a leader is open to hearing all kinds of feedback, can process bad news without becoming visibly angry, and can express gratitude to those who bring up uncomfortable subjects, team members will learn they can be open and forthright and will bring forward the most challenging topics.

If a leader only celebrates individual heroics and never collective effort or sustainability, the team learns that burning out is the price of being valued.

Ross: I’ve watched this play out with a CEO who prided himself on his “passion” and “energy.” In practice, this meant he brought a lot of intensity to his role. When he was worked up about something, his brow would knit, he’d pace, he’d run his hands through his hair, he’d toss off comments like “this is unacceptable,” and “we gotta get this fixed asap.”

What was interesting is that he thought he was modeling urgency, but what other executives saw was anxiety. Because of this behavior, his team had developed a pattern: They only brought him solutions. They held issues back until they were 90% solved because they didn’t want to get sucked into one of his displays of passion.

The effect of this meant that he was often the last person to know about major challenges, which made him more anxious when he finally found out … which reinforced the team’s tendency to hide problems longer.

He wasn’t a bad leader. He cared deeply about the company and the people. But his emotional responses had created a systemic dysfunction that no amount of “open door policy” rhetoric could solve. The team heard his comments about trust and transparency, but they responded to the friction they felt in the system.

From Constraint to Capability

If unexamined emotional patterns limit what teams can do, what opens up when we bring those patterns into awareness? What becomes possible when we learn to work with emotions intentionally?

This is where understanding our emotions can shift from describing challenges to helping us navigate them. It starts with seeing emotions not as distractions from the work, not as problems to be managed or eliminated, but as information about how the systems around us, our relationships, and our own thinking are functioning at the emotional level. It requires seeing emotions not as constraints but as enablers, as essential ingredients in how we decide, how we learn, and how we perform at our best.

Friction Is Fruitful

Emotions aren’t obstacles to thinking clearly and making good decisions. They’re essential to both.

As renowned neuroscientist Antonio Damasio discovered through his work with patients who had lost the ability to feel emotions, “We are not thinking machines that feel; we are feeling machines that think.” His patients could reason logically, but they couldn’t even make simple decisions like what to eat for lunch or when to schedule an appointment. Without emotions to shape their understanding, they were paralyzed by options.

Emotions aren’t obstacles to thinking clearly and making good decisions. They’re essential to both.

Emotions are how we interact, how we communicate, and how we decide what matters. They shape the narratives we live by and how we make sense of the world. They connect us to other people and help us read the room. They help us understand what deserves our attention, what requires action, and what can wait. They give us the energy to persist or the wisdom to step back.

The question, then, is how do we develop greater awareness of our emotions as we’re experiencing them—both individually and collectively—so we can work with them rather, than being driven by them unconsciously?

The Case for a Richer Emotional Vocabulary

It’s estimated that there are about 2,000 emotion words in the English language. It is also estimated that approximately 50% of those words describe negative emotions, 30% describe positive emotions, and 20% describe neutral emotional states. This is potentially useful for several reasons.

First, how many emotion words do you think you could list—positive, negative, or neutral? There is a common understanding that a core element of emotional intelligence relates simply to our ability to label our emotions. So, if you have fewer emotion words, in effect you have fewer emotions. Now this isn’t strictly true, you have the emotional experiences you have, but because you can’t distinguish between the nuances of your feelings, the range of your emotional understanding is limited.

This is like having different words for different shades of blue. A fine artist will likely have a greater reserve to draw from. Or consider the apocryphal fact that the Inuit people have hundreds of words for snow. While in English we may distinguish regularly between snow and ice, in Inuit there are specific words that distinguish between snow in the air, snow on the ground, soft deep snow, fine frosty snow, and wet slushy snow. Because they have more words to describe the different traits and states of snow, they are better able to communicate with nuance about winter conditions.

The same is true of emotion. If, instead of describing yourself as feeling gloomy (or blue, as it were), you were able to describe feeling crestfallen, that communicates an important difference in emotional experience beyond just “sad.” This is why emotion researchers view having an emotional lexicon as the foundation of emotional intelligence.

Second, isn’t it interesting that we seem to have more words for negative emotions? Perhaps it suggests that we’re more likely to encounter negative feelings. Or that negative feelings are more recognizable as distinct emotions. After all, how frequently do you pause to consider how happy you are when you’re experiencing a moment of frivolity? On the other hand, it’s quite common for a feeling of sadness to point itself out. And sadness that’s aware of itself tends to reinforce itself. Or perhaps this language disparity suggests that the real work of our emotional life is learning how to manage the negative feelings we experience in productive (or non-destructive) ways. As we’ve said, it’s much easier to act in emotionally intelligent ways when things are going well. It’s much harder when things feel like they’ve gone sideways or flipped upside down.

Finally, think about your own workday. You probably move through various emotional states all day long. Sometimes the friction feels internal, like it’s coming from your own doubts or how you see yourself in relation to a given task or project. Other times it feels external, like it’s coming from the people around you or the environment you’re working in. Often it’s both at once.

A richer emotional vocabulary helps you tell the difference. When you can name what you’re actually feeling—not just “stressed” but apprehensive or overwhelmed or resentful—you start to see patterns. You notice what kind of friction you’re in, where it’s coming from, and what it might be asking of you. That distinction matters, because the response to internal friction is different from the response to external friction.

This is what determines whether friction becomes productive or destructive. Emotions are information. The more precisely you can read them, the better equipped you are to work with them rather than be flattened by them.
