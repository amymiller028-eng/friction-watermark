INTRODUCTION

This is a book about the intelligence that is embedded in our everyday experience.

Not intelligence in the abstract—not IQ or pedigree or credentials, not the kind that gets you into a top school, or perhaps gets you hired. Wea mean the quieter, more demanding type of intelligence: the ability to notice what you’re feeling, understand why you’re feeling it, and use that information rather than be used by it.

This book is about the insight and information embedded in the challenging circumstances we encounter in life; in tension, in conflict. It is about the ways our teams and organizations really work (or don’t). It is about friction, the awareness we all have when things are going better or worse, when they seem to be easier or harder.

We are not suggesting that hard things are actually good if you really think about it. This is less the obstacle is the path, more the obstacle has a signal. We’re keen on the idea that everything we do in life contains emotional data that can help us better understand ourselves, make decisions, connect with others, and get things done.

This book may not solve all your problems, but it may change the way you think about your life. Who We Are

We didn’t set out to write a book about emotional intelligence together. In fact, we haven’t always seen eye-to-eye on the subject.

We are both consulting psychologists by training (think of the overlap in the Venn diagram between organizational and counseling psychology), but we came to this work from very different worlds.

Ross: I arrived in psychology from insurance and finance, while Maggie came straight from academia, with a brief detour through non-profit work. We met in graduate school—in fact, Maggie interviewed me as part of my application process to get into the same graduate program.

Maggie: And we’ve been friends, colleagues, and occasional antagonists ever since—the kind of collaborators who trust each other enough to say the thing the other person may not want to hear. This dynamic showed up early. I remember acting as a sort of graduate school guide for Ross when he was first getting started and hearing about all the research he thought he wanted to do. But he also talked about how he was maybe more wired like a practitioner and didn’t want to waste away working on his dissertation. What he really wanted to do was graduate and get busy doing the work of coaching and consulting.

Ross: Maggie’s expert guidance about our faculty and the structure of our program helped me learn the trade-offs I would have to make to get my degree more quickly. Because of her insight, I graduated without much delay and got to work using all that I had learned in an applied setting. Then, a few years after graduating when I was developing my first book project with the American Psychological Association about executive assessment,b I had gotten feedback from blind reviewers that emotional intelligence (EQ) was missing. Maggie had told me as much.

Maggie: Emotional intelligence was nowhere to be seen. It was such a useful book except that he was totally missing the EQ lens on CEO and senior leader assessment.

Ross: In fact, I didn’t think I was missing it. I wasn’t really onboard with the whole EQ thing at the time. I wasn’t enamored by the research (especially in the context of senior leaders), felt like the term had been over-used, and generally wasn’t convinced that it belonged in my book. But … I also wanted my proposal to get through the review process. So, in my reply to my reviewers I said I would include EQ, and when the book came out, here’s what I wrote:

There is some debate as to whether emotional intelligence is an ability that describes one’s capacity to understand emotion and use that understanding to improve thought (Mayer et al., 2008), or whether it is a combination of traits and skills that shape an individual’s ability to cope with the emotional demands of various situations (Bar-On, 1997).

Because of this lack of consensus, it is not always clear whether emotional intelligence is a distinct form of intelligence, or whether it is “an umbrella term for a broad array of constructs that are connected only by their non-redundancy with cognitive intelligence” (Joseph & Newman, 2010, p. 55).

Maggie: He wasn’t wrong, exactly. He just wasn’t right yet. I had started my career at the Center for Creative Leadership training leadership development programs for leaders of all different levels and what I saw in those 10+ years (which CCL’s derailment research backs up) was that people derail less frequently because of technical skill gaps, and more frequently because they just can’t adapt to change, build and lead teams, or they have problems with interpersonal relationships. Basically: People get hired for IQ and get demoted or fired for EQ.

Ross: Meanwhile, I spent the past decade doing executive assessments, succession planning, and leadership coaching in private equity and large corporate settings. But it wasn’t until the past five years or so, as my work shifted more heavily into executive coaching, that I started to see how impactful emotional intelligence is to the lived experience of a person trying to lead in an organization—and to all the people around them. Technical skill and strategic acumen only told part of the story. The real work happened in the quiet moments before a hard conversation, in the spike of defensiveness during a board meeting, in the heaviness someone carried home after a long day of trying to hold it all together. Leading started to seem less like a set of competencies to master and more like an emotional experience to navigate. And those who learned to work with that reality, rather than deny it, were the ones who grew in notable and sustainable ways.

Maggie: Then in 2021, I joined TalentSmartEQ. As I began working with clients, I started thinking about this idea that emotional intelligence (a wonderful and necessary set of skills) is easiest to do when things are going well. When your business is doing well, when you are connecting with your partner, when your parents are healthy, when your 2-year-old is sleeping. The challenge is that EQ seems to fail us right about the time we need it the most. When policy changes around tariffs blow your quarterly forecasts out of the water, when you have a peer openly criticizing you in all-hands meetings, when someone cuts you off in traffic, when you get a call that your 77-year-old mother has fallen and fractured her ribs before you get on stage for a keynote in front of 250 very skeptical construction leaders. What do you do then?

Ross: As our thinking about emotions and leadership began to converge, we started talking about a project. Maybe there was a way to bring this message to more people. Maybe we could survey a representative sample of working American adults and learn more about how we are all experiencing these dynamics at and outside of work today. That’s what this book is about …

At our core, we’re researchers, but we’re the type of researchers who don’t believe knowledge should stay in academic journals. We know the uncomfortable truth about great research: If it stays trapped in academic language, it doesn’t change anyone’s life. Translation is the work. Storytelling is the bridge. Practical application is how insight becomes behavior.

We’re not writing from a distance. We’ve spent years working with leaders in the real world, testing ideas, pressure-testing language, watching what lands and what doesn’t. The concepts in this book have been shaped in conversations with first-time managers crying at 4 A.M. sitting on meditation pillows wondering, Do I really want this job?, CEOs still getting pulled into conflict among direct reports about fairness in job titles when they’re preparing for earnings calls, and elite senior executives managing panic attacks at work. They have been tempered in the moments when theory meets time pressure, conflict, and ambiguity, when leaders must quietly carry their emotions while continuing to function.

We also wrote this book while living it ourselves.

Halfway through, we hit our own friction. Not the productive kind that sharpens ideas, the kind that exposes limits: internal resistance, resilience fatigue, competing responsibilities, the messiness of trying to build something meaningful while still doing everything else life requires. In other words: the exact terrain we’re inviting you to walk with us.

And then, at the two-yard line, life did what life does. Maggie’s mom, Lynne, passed away after years of battling Parkinson’s and dementia. Days later—not weeks, days—Ross and Din welcomed their third baby. Grief and joy, arriving almost simultaneously, neither waiting for the other to finish.

As Glennon Doyle, author of Untamed, reminds us, you can do hard things. We believe that. We believe it because we had to. In the friction-ful final push to finish this manuscript, we wrestled with so many of the very things you will read about in these pages. We used the strategies we are asking you to experiment with, not as researchers observing from a comfortable distance, but as people who needed them to work.

If you’re reading this because you feel friction, inside your own head, in your relationships, or in the systems you’re operating within, we’re not here to pretend we’re above it. We’re here because we’ve studied it, we’ve tested it, we’ve argued with it, and we’ve learned (sometimes the hard way) that friction isn’t just something to eliminate.

We fundamentally believe friction is something to understand and work with.

Who This Book Is For

This book is for the newly promoted high performer who still thinks, If I just work harder, I can out-run the messier parts. You’re good at solving problems, hitting targets, being the person people trust when something’s on fire. Then (maybe, suddenly) you’re the person others watch when something’s on fire. Your calendar fills with meetings you didn’t ask for. Your work becomes less about what you can do and more about what you can get done through other people.

You might be surprised about the emotional volume of the role (that definitely wasn’t in the job description), you give a piece of feedback you practiced three times in your head on the drive to work and the other person doesn’t hear it the way you intended and they shut down. You try to “stay neutral” in a tense meeting and realize your face has turned visibly red. You walk into a conversation intending to be calm and walk out thinking, Why did I say it like that? You’re still competent (maybe more competent than ever), but your job now includes an invisible layer: managing the human system, including your own.

This book is for you.

It’s for seasoned managers, leaders of teams, functions, transformations, clients, classrooms, volunteer committees, shift crews, hospital units, sales territories, product pods, and project plans. It’s for anyone who’s thinking: Where is the user’s manual for this mess?

We wrote this for the leaders who are tired of advice that assumes you’re either a robot or a saint.

You know the genre:

•  “Just delegate.”

•  “Set boundaries.”

•  “Give feedback.”

•  “Be authentic.”

•  “Build trust.”

Yes. And also … your direct report is spiraling, your boss is vague, two high performers are quietly (or maybe not quietly) in a cold war, the reorg is eating your best people, and you’re holding it together in a way that looks calm on the outside but feels expensive on the inside.

This book is for senior leaders, too. The ones who have enough experience to know that most leadership problems aren’t technical but emotional, relational, and political. They’re about fear, status, belonging, uncertainty, and the unspoken story everyone is protecting. Senior leaders don’t need more generic inspiration. They need sharper diagnosis, better language, and practices that help them stay steady when things get weird.

And we wrote it for the leaders without the title:

•  The parent who becomes the de facto manager of the kiddo’s soccer team who is somehow running a tiny organization with snacks, schedules, parents, and a child who has decided that today is the day to find their “leadership voice” by refusing to play defense

•  The member of a faith community trying to build unity while people disagree about everything from budget decisions to what “community” is supposed to mean

•  The PTA volunteer who showed up to plan a fundraiser and ended up mediating two adults arguing about the tone of an email

If you’ve ever found yourself thinking, I can’t not lead here, this book is for you.

And we wrote this for the people who support leaders day to day, the quiet force multipliers:

•  The HR partners who get pulled into everything once it’s already a problem

•  The L&D leaders who are expected to “fix culture” with a 60-minute workshop

•  The facilitators who can feel the room shift but need language that helps the group name what’s happening without making it worse

•  The chiefs of staff, project leads, operations managers, and assistants who often carry more emotional labor than anyone realizes

•  The executive coaches who help others navigate these difficult dynamics on a daily basis

If you help leaders do the job, you’ve probably had this experience: You can sense the friction in a situation immediately, but you don’t always have a way to name it. Or you have language, but it sounds too clinical in real life. Or you have tools, but they’re too complicated to use when someone is defensive, or embarrassed, or simply over it.

We wrote this book so you’d have words that work in real conversations.

Not “perfect words.” Useful words. The kind that help you lower the temperature without pretending nothing is wrong. The kind that help you tell the truth without turning it into a performance. The kind that help you lead without needing to be bulletproof. What we’ve learned from our own lives and from the leaders and teams we’ve worked with is that friction isn’t a sign that you’re failing; friction is information.

It’s the signal that something important is happening, inside you, between people, or inside a system. And you can either treat that signal like an interruption, or you can treat it like a dashboard light. Something to pay attention to before the engine overheats. We can promise you won’t always get it right. But if you’re ready to stop white-knuckling your way through the emotional reality of your job and start leading with more clarity, more choice, and a little more peace, you’ve come to the right place.

What’s in the Book

This book moves in multiple directions at once: inwardly and outwardly, diagnostically and practically. We start with friction itself—what it is, where it comes from, and why our collective obsession with eliminating it has cost us something we didn’t mean to give up.

In Part I, we dig into friction. We look at why our push toward frictionless work has come at a cost, why leading is unavoidably emotional, and how to recognize and work with the friction that’s already shaping your experience as a leader.

Chapter 1: We explore how our cultural obsession with frictionlessness—from AI to one-click shopping—has led us to eliminate the very struggles that make leadership meaningful, and why emotional friction, unlike logistical friction, is often the energy source that allows real growth and change to happen.

Chapter 2: We examine how emotional friction manifests at three interconnected levels—within ourselves (how our brains construct emotions from bodily sensations and context), between people (through emotional contagion and relational dynamics), and systemically (as cultural patterns that shape how organizations function)—and why learning to distinguish productive from destructive friction is essential to effective leadership.

Chapter 3: We introduce the “Friction Field,” a framework that maps friction by intensity (low to high) and source (internal versus external) and explore how research on flow states, growth mindset, cognitive offloading, and desirable difficulties reveals that the right kind of friction, strategically embraced, is essential for learning, growth, and peak performance.

Chapter 4: We preview insights from the National Emotions Survey we conducted as part of our research into friction dynamics in organizations. We explain why emotion has been cast aside in our workplaces, how friction shows up at work, and what emotional intelligence can help us do about it.

In Part 2, we get practical. Each chapter focuses on specific experiments you can use to build the four core skills of emotional intelligence while you are in the thick of friction. The tools are organized by type of friction so you can find what you need: tools for when you’re stuck in your own head, when you’re tangled up with other people, and when the system itself is working against you.

Together, these parts are meant to give you both a map and a set of moves: a way to see your leadership challenges through the lens of friction, and ways to respond that strengthen your capacity to lead.

Our hope is that this book gives you language for what you feel, curiosity about what others are carrying, and the confidence to engage your emotion rather than avoid it. Even when feelings are sharp, heavy, or unfamiliar, they are not obstacles to leadership; they are raw material.

If we can make the emotional forces shaping your work more visible and more workable—more connected to the decisions you make every day—then friction won’t be something you merely endure. It will become one of the primary ways you learn, lead, and grow.
