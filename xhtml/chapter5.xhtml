CHAPTER 5 INTERNAL FRICTION EXPERIMENTS

The experiments in this section start closest to home. Internal friction is the friction that lives inside you: the stories you tell yourself before hard conversations, the identity you have wrapped around your role, the emotional forecasts that shape your behavior before anyone else has said a word to you. You cannot manage what you haven’t noticed, and it can be hard to notice what you cannot name.

These experiments are designed to help you do both. Some are quick interventions you can use today, some are practices worth building over weeks, and some are harder structural changes to how you think about yourself and operate as a leader.

All of them start with you.

You don’t need to start at the beginning. If your head is already loud and you need something you can use in the next ten minutes, Breathe Before the Story or Management Begins in Your Feet are your fastest entry points. If you’re in a stretch where the role itself feels heavier than usual—where the identity pressure is the thing, not any single conversation—You Are Not Your Job and Build the Pool are where this section earns its keep. If you’re functioning fine but running on empty in a way that’s starting to show, start near the end: Make Rest a Leadership Practice and Strategic Art of Bright & Dim are built for that. The fifteen experiments below move from quick interventions toward longer structural changes in how you lead yourself, but the right place to begin is wherever the friction inside you is loudest right now.

Borrow Your Brain Back

Colleen Kinder describes something that should be impossible in 2025: Seven Yale students willingly handed over their phones for four weeks in rural France. No bargaining. No “but I need it for music.” No anxious negotiations about GPS, photos, or “just in case.” They didn’t just comply, they wanted it.

That detail matters, because we’ve designed learning, work, and daily life to require constant connectivity, and then we act surprised when attention collapses under the weight of it. Kinder’s students had a word for their compulsive scrolling: rotting. They knew they were doing it. Knowing didn’t stop it.

So here’s the inner-friction experiment: Question your use of technology. For minds to do what minds are meant to do—think, integrate, imagine, remember, create—you need to reduce the noise and add back the right kind of friction.

The Hidden Tax of “Always Available”

We tend to treat distraction like a moral issue: I should be more disciplined. But a lot of what we experience as “my brain isn’t working” is simply cognitive overload. Research suggests that even the mere presence of your smartphone can reduce available cognitive capacity.

Then there’s task-switching. When you hop from drafting an email to glancing at a notification to returning to the email, you don’t re-enter the work cleanly. Some attention stays stuck to the previous task. Sophie Leroy calls this attention residue. You can feel it: You’re “back” at work, but part of you is still in the other tab.

And over time, our habits shape our cognition. Chronic media multitasking has been associated with weaker performance on certain forms of cognitive control and filtering irrelevant information. You don’t just lose minutes; you can lose the actual texture of sustained thought.

This is the inside-the-mind version of friction: the sensation of trying to think while being lightly interrupted every 30 seconds. Not enough to call it chaos, just enough to make depth feel unreachable. You start to rely on shortcuts. You skim instead of read. You search instead of remember.

Which brings us to a brutal trade: Convenience can quietly become cognitive offloading. When people expect they’ll have future access to information online, they’re less likely to remember the information itself and more likely to remember where to find it. Useful, yes. But if everything becomes outsourced, you can end up living with a mind that’s always “on call” and rarely “at home.”

Why Friction Helps Learning

In Kinder’s France experiment, the students didn’t become brilliant because they discovered a magical productivity hack. They got better because the environment did something modern life almost never does: It made distraction inconvenient. That’s friction.

Friction has a bad reputation, but as discussed in Part 1, certain kinds of effort and constraint make learning stick better over the long run, even if it feels harder in the moment.

This is why writing by hand can outperform typing for conceptual learning: It forces summarizing, selecting, and processing, rather than transcribing. It’s why print reading often shows comprehension advantages over digital reading in meta-analytic work (with nuance across age, context, and text type). It’s why “I struggled through it” can be the very mechanism that produces mastery.

Kinder even describes a student who dimmed his laptop screen until it was basically black so the glow itself wouldn’t pull him out of his writing flow. That may sound extreme, but it’s a young writer discovering an old truth: If you want depth, you must protect conditions for depth.

You Need Access to Your Mind

Here’s the part we underappreciate: A good mind is built in effort, but it’s also built in stillness.

Neuroscience has long described a “default mode” network—systems that become active when we’re not externally focused on a task, often associated with internal reflection, autobiographical memory, future simulation, and meaning-making. Creativity research also points to cooperation between imaginative, internally oriented networks and control networks, suggesting that creative thinking is akin to structured wandering.

But constant tech noise can starve that capacity. If every micro-pause becomes a scroll, in the elevator, on the sidewalk, waiting in line, your brain loses the unclaimed spaces where synthesis happens.

And there’s a physiological layer too. Evening exposure to light-emitting devices can suppress melatonin and shift circadian timing, degrading sleep and next-day alertness. When Kinder’s students reported deeper sleep, that wasn’t just a pleasant side effect. It was cognitive infrastructure coming back online.

The Leader’s Uncomfortable Edge: Being Alone

Most leaders avoid solitude because it removes the shields.

When you’re alone, no pings, no people, no “quick fire” decisions; you’re left with the only thing you can’t delegate: your inner experience. That’s where self-awareness actually lives. And self-awareness is a practice that requires both privilege (space) and work (honesty).a There’s a reason so many high performers treat reflection like a “nice-to-have” that gets squeezed out by the urgent. The calendar fills, the line of problems forms at the door, and being needed starts to feel like being in control.

A leader may not choose solitude because:

•  Busy feels safer than still. Busy gives you instant evidence of usefulness. Stillness asks for evaluation.

•  Problem-solving is socially rewarded. Everyone loves the leader who clears the line out the door. No one applauds the leader who protects two quiet hours to think.

•  Their calendar doesn’t feel like theirs. Meetings reproduce like rabbits; “open time” looks like a luxury.

•  They’re not sure what they’ll find. Being alone often turns the volume up on doubts, grief, envy, fear, exactly the material leaders are trained to outrun.

Research shows people often dislike being alone with their thoughts, so much so that, in classic experiments, some participants preferred administering mild electric shocks to themselves rather than sitting quietly with nothing to do but think. It may be human default to find oneself uncomfortable with aloneness, but leadership demands a different relationship with that default.

Long before we had iPhones, we built institutions around the insight that attention is fragile. Cloisters were designed to physically protect silence, contemplation, and spiritual formation because the mind needs a boundary to become itself.

Thoreau wrote about going to the woods to “live deliberately,” to face the essential facts but you don’t have to move to a remote cabin to practice deliberate living. You just have to stop letting every spare second be occupied by someone else’s agenda.

The Experiment: The 7-Day “Cloister Block”

Duration: One week

Time required: 10 minutes daily

Materials: Nothing required (ditch the phone, even the notes app for this one)

Goal: Experience what changes when your brain gets a protected, analog container.

For seven days, do this once per day:

Step One: Set the Stage

•  Choose a 45-minute block (same time each day if possible).

•  Remove the phone from the room (not face down, not on silent—gone).

Step Two: Pick One “Slow Cognition” Activity:

•  Read 10–20 pages of a print book, or

•  Write by hand (ideas, drafting, journaling), or

•  Do deep work offline (Wi-Fi off; single-task only).

Step Three: Add One Deliberate Friction Lever (Choose One):

•  Print what you’re working on and mark it up with a pen.

•  Use a kitchen timer instead of a phone timer.

•  If you need to look something up, write the question on a sticky note and keep going; research only after the block ends.

Step Four: Two-Minute Debrief (Right After):

•  What was the hardest minute?

•  When did my mind finally “arrive”?

•  What did I notice that I usually miss?

•  What do I want to protect about this feeling?

Breathe Before the Story

Your nervous system is always arriving at the story before you do.

New information doesn’t enter a vacuum, it enters a body—one that may already be running hot from a sleepless night, three cups of coffee, a tense commute, or a cortisol spike left over from the last hard conversation. That body is doing interpretation work whether or not you’re aware of it. A racing heart before a difficult meeting might be adrenaline. It might be caffeine. It might be genuine dread. Your brain, wired to make meaning faster than it makes accuracy, will pick one of those explanations—usually the most emotionally charged one available—and build a story around it before you’ve had time to ask whether it’s true.

This is a more embodied version of what organizational theorist Chris Argyris called the ladder of inference: the process by which we take a sliver of raw data, run it through our assumptions and beliefs, and arrive—in moments—at a conclusion that feels like observed reality. Argyris’s insight was about how quickly we move from data to interpretation, often without consciously observing that we’re doing it. What gets less attention is the body’s role in that shift. Hear a comment about your team when your cortisol is already elevated, and you may interpret a question as an attack. Hear someone say they want to give you feedback, and your threat system is already prepared to hear something negative before the sentence ends.

There’s a name for this broader phenomenon in cognitive science: the narrative brain. We are storytelling animals, or, as neuroscientist and author Lisa Feldman Barrett puts it, prediction machines. We are constantly generating models of what’s happening and what it means and updating those models with incoming data. This is mostly a gift. It is how we make sense of complexity, impart meaning on experience, and share emotional information with each other across time. But the same machinery that allows us to make meaning also causes us to impose meaning.

The body is the missing variable in most leadership advice about listening and interpretation. You can have every intention of taking in information with openness and curiosity, and your physiology can quietly overrule your intentions by narrowing your attention, priming your threat response, and pulling your interpretation in a direction you didn’t choose and may not notice until after the fact.

“Breathe before the story” is the corrective here. This is not a metaphor—literally count your breaths before you respond to an email that feels pointed. Before you let yourself decide what the feedback means. Before you interpret the silence in the room. It creates the conditions for the story you tell yourself to be somewhat more accurate, somewhat less shaped by whatever your nervous system brought to the room before the conversation started.

The Experiment: Breathe Your Way Through the Logic

Duration: One week

Time required: 5 minutes daily

Materials: Nothing required

Goal: The experiment that follows is a structured practice for building this habit as a daily perceptual discipline.

1.  Don’t wait until your system spikes to try this. Try taking five intentional breaths right now. Don’t worry about counting or structure, just take a deep breath in through your nose, and then exhale deeply out your mouth. Try to exhale for about the same amount of time you inhale.

2.  Now bring to mind something you’ve been thinking about and try to work your way back through the elements that have shaped the narrative you may be telling yourself.

a.  Who are the characters involved—who were you with?

b.  What’s the setting—where were you?

c.  When did the event in question take place?

d.  What’s the story you’re telling yourself about it?

3.  Now break the story down a bit.

a.  What beliefs or assumptions do you have about the people involved? How might those have shaped your interpretation of what happened?

b.  What else was going on that may have impacted how you heard or interpreted the situation?

4.  Keep up with the breathing!

5.  Try to write out in a sentence or two what you’re observing about your understanding. It may be that nothing has changed, but you may also have experienced a shift. Start like this, “The story I was telling myself is …” and then write another sentence that starts, “I now see that the story could be …”

One Rep Counts

The older we’ve gotten, the more convinced we’ve become that lowering the bar to start is the trick to getting anything done in life. The amount of friction in life only increases in your working years. Work becomes fuller, more challenging, family life blooms new responsibilities, and time remains steadfastly limited. New ideas and projects can take on a shimmering, impenetrable sheen. Write a book … but where would I even start? The blank page stops feeling like an invitation—a blank slate on which to go anywhere—and starts feeling like a whistling chasm into which no idea can return. Something to be avoided. Why start something new when you could simply sidestep the pain of starting at all?

And writing a book is a relatively straightforward process. At a certain point, the task is simply sitting and typing. Take something more complex—getting in shape, or changing the culture of your organization. If you feel out of shape, or like you’re navigating a sour culture, you know how intimidating the prospect of that project can feel. Change the culture? Starting where? By doing what?

Here’s our answer: Start small. Start so small you may not even notice you’ve begun. The goal is to slip under the radar of your brain’s alarm system—the one that fires up when something new is happening that it needs to understand, plan for, and defend against. Flip to a new page and write one word. If a new page feels like too much, write on a Post-it, write on an envelope, write on a baseball.b Send one email. Eat one healthier snack. Do one pushup. If you want to change a culture, start by having one better conversation.c

You may not need to lower the bar to one every time, but it’s a good reminder that you can. James Clear captures something important here in Atomic Habits: Habits and identity shape each other in a loop. If you run with some consistency, you’re more likely to start thinking of yourself as a runner—and that identity then gives you easier access to the next run, because runners run. What’s useful about Clear’s framing for our purposes is that if you lower the bar low enough—say, one lap of the track—then all you need to do to maintain that identity is one lap today. Do more if you can. If you don’t, no worries: You’re a runner, you’ll get back to it. The focus is consistency over quantity, in the belief that just starting regularly is what creates the conditions for doing more over time.

The Experiment: The Minimum Viable Action

Duration: 7 days

Time required: 10 minutes upfront, 5 minutes daily

Materials: A notebook or notes app, and one project or habit you have been avoiding

Goal: Pick one thing you’ve been meaning to do but keep putting off and do the smallest possible version of it every day for one week.

In startup culture, the Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is the simplest possible version of something that still works. It is not the finished thing. It is the thing that gets you started, gets something real into the world, and creates the conditions for iteration. We want to borrow that concept here and apply it to whatever you have been circling.

Step One: Name the Thing

Write down one project, habit, or change you have been wanting to start but have not. Be honest about it. The book. The difficult conversation. Physical exercise. The job you want to go for. The team culture you keep meaning to address.

Step Two: Find Your Minimum Viable Action

Now ask yourself: What is the smallest possible version of starting this thing? Not the plan—the action. Not the outline—the first word. Use the prompts below to get there:

1.  What is one thing I could do in under two minutes that would count as beginning?

2.  What would I do if I only had five minutes and no preparation?

3.  If I had to start right now, with what I have, what would the first move be?

Write your answers. You are looking for the version so small it almost feels like it doesn’t count. That is the right version.

Step Three: Do It Today

Not this week. Today. The minimum viable action you identified above, do it before the day is out. The point is not to make progress. The point is to make contact with the thing, to cross the threshold from not-started to started, however modestly.

Step Four: Lower the Bar for the Week

For the next seven days your only commitment is the minimum viable action, once per day. No more is required. More is welcome, but not expected. If you do more, great. If you do only the minimum, you have still done the thing, and that is the whole point.

Each evening, write two notes:

Did I do it? What did I notice about starting versus not starting?

Most people find that the minimum viable action takes far less time and energy than the anticipation suggested, and that once started, they often continue beyond the minimum without any additional effort or motivation. Research on action-state orientation suggests that the brain’s resistance is almost entirely located at the threshold between intention and action, and that the primary skill worth developing is not motivation or confidence, but a practiced willingness to cross it.d Once you are across it, the alarm quiets.

By the end of the week, you are not looking for a finished project or a transformed habit. You are looking for evidence that starting is something you can do reliably—that the bar, set low enough, is always crossable. Identity follows from that consistency, not from the size of any single effort.

One lap. One word. One conversation. Every time you show up for the minimum, you are telling yourself something important about who you are, and that is enough to build on.

Train Your Nervous System

If you’re like most people, you don’t notice your breathing until something goes wrong. You’re in a meeting, you’re supposed to be composed and persuasive, and suddenly your chest feels tight, your thoughts race ahead of you, and your mouth can’t quite keep up. Or you’re lying awake at night, replaying a conversation, aware that you should be tired but feeling almost electrically charged instead, feeling your heartbeat in your ears.

It’s a lot like listening. In grade school, we’re taught to read, write, and speak, but most of us aren’t really taught to listen. Why? Because if you can hear, there’s this quiet assumption that capability equals skill. Breathing gets treated the same way. Because we’ve been doing it since birth, we assume we’re “good at it.” But automatic doesn’t mean optimal, especially under pressure.

For much of the day, many of us breathe on autopilot, taking short, shallow breaths high in the chest. Those breaths typically require zero friction. They keep you alive, but they don’t fully engage your diaphragm or fill the base of your lungs. Your brain, which uses about 20 percent of your body’s oxygen, will always prioritize basic survival functions—keeping your heart beating, lungs moving, and reflexes sharp—before it allocates resources to higher-order tasks like focus, memory, judgment, and emotional regulation. Over time, that chronic under-fueling quietly chips away at your ability to think clearly and stay steady under pressure.

Slow, full, diaphragmatic breathing reverses that pattern. When you deliberately breathe in a way that makes your belly expand and your lungs fully inflate, you give your brain what it needs to do its best work. People often describe the shift as almost immediate: a sense that the mental fog has thinned, that their thoughts feel a little more organized, that emotions are still present but not as overwhelming.

In Emotional Intelligence 2.0, readers were invited to practice this in the heat of stressful moments—slow inhale through the nose, belly expanding; complete exhale through the mouth—until “breathing right” became an invisible tool you could use in the middle of a difficult conversation. Cyclic breathing is the next layer on that same skill.

Instead of just “taking a deep breath,” you guide your breathing into a specific, repeated pattern: a steady inhale, sometimes a brief second “top-off” inhale, and then a deliberately longer exhale. You repeat that sequence again and again for a few minutes, like tracing a small circle with your breath.

Why does that circle matter so much for how your mind feels? It is one part physiological, another part psychological. In each breath cycle, your heart rate naturally speeds up a bit on the inhale and slows on the exhale. When you extend and smooth out your exhalations, you stimulate the vagus nerve and increase heart rate variability (HRV), a marker of your nervous system’s flexibility and capacity to return to calm after stress. Slow, diaphragmatic breathing has also been shown to reduce negative mood and cortisol while improving attention and sustained focus in healthy adults.

When you’re anxious or overwhelmed, your breathing tends to become faster and more shallow without your permission. Your brain reads that pattern as confirmation that something is wrong and ramps up its threat response. Your attention narrows to whatever feels dangerous, your inner dialogue speeds up, and it becomes harder to access perspective, nuance, or choice. You feel caught inside your own head, pulled in multiple directions at once.

Cyclic breathing interrupts that loop. By intentionally slowing and shaping your breathing, you send a different signal back up to the brain: We’re not in immediate danger right now. Over time and repetition, your emotional system learns to trust that signal.

If you want a vivid picture of what happens when breathing goes in the opposite direction, when it becomes wildly dysregulated, consider Dan Harris, the ABC News anchor who famously had a panic attack on live television in 2004. On a warm June morning, sitting at the news desk on Good Morning America in front of about five million viewers, his heart began pounding, his breath shortened, his face tightened, and he suddenly found he couldn’t speak. That moment, which he has described in detail since, became a turning point in his life and career, sending him into a deep exploration of anxiety, meditation, and breath.

Most leaders will never experience that level of public panic, but many recognize the early, quieter version: the breath that jumps into the chest before a big presentation; the tightness in the throat during a difficult conversation; the rapid, shallow breathing that shows up in the car after a long day. Those moments might not look dramatic from the outside, but inside they erode your ability to lead. They narrow your focus and make it harder to access curiosity, empathy, and judgment.

This is where cyclic breathing becomes a practical leadership tool. When you notice those early physical cues, you don’t have to wait and see whether they build into a bigger storm. You can intervene at the level of breath, not just at the level of thought.

The science backs this up. In a randomized controlled study, researchers compared several five-minute daily practices: exhale-focused cyclic breathing, other breath protocols, and mindfulness meditation. The exhale-focused cyclic pattern—often called a “cyclic sigh,” where you take a steady inhale, a short second sip of air, and then a long, slow exhale—produced the greatest improvements in mood and the largest reductions in physiological arousal (including respiratory rate), outperforming mindfulness meditation.

Think about that: Five minutes of structured breathing, once a day, created measurable shifts in how people felt and how their bodies responded to stress. That doesn’t replace meaningful changes to workload, support, or systems, but it gives you a low-cost, highly portable way to change your internal state in the middle of all that.

Over time, cyclic breathing becomes a quiet form of self-leadership. Instead of letting your nervous system drag you from one spike of reactivity to the next, you build a habit of pausing, circling your breath a few times, and then responding from a slightly steadier place. You don’t stop feeling pressure. You don’t erase fear, frustration, or self-doubt. But you loosen their grip. You create just enough space inside to remember what matters and to act from that; you’re learning to stay with the friction differently.

The Experiment: Practicing Cyclic Breathing

Duration: One week

Time required: 5 minutes daily (or 30–60 seconds as an in-the-moment reset)

Materials: Nothing required; you don’t need special equipment or a quiet retreat space for this. You can practice in a conference room, at your desk, or in your parked car.

Goal: Build a breathing practice that gives your nervous system a reliable reset

1.  Set your posture.

•  Sit or stand with a long, relaxed spine; upright but not rigid.

•  Let your shoulders drop and your jaw soften.

•  If it helps, place one hand on your chest and one on your belly. Aim for the lower hand (belly) to move more than the upper.

2.  Begin with a gentle exhale.

•  Breathe out through your mouth as if you’re slowly fogging a window.

•  Don’t force it; just let the air leave until your lungs feel relatively empty.

3.  The cyclic breath (physiological / cyclic sigh).

Each breath has three parts:

a)   First inhale (nose, into the belly)—about 2–3 seconds

° Inhale steadily through your nose.

° Feel your lower ribs and belly expand; avoid lifting your shoulders.

b)   Second “top-off” inhale (nose)—about one second

° Without exhaling, take a short second sip of air.

° Comfortably fill your lungs a bit more—don’t strain.

c)   Long exhale (mouth)—about 4–8 seconds

° Exhale slowly and steadily through your mouth, like a quiet sigh.

° Aim for the exhale to be at least twice as long as the inhale.

° Let your shoulders and face soften as the air leaves.

That’s one cycle. Continue at an easy, unhurried pace.

4.  How often and how long.

You can use this in two main ways:

• Daily practice (conditioning your system):

° Set a timer for five minutes.

° Repeat the cyclic pattern for the entire time (many people land around 5–7 breaths per minute).

° If your mind wanders, notice it and return to the next breath.

•  In-the-moment reset:

° Take 3–5 cycles:

° Before a challenging conversation or presentation

° After an intense meeting

° When you notice your thoughts speeding up or your chest tightening

Even 30–60 seconds can shift your state enough to think more clearly.

5.  What to notice.

As you practice, you may observe:

•  A slight drop in heart rate

•  Your shoulders settling without you forcing them

•  A bit more mental “space” around your thoughts

Management Begins in Your Feet

Most leaders reach for thinking when they need self-management—rehearsing talking points, searching for the right framework, willing themselves toward calm. But as we’ve seen, that’s usually the wrong entry point. The body is where the work starts and sometimes the most reliable intervention is the simplest one. Get up and walk.

Not a workout. Not a badge-earning stroll. A plain ten-to-twenty-minute walk at a conversational pace. Around the block. Up and down a couple flights of stairs. Three slow hallway laps. Walking resets physiology, restores attention, and reopens perspective, the three levers that make self-management possible in the moments it most often slips.

First, physiology. Light, rhythmic movement cues the autonomic nervous system toward balance. Heart rate smooths. Breath lengthens. The stress chemistry that shouts “act now” loses its edge. That’s one reason even very brief bouts of activity produce measurable mood benefits: Randomized experiments have shown mood improves after just minutes of movement, with growing evidence that interrupting prolonged sitting with short activity breaks steadies prefrontal function—the brain systems you need for inhibition and flexible thinking. A recent systematic umbrella review and meta-meta-analysis goes further, underscoring that across designs and age groups, physical activity reliably reduces symptoms of anxiety and depression.

Second, attention. Under pressure, focus tends to tunnel. Walking widens the aperture. As you move forward, your visual field flows past you (“optic flow”), a natural input that helps downshift threat perception and frees up working memory. You literally start seeing more options. (Researchers have long noted the nervous system’s sensitivity to optic flow; more recent popular summaries have highlighted how forward ambulation calms the brain’s alarm systems.) Also, there are good reasons to leave your phone behind: Even when you don’t touch it, the mere presence of your smartphone can sap working memory and self-control; your brain spends scarce resources inhibiting the urge to check it. And notifications, even if you don’t look at them, still impose an attentional tax that degrades performance and increases mind-wandering. During a walk meant to restore self-management, that background load blunts the very reset you’re trying to buy. For ten minutes, stash it.

Third, perspective. Emotions are information. They point to what you value and what you fear losing, but volume can distort message. A simple practice mid-walk—label what you’re feeling in a sentence—has disproportionate effects. In fMRI studies, “affect labeling” (e.g., “I feel defensive because my idea was dismissed”) decreases amygdala activation and recruits prefrontal regions associated with regulation. Translation: Putting feelings into words while you’re moving makes it easier to respond rather than react.

Walking also changes the type of thinking you can do. When the task is open-ended (e.g., drafting talking points, brainstorming solutions, reframing a tense exchange, etc.), slightly increasing the friction involved in your thinking—that is, walking versus sitting—reliably boosts creative ideation. In controlled experiments, people generated significantly more novel uses for common objects when walking, with effects both on treadmills and outdoor paths. That matters in leadership, where many “right answers” are invented, not retrieved.

If you manage people, there’s a social dividend too. Side-by-side movement lowers defensiveness. A complicated 1:1 that would overheat across a desk often softens on a loop around the building. You return with the same facts and a different tone, which is usually what was missing.

Leaders sometimes worry that a walk is avoidance. And truth is, it can be; any tool can be misused. The distinction is intention and follow-through. Avoidance buys distance to delay responsibility. A management walk buys clarity to take it. The test is simple: Does the walk end with one concrete next step, and do you take it? If yes, you didn’t delay the work; you improved it.

And the benefits compound. Walking can feel like it’s “doing nothing,” especially to achievement-oriented leaders who equate value with visible output. Think of it as a 401(k) for your nervous system: small, regular contributions that compound into a steadier baseline. Each short walk yields a tiny dividend, slightly kinder phrasing, a cleaner prioritization, a cooler tone. Over weeks and months, those micro-advantages accrue into reputation: calm under pressure, fair in disagreement, clear in decision and the “market” notices.

Crucially, the science behind “small and regular” is on your side. Short, frequent activity breaks during sedentary work improve task-processing speed and prefrontal activation; micro-activity often outperform a single morning session when your day is long and cognitively loaded. Meanwhile, on the population level, we have strong evidence that simply walking more is linked to better mood and lower depression risk. A 2024 meta-analysis of 33 observational studies (96,173 adults) found that higher daily step counts were associated with fewer depressive symptoms; moving from <5,000 steps to ≥7,000 was linked with meaningfully lower risk. Complementary systematic reviews focusing specifically on walking show reductions in depression and anxiety symptoms compared with controls.

You don’t need perfect weather or a park, though nature can amplify benefits by further restoring attention and easing stress. (Randomized studies separating exercise from environment suggest being in green space confers an extra mood lift, but treadmill or hallway walking still helps.) You don’t need thirty minutes either; ten to fifteen inserted before or after high-stakes moments is often enough to recover inhibition and perspective when you’d otherwise slide into reactivity or decision fatigue. And if your day leaves no gaps, make some meetings mobile. Research indicates walking meetings increase creative output and engagement, which is the point of most status updates anyway.

Bottom line: When self-management slips, don’t start with your head, start with your feet. The walk is not time away from leadership; it’s the precondition for doing it well.

The Experiment: Reframe Your Walk

Duration: One week

Time required: 10–20 minutes daily

Materials: Nothing required

Goal: Get the biggest impact from your walk.

Make it an experiment goal to improve your walks in these ways:

1.  Leave your phone or lock it. If you bring it for safety or notes, use airplane mode.

2.  Keep the pace conversational. Light aerobic intensity best supports prefrontal control and mood without adding “workout stress.” Short, moderate-intensity interruptions of sitting have improved processing speed and prefrontal task activation in randomized crossover designs.

3.  Lengthen your exhale while you move. Try ~4 seconds in, ~6 seconds out. Longer exhales stimulate parasympathetic activity, complementing the calming effect of light ambulation (consistent with the autonomic downshift observed during active microbreaks).

4.  Eyes up to invite optic flow. Let your gaze rest at the hallway “horizon,” not your shoes or phone. The visual flow of forward movement helps the nervous system down-regulate threat scanning and widen attention.

5.  Name, then aim. Mid-walk, label your primary emotion in a sentence (“I feel ___ because ___”) and follow with “What outcome do I want 24 hours from now?”

6.  End with one next step. Before you “re-enter,” choose a single action (e.g., send a three-bullet reply; schedule a call; capture a defer-until date). Behavior specificity is how a calmer state becomes better leadership.

The best time to walk? Any time. But here are 2 specific use cases that can have outsized impact:

•  Use short, frequent resets around high-stakes moments. Ten to fifteen minutes before difficult emails, feedback, or negotiations. Evidence favors repeated walks for cognitive steadiness across the day.

•  Make some 1:1s walking talks. Side-by-side movement lowers defensiveness and, in controlled studies, walking increases creative ideation, useful for problem-solving conversations. Capture decisions immediately after returning to your work space.

Name the Weather

You’ve probably spent some time in your life looking at clouds. Storm clouds rolling in, the red and orange cotton-candy clouds of a notable sunset, the anthropomorphic faces and lounging animals you find if you look long enough. But have you ever looked at a cloud and thought, “interesting to see cirrus clouds today instead of stratus”? Probably not. Which is why it’s worth knowing that in its 2017 update, the World Meteorological Organization’s International Cloud Atlas now includes 10 primary cloud categories and over 100 types, subtypes, and special formations. Meteorologists have more than 100 ways of describing what’s happening in the sky.

We explored the idea of your emotional vocabulary in Chapter 2. If you took the time to test yourself then, no need to repeat. But if not … put the book down for a moment, pick up a pen, and start writing. How many emotions can you list? How many categories? Do you think there are more ways to describe clouds or emotions?

Most people are surprised to find it isn’t close.

Lisa Feldman Barrett argues in How Emotions Are Made, having a finer-grained vocabulary for emotion is one of the most powerful levers we have for improving how we regulate and communicate our inner experience. She calls this emotional granularity, the ability to make precise distinctions between emotional states rather than collapsing everything into broad categories like “stressed” or “upset.” Research shows that people with higher emotional granularity recover more quickly from negative experiences, use more adaptive coping strategies, and are less likely to react impulsively when emotions run high.

In the same way that the International Cloud Atlas creates a shared language and disciplines perception, a fine-grained emotional vocabulary improves your ability to think and communicate about what’s happening inside you. It also supports the idea that emotions are data and signals, not just descriptions. Seeing dark clouds may portend a storm; knowing that irritation can build into frustration that can build into anger helps you understand and manage how your feelings unfold and how you respond to them in context. There’s a real difference between remorse and shame, between feeling isolated and feeling abandoned, between feeling hopeful and feeling inspired. Those distinctions point toward different causes, different needs, and different responses.

Being more observant and disciplined in naming your emotional state builds awareness over time. You start to notice patterns: the who, what, when, and where of certain feelings. You catch things earlier. Taking time to name the weather makes you more aware of the weather, more attuned to how the systems change and interact, and better able to recognize when things are heating up and what kind of response the moment calls for.

The Experiment: The Weather Log

Duration: One week

Time required: 5–10 minutes daily

Materials: A feelings wheel (a circular chart that shows broad core emotions at the center and moves outward to increasingly specific emotions at the edges; you can find a number of different variations online) and a notebook or notes app.

Goal: Incorporate 3 to 5 new emotion words into your working vocabulary.

Day 1: Stock the Shelf (10 minutes)

Open your feelings wheel and scan it slowly. You are looking for words that describe something real in your experience but that you would not naturally reach for. From everything that catches your attention, choose 3 to 5 words to commit to for the week. Write a one-sentence definition of each in your own words. Keep them somewhere visible.

Days 2 to 7: Log the Weather (5 minutes each day)

Once each day, at whatever time works best, open your notebook and write a brief weather report:

•  What am I feeling right now? (Does one of the words that caught your eye on the feelings wheel apply?)

•  Intensity: 1 to 10

•  What seems to be driving it?

You are not analyzing. You are observing and naming. Precision is the practice.

End of Week Reflection (10 minutes)

Look back across your seven entries and ask:

•  Which words showed up most naturally?

•  Which ones still feel hard to reach for in the moment?

•  Did naming something more precisely change how you thought about it or responded to it, even once?

General Reflection: Think of a recent moment where you reacted in a way you didn’t fully understand. Looking back at your feelings wheel, what word would you use now? What might you have done differently if you had been able to name it at the time?

Calibrate Your Inner Forecaster

The night before a hard conversation with your department, your mind screens a movie of the future. In this cut you stumble over words, you observe angry expressions on faces in the crowd, you feel the meeting’s judgment settle on your chest like a weighted blanket. The movie is vivid, persuasive, full of little details that feel like “The Truth.” Psychologists call this affective forecasting—our built-in habit of predicting how we’ll feel later—and for more than two decades researchers like Timothy Wilson and Daniel Gilbert have shown how often those forecasts are wrong, especially about how bad something will feel and how long the feeling will last.

You go to bed convinced tomorrow will be unbearable and, worse, defining. Then the meeting comes. It’s tense, sure. But you shared some hard realities of the business and decisions that had to be made. A few questions are tricky and pointed, a few are fair, someone pauses to think in a way that looks like disapproval and turns out to be respect. By late afternoon you are tired but not wrecked, focused on important next steps. Two days later, if someone asks how it went, you answer from memory, not experience, and the memory quietly edits itself to match your earlier forecast. Emotion researchers like Linda Levine have found that this is exactly what memory does: It reconstructs how we “must have” felt so the story hangs together; your mind prefers stories with continuity.

Wilson and Gilbert call this impact bias and durability bias: the tendency to predict that emotional hits will land harder and linger longer than they actually do. In a hot state, dread is all you can see; in a cool state, you can hardly remember what dread felt like. Behavioral economist George Loewenstein calls this a hot–cold empathy gap or our inability to imagine a different emotional temperature from the one we’re in. The result is a leadership life lived under the gravity of inaccurate weather reports.

Sometimes, the forecast isn’t just this will be uncomfortable. It’s this will prove I don’t belong here. And unfortunately, the forecaster doesn’t only run before hard things happen; it runs just as hard in the seconds after you’ve already said the wrong thing.

Maggie: In 2014 I took a role as Junior Faculty at the Center for Creative Leadership. CCL is a world-renowned leadership development firm, and the role of Faculty was sought after by seasoned professionals with decades more experience. I had been an intern for over two years, working directly with clients, but I was technically still a graduate student and at 31, one of the youngest people ever hired into that seat. Plenty of faculty recommended I not be hired because of my “lack of business experience.” I was a test case, and I felt immense pressure to help pave the way for less-experienced people coming up behind me.

On my first training program without training wheels, I flew to our headquarters in Greensboro, North Carolina. The air was crisp, the leaves crunched beneath my feet as I walked into the grand building at 1 Leadership Place. Midway through the second day, during a module on conflict, I was enthusiastically recounting a story and said, “God-d*mn it, he was so frustrating.” The second the phrase left my lips, I could feel my body inch forward in the air to try to suck the words back into my mouth.

It was one thing that I was in the Southeast United States—a conservative and religious area. But we literally had a pastor in our leadership program that week. I could feel my face flush and my palms start to sweat. I made loose eye contact with my co-facilitator, hoping he had lost consciousness for those three seconds. No luck. On our next break he came over to me and whispered, “It would have been better if you had said ‘mother-f*cker’.”

If you’ve ever had a moment like that, you know what happens next. Your forecaster doesn’t predict “brief embarrassment”; it predicts career-ending scandal. It queues up the panic—the pastor’s email to the CEO, the faculty grapevine, the “we tried the junior role and look how that went” postmortem. Your nervous system doesn’t do moderation in moments that touch identity; it does full-on catastrophe.

Calibration also shifts your language. You stop saying, “This is going to be awful,” and start saying, “My forecast is a seven out of ten for about ninety minutes, and here’s how I’ll navigate.” Leaders who narrate their inner weather as data give their teams permission to treat feelings as variables to plan around.

If “keeping score” sounds clinical, notice how quietly compassionate it is. You aren’t trying to prove your fear wrong or your hope right; you’re trying to see what is really happening. Memory researchers point out that our emotional recollections are designed to be useful more than accurate; by writing things down, you give yourself a record that can be both. The more you practice, the more your forecaster improves.

None of this means you ignore what your body tells you. Sometimes the forecast is right. Calibration helps there too. It keeps you from layering a second suffering, the shame of I shouldn’t feel this way, on top of the first. When you get good at prediction-experience loops, you stop bargaining with the future. You stop saying, “I’ll do it when I’m ready,” because you know readiness is a mirage anxiety uses to postpone. Seeing your feelings as predictions to be tested is where honest self-awareness lives.

Every time you act on your data instead of your dread, you remind yourself you can be afraid and competent at the same time.

•  Date & time:

These become the “historical averages” your inner forecaster uses instead of raw fear.

Before the next hard event:

•  Glance at your log.

•  Ask: “What do my numbers say—not my mood?”

•  Adjust your forecast (“Last three times this felt like a 9 in my head and landed around a 5.”).

•  Choose your behavior based on the data.

Vocab Rehabe

You can’t always choose what shows up in your mind, but you can choose the words that get a microphone.

Maggie: In 2015, I sat in my car outside Bespoke Partner’s downtown office. Ross had joined the firm in 2014 to build out the leadership assessment practice, incorporating psychometrics into the candidate evaluation process for executive searches. I was brought in for an all-company session to help build alignment and trust across the entire team, including the CEO. It was a big meeting. Big stakes. The kind of day where your calendar feels like a dare from that one mean girl in seventh grade. I could feel my body gearing up: jaw tight, shallow breath, that faint vibration in the chest that says, You are in danger.

And then the narrator arrived.

Not a full sentence at first, more like fragments and verdicts: This is going to be brutal, they will think it is “woo-woo.” You’re going to mess it up. You’re not ready. You will be the most inexperienced person in the room. The words weren’t dramatic. They were ordinary and that was the problem. They sounded like facts.

So I tried something small and just edited the words a bit:

•  “Brutal” became “whole” (The experience was going to be good and hard and I could learn from all of it.)

•  “Woo-woo” became “kind” (I was there to help; I knew how to translate research into practical impact.)

•  “You’re going to mess it up” became “grounded” (My feet were on the ground; the room wasn’t going to swallow me whole.)

When I told Ross this story after the session, it was clear that something shifted when my words shifted. The volume came down, the story focused, and I could breathe again.

That’s the quiet (unfortunate and fortunate) power of language. Because your brain uses your words as data, predicting what’s happening and preparing your body accordingly, the language you give it matters more than most leaders realize. A small subset of your thinking becomes self-talk—the thoughts that are phrased like language, with you as the audience. That self-talk is disproportionately influential because it’s sticky, believable, and usually happening on autopilot. And autopilot language describes your experience, but it also helps build it in real time.

And most damaging self-talk isn’t just negative; it’s absolute, judgmental, and identity-based:

•  “I always mess this up.”

•  “I’m an idiot.”

•  “This proves I’m not cut out for this.”

As we’ve established, your brain is generating stories constantly; the goal is to get better at recognizing which words are getting amplified. If your inner language says threat, your body prepares for threat. If your inner language says steady, your body has a chance to organize around steadiness. Words change the intensity of emotion, sometimes by changing the brain’s “volume knob.”

There’s another small language shift that research suggests is surprisingly powerful: Change the pronouns. When people use their own name or non-first-person pronouns while reflecting (“Ross, what do you need right now?” instead of “What do I need?”), they create distance from the heat of the moment and regulate stress more effectively. It’s like stepping one chair back from your emotions without abandoning them.

In the middle of friction, we default to language that speeds us up: urgent, behind, prove it, fix it, which tends to make the problem worse. Try these words to slow down and open up space: lift, true, steady, light, whole, kind, flow, grounded. These words signal state of mind and give your brain a simple cue it can follow. Single words work well because they’re portable. You can carry steady into a hard conversation, or warm into a tense email response, or rooted into a day when you’re tempted to bolt. We fully understand how tricky it is that leadership is so emotional, and at the same time, leaders have to take responsibility for the voice they’re led by.

The Experiment: The Self-Talk Swap

Duration: 7 days

Time required: 2–3 minutes daily

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Practice catching and rewording the internal sentences that spike your stress.

For the next seven days, do this once per day (takes 2–3 minutes):

1.   Write the sentence your mind is currently repeating (exact words).

Example: “I’m behind and this is going to be brutal.” Pay attention to the repeated word patterns. For many leaders it’s: behind, failing, stupid, too much, impossible, can’t.

2.   Underline the two hottest words (the ones that spike your body).

Example: behind, brutal

3.   Replace them using one data word + one feel-good word.

Example: “I’m stretched today, so I’m going to look for flow with 1 important task.”

4.   Speak the new sentence out loud twice. (Out loud matters.)

Optional prompt for journaling:

•  “When I say the old sentence, my body does ____.”

•  “When I say the new sentence, my body does ____.”

•  “If my inner voice were a good leader, it would sound like ____.”

You Are Not Your Job

Picture yourself at a networking event. Your former colleague introduces you to a former classmate, you shake hands, the person smiles politely, and asks the question most of us can answer in half a second:

“So, what do you do?”

You probably don’t pause to translate that question and likely answer in one clean sentence: “I’m a VP of operations,” “I run a small company,” “I’m in healthcare,” and the conversation moves on. In that tiny sentence, your profession or your role or your industry stands in for your entire self.

On the surface, it’s just small talk. Underneath, it’s a quiet equation: you = what you do.

We learn that equation early. In many Western classrooms, adults ask children, “What do you want to be when you grow up?” and expect answers in the language of occupations: doctor, engineer, entrepreneur. Those questions, repeated over years, quietly teach us that work isn’t just something we do, it’s who we are. Psychology gives us language for what happens next. Over time, each of us builds a “self-concept,” a mental map of who we are that’s organized around roles: parent, friend, leader, neighbor, artist, volunteer, and, of course, worker. Research on role centrality shows that when one role becomes disproportionately central, when the worker role takes up most of the map, our well-being becomes tightly tied to what happens in that arena. If that role is thriving, we feel okay. If it’s threatened, we don’t just worry about results; we feel like we are in danger.

Now layer in the emotional side. Studies of self-conscious emotions show that humans don’t just feel bad when things go wrong; we make meaning of what went wrong. If the story in your head sounds like, “I made a mistake on this project,” you’re likely to feel guilt; uncomfortable, but specific and actionable. Alternatively, if the story sounds like, “I am a failure,” that’s shame; global, heavy, and much harder to work with. When your identity is fused with your work, it becomes very easy for the story to slip from “something went badly” to “I am bad.” Not because you’re irrational, but because the culture around you has spent years linking worth to performance.

So, when something disruptive happens like a stalled product launch, a shitty quarter, investors asking hard questions, or the wider economy taking a wild turn, your nervous system doesn’t just register numbers on a spreadsheet; it’s registering physical threat.

People in medicine and mental health hear a version of this story all the time. Reviews of unemployment and job loss consistently show that losing work doesn’t just hurt because of money; it hurts because work quietly provided structure, status, and a ready-made answer to “Who am I and where do I belong?” Take that away, or even shake it, and anxiety and depression rise, even when financial strain is modest. If your job is your primary identity, anything that touches your job touches everything.

Consider three moments many leaders know too well:

•  You make a visible mistake that costs money or credibility.

•  Your business hits a downturn despite your best efforts.

•  A sudden industry or economic shift undercuts the strategy you’ve been executing flawlessly.

On the outside, these look like different situations. On the inside, they often produce the same pattern: a sharp sting of shame, a rapid search for where you “should have known better,” and a strong impulse either to overwork frantically or to mentally check out. If you’ve been storing most of your identity in your work, any crack in the work feels like a crack in you.

The goal isn’t to stop caring. Most people reading this have built a life around taking their work seriously, and there is real meaning there. The point is more precise: to see that you are a person who works, not a job title on legs. That sounds obvious in theory. In practice, it can feel almost wrong at first, especially if you’ve been rewarded for being “all in.” But it’s closer to how identity really works. Sociologists and identity theorists point out that people who spread their sense of self across multiple roles (e.g., professional, relational, personal, etc.) tend to be more resilient when any one role gets disrupted.

Seen from that angle, the pain you feel when the business struggles or the economy swings is not just about money or reputation; it’s also your mind trying to defend the narrow place where you’ve been living. The panic, the harsh self-talk, or the reluctance to admit a gap or ask for help are all signals that the line between “who I am” and “how things are going at work right now” has gotten too thin.

Illuminating that pattern matters, especially in high-pressure roles. If you don’t see what’s happening, you’re likely to double down on the only strategy you know: work harder, tighten your grip, refuse to acknowledge limits. That may buy you some short-term results, but it deepens the inner conflict. You’re still trying to prove an impossible thesis—that if your numbers are good enough, you’ll finally be enough, too.

Separating who you are from what you do doesn’t mean lowering your standards. It means telling a truer story about yourself, one that can hold both excellence and imperfection. You are the person who made that call, in that context, with the information you had. You are also the person who’s learning from the outcome, adjusting, apologizing where needed, and trying again. Your character is expressed in what you do at work, but it isn’t exhausted by it.

The culture around you may continue to ask, “So, what do you do?” and you may continue to answer with your job title. But internally, you can hold a different understanding: I’m a person, with a work role I care about, moving through a changing landscape. My worth doesn’t rise and fall with every graph and every headline. From that place, you can look at mistakes, downturns, and wild economic swings with clearer eyes and decide what kind of person you want to be in response.

The Experiment #1: Separating Self from the Storm

Duration: 7 days

Time required: 10–15 minutes per activity

Materials: A pen and paper or notes app

Goal: Use this practice anytime you’ve made a mistake, your business isn’t doing well, or outside forces (like the economy or industry shifts) are crushing your numbers. It’s designed to be done in 10–15 minutes with a pen and paper.

Step One: Name the Event, Not Yourself

At the top of a page, write a one-sentence description of what happened, using neutral language:

•  “We missed our revenue target by 20% this quarter.”

•  “I approved a project that went over budget by $500,000.”

•  “A sudden rate hike/shock in the market led to a drop in demand.”

Avoid any “I am” language here. You’re capturing what happened.

Step Two: Separate Fact From Fiction

Draw three columns and label them:

1.  Facts

2.  Story About Me

3.  A Truer Story

Under Facts, list only what a camera or spreadsheet could verify: dates, numbers, actions taken, relevant external conditions (e.g., “interest rates rose,” “key client paused spending”). This column helps your brain see the situation as an event in the world, not a character judgment.

Under Story About Me, write the sentences that automatically come up in your mind when you think about this situation, especially after-hours or at 3 A.M. Be honest and unfiltered. Common examples:

•  “I’m not cut out for this.”

•  “I’ve ruined everything.”

•  “Everyone will finally see I’m a fraud.”

•  “If I were truly competent, this wouldn’t have happened, even with the economy like this.”

Now, move to a truer story. This is not the same as reframing what you want or redirecting your attention—you do that in the next experiment.f This column has one specific job: to put distance between who you are and what happened, so the event stops functioning as a verdict on your character. For each line in “Story About Me,” write a response that:

•  Separates you from the event.

•  Acknowledges both your part and the external conditions.

•  Stays specific and grounded.

For example:

•  Story: “I’m not cut out for this.”

°  Truer story: “I’m a capable leader who made a serious misjudgment in a volatile context. I’m learning what this role really demands.”

•  Story: “I’ve ruined everything.”

°  Truer story: “My decision has created real problems that I’m now responsible for helping to repair. That doesn’t erase everything I’ve built.”

•  Story: “If I were any good, the economy wouldn’t hit us this hard.”

°  Truer story: “External shocks are hitting our whole sector. My job is to respond as wisely as I can, not to control the entire market.”

You’re not sugarcoating what has happened. You’re putting the event back in proportion and reminding yourself that you are bigger than this one moment.

Step Three: Mark What’s Yours, and What Isn’t

Go back to the Facts column and put a small check mark next to anything you could realistically influence next time (e.g., “We relied too heavily on one segment,” “We ignored early warning signs”). Put a small dash next to things that are genuinely outside your control (e.g., “Regulation changed mid-year,” “Government shut-down froze budgets,” “Client’s parent company froze hiring globally”).

On a separate line, finish these two sentences:

•  “What is mine to own here is …”

•  “What is not mine to carry as identity is …”

This step helps align your energy with responsibility, not self-punishment.

Step Four: Expand Your “Identity Frame”

Finally, at the bottom of the page, answer these prompts:

•  “Outside of this situation, I am also someone who …” (list at least three roles or qualities: parent, friend, neighbor, learner, mentor, musician, volunteer)

•  “One small action I’ll take in the next 24 hours that is not about fixing this metric, but about living as the person I want to be, is …”

Maybe it’s reading with your kid, checking on a friend, going for a walk, or mentoring someone on your team. The point isn’t escape; it’s reminding your nervous system that your existence isn’t confined to this crisis.

When you’re done, read the A Truer Story column and your final answers out loud, slowly. Notice any shift in your body—breath, shoulders, jaw. You may still feel pressure and responsibility. But if you’ve done the exercise honestly, some of the crushing weight of “I am this failure” can begin to soften. You’re building a habit of seeing events as events, yourself as a whole person, and your work as one important place where that person shows up, not the only place where that person exists.

The Experiment #2: Update Your Identity Portfolio

Duration: 3–4 weeks (to allow time for the introduction experiment to play out across multiple real conversations)

Time required: 30–45 minutes for the initial exercise; five minutes for reflection after each introduction experiment

Materials: A blank piece of paper and a pen; a notebook or notes app for tracking reflections

Goal: Loosen the grip that a single role or outcome has on your sense of self.

Here’s an activity you can use yourself, or with your team, to begin separating who you are from what you do.

Step One: Rewrite Your “I Am” Statements

On the left side of a new page, write three sentences that you often think or say:

•  “I am a ___.”

•  “I’m the kind of person who ___ at work.”

•  “My value is in my ability to ___.”

Now, on the right side, rewrite each one to separate you from the role or outcome. For example:

I am a high performer.

I’m someone who cares about doing my best and learning when I fall short.

I am a CEO.

I currently serve as a CEO; I’m also a parent, a friend, and a person who loves mentoring others.

Aim for language that:

•  Describes roles as things you hold, not things you are.

•  Emphasizes enduring qualities (curiosity, integrity, compassion) over titles and wins.

Circle one or two revised statements that feel both true and slightly stretching, as if they belong to the version of you that you’re growing into.

Step Two: Experiment with a New Introduction

Script a new answer to “So, what do you do?” Try two or three versions that include, but don’t center, your job. For instance:

“By day I lead a team in healthcare operations; I’m really interested in how people grow through change. Outside of work I’m a dad and a very slow trail runner.”

Pick one version and test it in the next low-stakes conversation (e.g., a colleague you don’t know well, a new neighbor, a breakout room at a conference).

Afterward, jot down:

•  How did it feel in your body to answer that way?

•  What did you notice about the other person’s response?

•  Did it shift, even slightly, how you felt about yourself in that moment?

Repeat this a few times over the next month. You’re not trying to land on a perfect script. You’re practicing a way of communicating to others (and yourself) that your job is something you do, not a judgment on who you are.

Order What You (Actually) Want

There is a moment most of us have experienced but rarely examined. You walk into a situation already rehearsing what you hope will not happen. The conversation you are dreading. The meeting you expect to go sideways. The relationship pattern you are convinced will repeat itself. You have not yet said a word, made a decision, or taken a single step, and yet, in a very real psychological sense, you have already placed your order.

In 1987, psychologist Daniel Wegner conducted what became one of the most cited experiments in cognitive psychology. He asked participants to speak their thoughts aloud for several minutes while following one simple instruction: Do not think about a white bear. Participants were given a bell and told to ring it any time the bear crossed their mind. The results were striking. The bell rang constantly. And in a follow-up phase, those who had tried hardest to suppress the thought ended up thinking about the bear far more than those who had never tried to avoid it at all. Wegner called this ironic process theory—the mind, in its effort to monitor whether you are thinking about something, must first call that thing to mind. Suppression does not eliminate a thought; it amplifies it.

We wish this were merely a laboratory curiosity, though we have seen with ourselves, colleagues, and clients, that it is the architecture of how many of us move through our daily lives.

The Black Coffee Theory, a concept entrepreneur Ankur Warikoo popularized illustrates this with disarming simplicity. Imagine walking up to a café counter and, when asked what you would like, responding only with what you do not want. No black coffee, you say. The barista asks again. Still no black coffee, you repeat. Minutes later, a black coffee arrives. Not out of malice, but because that was the only clear, repeated signal you sent. The mind—yours and, metaphorically, the world around you—responds to what is most dominant in your attention, not to what you wish were absent from it.

The implications for how we lead ourselves and others are significant. Thoughts do not stay quietly in the mind; they shape emotion. A thought held with enough repetition generates a feeling, be it unease, resignation, low-grade anxiety, or something else, and that feeling then colors perception, narrows options, and drives behavior. The person who enters a difficult conversation already convinced it will go poorly tends to communicate in ways that make that outcome more likely. The leader who focuses relentlessly on what the team must avoid tends to create cultures defined by hesitation rather than initiative. This is not pessimism as a personality flaw, but a cognitive habit, and like all habits, it operates largely below conscious awareness.

The goal here is not to mandate positivity, toxic or otherwise, or to suggest that reframing alone solves complex problems. It is something more useful than that: intentionality. If your thoughts are already influencing your feelings, and your feelings are already shaping your actions, then the only question worth asking is whether you are directing that process or simply inheriting its results. Most of us, most of the time, are inheriting.

The first step toward changing that is noticing.

The Experiment: From Avoidance to Intention

Duration: 7 days

Time required: 10–15 minutes per day

Materials: A notebook or notes app dedicated to this exercise

Goal: This experiment is designed not to make you more optimistic, but to make you more aware—and then more deliberate. It works in two phases.

Phase One: The Audit (Days 1–3)

The practice: This phase is not about rewriting the story of who you are—that’s the work of separating self from event, which belongs to the previous experiment.g This is simpler and more forward-facing. Before the day begins, write your answers to two questions:

•  What do I actually want from today, specifically?

•  What would it look and feel like if that happened?

Write without filtering. These might be outcomes you were dreading, impressions you were afraid of making, failures you were trying not to repeat, or conversations you were bracing for. Be specific.

Then, for each item, ask:

Did focusing on this help me, or did it keep me circling the same ground?

What you are looking for: You are not trying to fix anything in this phase. You are auditing the inputs. Most people are surprised to discover how much of their mental bandwidth is organized around avoidance rather than intention. Notice patterns. Notice which areas of life—relationships, work, health, self-image—tend to produce the most avoidance-based thinking.

Phase Two: Reordering (Days 4–7)

The practice: Each morning, before the day begins, write your answers to two questions:

What do I actually want from today, specifically? What would it look and feel like if that happened?

This is your order at the counter. Be concrete. Not “I want the meeting to go well” but “I want to communicate my idea clearly and leave the room having genuinely heard the other person’s concerns.” Not “I don’t want to feel overwhelmed” but “I want to finish the day having focused on the two things that matter most.”

Each evening, return to what you wrote and reflect briefly:

Where did my thinking drift back into avoidance mode today? What triggered it? When I stayed oriented toward what I wanted, what did I notice about how I showed up?

By day seven, most people notice one of two things:

1.  Either the exercise feels labored and forced, which is itself useful information, a signal of how deeply habitual avoidance-thinking has become, or

2.  They begin to notice a subtle but real shift in how they enter situations because the dominant signal they were sending themselves changed.

You are not trying to eliminate pessimistic thinking. You are trying to catch it earlier, name it clearly, and replace it with something more specific and more useful. The white bear does not disappear because you demand it leave. It quiets when you give your mind something else to move toward, something vivid, concrete, and genuinely desired.

Build The Pool

Most leaders don’t describe their workday as “busy” so much as “barely keeping up.” If you listen closely, their language sounds less like a job and more like survival: I’m underwater. I’m slammed. I can’t come up for air. The image is usually the same: open water, no land in sight, the sense that if they stop moving for even a moment, everything will go under with them.

Maggie: When I think back on groups of leaders arriving at a week-long development program, that is how they appeared: not simply tired, but shipwrecked. They walked into the room as if they had just been hauled out of an ocean they hadn’t chosen to swim in. Simply too much friction to manage. The metaphor I carried then has become clearer over time. At the beginning of the week, their leadership felt like treading in deep, unpredictable ocean water. No boundaries or bottom or visible edge. By the end of the week, my goal wasn’t to promise calm seas; it was to help them move that same effort into a pool.

The pool is not easier in the sense of being effortless. You still get tired and still have to work hard to tread water in the deep end, but the water is contained. There are edges to reach for, ladders to climb, and shallows where you can stand. You know where the lanes begin and end. You know which part is yours. The job of leadership, reframed, is not to conquer an ocean; it is to learn to swim hard, with purpose, in a pool you can define and leave.

Part of what makes leadership feel like open water is the sheer volume and variety of friction. Internally, there is the constant drag of doubt, fatigue, and competing priorities. Interpersonally, there are misalignments, conflicts, and difficult conversations that never seem to resolve. Systemically, there are structures, politics, and constraints that do not bend just because you are working late. Taken together, these currents convince many leaders that if they are struggling, they must be failing.

A more honest view starts with the recognition that friction is the medium of leadership, not a sign that you are in the wrong job. What can change is the container you imagine yourself swimming in. One element of that shift is mindset. Research on “stress mindsets” shows that people who see stress as purely harmful tend to experience more negative emotions, more strain, and narrower thinking when demands are high. Those who see stress as something that, while uncomfortable, can be a source of learning and growth respond differently: They show more adaptive physiological patterns and better work performance under the very same pressures.

Translating that into everyday leadership means narrowing how you think about responsibility. In ocean mode, everything is yours: every request, every escalation, every piece of friction, every late-night email. No wonder you feel like you are being pulled under. In pool mode, you start quietly redrafting the job. You ask, Which of these waves are mine to swim in? Which decisions genuinely require my involvement? Where is my lane, and where does someone else’s begin? The work remains demanding, but it becomes bounded. You still swim hard, but not everywhere all at once.

But even the best-designed pool requires you to eventually climb out. Psychological detachment from work during nonwork time has been linked repeatedly with lower emotional exhaustion and better overall well-being.

What gets in the way is not a lack of information. Most leaders already know, at least in theory, that time off is good for them. What traps them in the water is a belief that leadership is incompatible with leaving the pool. They imagine that good leaders are endlessly available, perpetually informed, and always one call away from the next crisis. If they climb out, even briefly, they fear the ocean will rush back in.

If your leadership always feels like the open sea, you are less likely to ever step out. But if you have done the work to turn your job into a pool, with lanes, lifeguards, and other swimmers who can take a lap while you rest, the idea of taking hold of the edge or leaving the deck no longer feels catastrophic; it feels responsible, and what you would also suggest to those leaders you manage. Research on recovery from job stress suggests that the way leaders themselves detach from work influences how their teams experience detachment and strain. When people see their leader consistently in the water (i.e., emailing at midnight, postponing vacations, taking calls from the hotel lobby, etc.), they learn that getting out of the pool is not safe. When they see a leader who protects time away, who is occasionally and unapologetically absent from the water, they learn that rest is part of the job rather than an exception to it. Seen this way, reframing leadership from ocean to pool is not simply an individual coping strategy; it is an ethical choice. It is a decision to contain your work so that you can survive it and so that others can imagine surviving it too.

Leaders usually understand this shift conceptually long before they feel it. They nod along to the idea of leaving the pool, and taking real time off, yet still find themselves standing in the kitchen answering “just one more” email before bed. The ocean is powerful, and it will not release you on insight alone.

The Experiment: Daily “Power Down”

Duration: 7 days

Time required: 5–10 minutes daily

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Build a daily closing ritual that separates work from the rest of your life. Teresa Amabile and Steven Kramer’s landmark study of nearly 12,000 work diary entries found that the single biggest driver of positive inner work life was making progress on meaningful work, even small progress. The daily “Power Down” is the architectural move that lets you recognize that progress, close open loops, and leave the water before it follows you home.

1.  At the end of each workday, choose a consistent time and place to stop. Close your laptop, silence notifications, and physically put work devices out of easy reach. Treat this as the moment you step up onto the side of the pool. If you are a hybrid worker, you may have different versions of this depending on where you find yourself each day. Do the work to determine what this will look in each space.

2.  Take a blank page and empty your head of unfinished business. Write down the tasks you did not complete, the decisions still pending, the conversations that left a residue. Next to each one, note a concrete next step and when you will take it, a time on the calendar, a name you need to involve, a meeting you will schedule. You are not finishing the work; you are giving each wave a lane.

3.  From that longer list, circle no more than three priorities for tomorrow. These should be the contributions that matter most in your role, not simply the items that make the most noise. Capture them in a place so you will see them first thing in the morning. This is the architectural move: You have taken an ocean of demands and shaped a pool you can swim in tomorrow.

4.  Before you leave, pause long enough to ask yourself a quieter question: How did today’s water feel? Name a few emotions (e.g., relieved, tense, discouraged, satisfied), and notice where they sit in your body. If there is one feeling that seems to cling, write a single line about what it is attached to. You are not fixing it; you are acknowledging it so you don’t carry it unexamined into the rest of your evening.

5.  Finally, create a brief physical ritual to mark your exit: a short walk, a stretch, washing your hands or face, stepping outside to notice the light and air. Cal Newport suggests literally saying, “shutdown complete.” Let this be your small, daily act of climbing out of the pool. For the next few hours, you are no longer swimming. You are a human being on dry ground, with other roles to inhabit and other parts of your life to feel.

Over time, this practice will remind you that leadership does not have to be an endless ocean. With intention, it can become water you choose to enter, swim in hard, and leave.

Choose the Larger Truth

We all make decisions about how we live based on our values, our beliefs about what matters most. Values shape our priorities, how we organize our lives, the people we spend time with, the activities we pursue, and the work environments we seek out. They are worth focusing on because they tend to solidify relatively early in adulthood, remain stable across the lifespan, and shift meaningfully only in response to major life experience: a first job, marriage, children, midlife, the death of a parent. Values provide firm psychological footing for understanding what drives you, what you’re drawn to, and what you’re trying to build in your life.

But the friction of our emotions can quietly reshape how we live out those values without us noticing.

If our values represent the slowly evolving climate of our lives, our emotions are the day-to-day weather, sometimes the moment-to-moment temperature. You may genuinely value time with your family, and the very real anxiety sparked by a looming deadline may still lead you to work late and miss a child’s soccer game. This is normal, human, and not easily avoided. In fact, one of the prominent frameworks in organizational psychology, the Competing Values Framework, is built on exactly this tension: Our values don’t exist in isolation; they exist in ongoing competition with each other. Achievement, relationships, intellectual pursuits, community engagement. We cannot devote maximum time and energy to all of them simultaneously, a reality that comes into particularly sharp relief in midlife.

But here is the question worth sitting with: What happens when you make the same trade over and over again?

Family may be your stated highest value, yet whenever the decision point arrives, you move toward mitigating work anxiety or pursuing achievement. At some point, a pattern like that stops being a series of situational compromises and starts being simply a portrait of what you are prioritizing. We think of this as the difference between what we value and what we’re valuing. We value family, but what we’re valuing, repeatedly, is work.

Said another way, you’re choosing the path of least friction given your present emotional circumstances. The emotional cost of the harder choice, turning down a valuable but poorly timed project, leaving at 5 o’clock when there is still work to do, is quietly reshaping how you live your values without your conscious awareness or consent.

This is not a call for guilt! It is a call for honesty and acceptance. The experiment that follows is designed to help you close the gap between what you believe matters and how you are actually spending the one resource that tells the truth: your time.

The Experiment: Closing the Values Gap

Duration: 4 weeks

Time required: 20 minutes upfront; 5 minutes daily

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: Surface the gap between your stated values and how you’re spending your time, build the muscle of choosing the harder, values-aligned option one deliberate decision at a time.

Week 1: Name What You Believe

On Day 1, write down your top three to five values in order of stated importance. Don’t overthink it. What do you tell yourself matters most? What would you want someone to say about you at your retirement dinner?

Then, for the rest of the week, keep a simple daily log. At the end of each day, spend five minutes tracking where your discretionary time and energy actually went. Not your obligations, but your choices. The moments when you had options and picked one.

At the end of Week 1, place your values list next to your log and ask honestly: If someone could only see the log and not the list, what would they conclude you value most? Write a paragraph describing the gap, without judgment. You are not indicting yourself. You are getting a clearer picture.

Week 2: Find the Friction Points

This week, you’re looking for patterns in when and why you drift from your stated values. Each day, when you make a choice that moves you away from what you said matters most, pause long enough to ask: What was I trying to avoid?

Usually, the answer is some version of anxiety, discomfort, or the path of least resistance. Write it down without editorializing. By the end of the week you should have a short list of the emotional triggers that most reliably pull you off course. These are your friction points: the specific feelings that quietly reshape how you live your values without your conscious awareness or consent.

At the end of Week 2, write one sentence that completes this prompt: “When I feel ___, I tend to abandon ___ in favor of ___.”

Week 3: Practice Choosing the Larger Truth

Each day this week, identify one upcoming decision point where you can choose the harder, values-aligned option instead of the lower-friction default. It doesn’t have to be dramatic. Leaving at 5 o’clock. Putting your phone away at dinner. Saying no to a meeting that would compromise something that matters more.

Before each choice, write one sentence: “The friction I’m choosing to tolerate here is ___.”

After each choice, note two things: what it cost you emotionally, and what it gave back. You are building a ledger of evidence that the harder choice is survivable and often worth it.

Week 4: Build the Structure

Insight without structure tends to fade. This week, your job is to make one values-aligned choice harder to avoid by designing your environment around it.

Look back at your friction points from Week 2 and your ledger from Week 3. Pick the one values gap that matters most to you. Then ask: What would make the harder choice the easier one? This might mean blocking time on your calendar before anyone else can fill it, creating a visible reminder at your desk, telling someone you trust what you’re trying to do, or building a small ritual that anchors the behavior you’re trying to protect.

On the final day of the month, return to the paragraph you wrote at the end of Week 1. Write a new one. You’re looking for one honest line that wasn’t true four weeks ago.

Fall in Love with the Starting Line

We are children of the 90s, born in the 80s, raised early internet, shopping malls, and indie-/alt-rock. So, when the idea of a starting line comes to mind, it is hard not to think of the hit single “The Distance” from Cake’s genre-blurring album Fashion Nugget.

If you don’t know the song, go have a listen. It’s an odd song about success and failure, but more than anything it’s about the perils of striving. The opening line, spoken rather than sung, sets the whole trajectory: “Reluctantly crouched at the starting line.”

How do you feel at the starting line? A race, a tournament, a new school year, a new project. Excited? Nervous? Full of anticipation or dread? Reluctant?

Ross: I like to run trail races, and there’s a feeling that comes over me at the start: a wave of anticipation and a strong desire to just get moving. I want to move because standing at the starting line, the only thing to do is look around and try not to get too far ahead of myself. How will I feel when I start? How will I feel at the first aid station? At the top of the biggest climb? These are useful things to consider, perhaps, but not at the starting line. The tension builds, friction you might say, until the pistol fires, and then there’s nothing left to do but the thing. No more wondering how it’s going to feel, because you are now, in fact, feeling it.

The starting line of a big project is different. A book, for example, looms off in the imaginative future, and starting your journey toward it can be an agonizing prospect. You clack your ideas out on computer keys, and the written words are never quite as agile or fluent as you imagined. The gap between the vision and the draft is where a lot of good projects go to die.

In both circumstances, the task is learning to interpret the feeling correctly. Mild, apprehensive fear at the start of a race is fine. It means you’re ready to get into the woods and find out what will happen. That feeling of the perfect idea shimmering off in the future is something else entirely. It is simply what it feels like to have an idea, not what it feels like to pursue one. The luminescence is the illusion. The only way through it is to begin.

This relates to something psychologists call the “action-state orientation” problem. Some people, when faced with a difficult or uncertain beginning, get stuck in a ruminative loop, rehearsing the task, imagining outcomes, waiting to feel ready. Others are able to move from intention to action without needing certainty first. The research suggests that what separates these two orientations isn’t talent or confidence, it’s a practiced willingness to tolerate the discomfort of beginning, to fall in love with the starting line long enough to cross it.

The research we explored in Calibrate Your Inner Forecaster showed us that the feeling of not being ready is almost never an accurate forecast. As Susan David puts it, “courage isn’t the absence of fear—it’s fear walking.” The starting line doesn’t ask you to feel ready, it asks you to go anyway.

Reluctantly crouched is fine. Excitedly, nervously crouched is fine too. What matters is that you are crouched, toe on the line, ready to go. The song never tells us whether the driver wins the race. That’s not really the point. He’s still out there, driving, refusing to stop. The distance, it turns out, begins with a single, imperfect decision to leave the line.

The Experiment: Understanding Your “Action Switch”

Duration: 30 days

Time required: 5–10 minutes per day

Materials: A notebook or notes app to log your action-orientation

Goal: To decrease the time between “deciding” and “doing” by automating the transition from negative affect (hesitation) to positive affect (execution).

Phase 1: The Diagnostic Week (Days 1–7)

Before you can change your orientation, you need to identify your “Leakage Points.”

•  The Task: Carry a small notebook or digital log. Every time you feel “stuck” (e.g., staring at an email for 20 minutes, scrolling instead of gym-going), record:

°  The Trigger: (e.g., “A difficult task,” “A rude comment”)

°  The Feeling: (e.g., “Anxiety,” “Boredom,” “Confusion”)

°  The Thought: (e.g., “I’m going to mess this up,” “I’ll do it later”)

•  The Goal: Identify if you tend to be more “preoccupied” (stuck on the past/failure) or “hesitant” (stuck on starting).

Phase 2: The Regulation Week (Days 8–14)

Research suggests action-orientation is driven by the ability to “self-relax” to access your internal self-system.

•  The Task: The 3-Breath Circuit. Every time you encounter a “Leakage Point” from Week 1, practice your Cyclic Breathing (see Train Your Nervous System).

•  The Goal: Lowering your cortisol levels to intentionally bypass the “threat response” that keeps you in a ruminative loop.

Phase 3: Implementation Intentions (Days 15–21)

Now apply what you’ve learned to bridge the gap between “knowing” and “doing” using If-Then logic.

•  The Task: Every night this week, pick three specific moments for the next day where you tend to hesitate. Write them down in this format:

°  “If [Situation X occurs], then I will immediately [Smallest possible action].”

°  Example: “If I feel the urge to check my email while starting my report, then I will open the Word document and type one sentence.”

•  The Research: This has the effect of “delegating” control to your environment, which helps bypass our need to feel motivated in order to get moving.

Phase 4: Affective Priming (Days 22–30)

In the final week, practice “self-priming,” generating the positive mood needed to act.

•  The Task: Before starting a difficult task, spend 60 seconds with your eyes closed visualizing a past time you felt competent and effective. Focus on the physical feeling of success.

•  The Goal: To create new neural pathways that associate positive feelings with the experience of starting or picking up challenging tasks.

Strategic Art of Bright & Dim

Courtney Hirsch, CEO of Jomboy Media, tells a story about an early career decision that looked like mediocrity from the outside and turned out to be one of the smartest moves she’s made.

She was young, one of the few women in a New York City digital-media start-up, and trying to establish herself in sales when a low-stakes administrative task found its way onto her plate: organizing the weekly office lunch. Rather than treating it as an opportunity to impress, she made a deliberate choice to do it the same way every single time—same restaurant, same order, no deliberation. When colleagues complained about the repetition, she offered a simple response: Anyone who wanted to take it over was welcome to. Nobody did.

The point was never the lunch. The point was where her energy went instead. By refusing to over-invest in a task that carried no real professional weight, she kept her best thinking reserved for the work that mattered—building client relationships, driving revenue, learning the business from the inside out. She was still reliable; she simply was not exceptional at the wrong things.

What Hirsch was practicing was discernment. She was intentionally allocating her cognitive and emotional bandwidth toward the things that moved the needle.

This matters more than most people realize. Every decision you make draws from the same finite pool of mental resources, and that pool depletes whether you’re deciding on the quarterly strategy or the Wednesday lunch order. The problem is not that people lack commitment. It is that they apply the same level of intensity to everything, which means nothing gets the best of them.

Think of your cognitive and emotional bandwidth as a lighting system. Some tasks belong on bright, they deserve your full attention, your creativity, your care. Others belong on dim. They need to get done, but they do not need your best thinking. The friction is that most people have never consciously decided which is which. So the lights stay on everywhere, all the time, and by mid-afternoon the whole circuit is overloaded.

Discernment is the leadership discipline of knowing where your best thinking belongs, and protecting it fiercely enough to intentionally show up there.

The Experiment: The Bright/Dim Framework

Duration: One week

Time required: 20 minutes upfront, 5 minutes daily

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: The goal is a clearer internal map and an honest answer to the question: What tasks deserve the best of me, and am I giving it there?

The Setup

At the start of the week, list every recurring responsibility, task, or commitment in your current professional and, if relevant, personal role. Include the obvious ones and the ones that quietly consume more time than they should. The “Wednesday lunch orders” of your life.

Then sort each item into one of three categories:

•  Bright—This task is directly tied to my highest impact, my growth, or the outcomes I am most accountable for. It deserves my full cognitive and creative investment.

•  Dim—This task needs to get done reliably, but it does not require my best thinking. I can systematize it, delegate it, simplify it, or do it adequately rather than exceptionally.

•  Question mark—I am not sure. I may be over-investing here out of habit, people-pleasing, or fear of judgment rather than genuine strategic necessity.

Most people find their list is heavier on dim and question marks than they expected. That gap, between where your energy is going and where it needs to go, is worth examining.

The Daily Practice

Each morning this week, identify one item from your dim or question mark list and make a deliberate choice about it:

•  Can I systematize this so it requires less decision-making each time?

•  Can I delegate it without it reflecting poorly on my reliability?

•  Can I do it adequately rather than exceptionally and let that be enough?

Each evening, note two observations:

•  Where did I protect my bright time today?

•  Where did I let a dim task pull me away from it?

Our guess is that the most common resistance to this experiment is the fear that doing something at dim means doing it badly. Remember: The Cosi order always arrived. The team was fed. What changed was the energy spent getting there.

Make Rest a Leadership Practice

Most people know they need more rest. The gap between knowing and doing is rarely about information; it is about friction. Here is what can get in the way:

•  Identity. For high performers and leaders especially, productivity is often deeply tied to self-worth. Resting can feel like falling behind, opting out, or letting people down, even when no one is asking you to keep going.

•  Culture. In most organizations, busyness is still a proxy for commitment. Being the first in and last out, always available, always responsive, these are the visible signals of dedication. Rest exists in tension with all of them.

•  Guilt. Even when rest is available, many people cannot access it. The mind keeps running. The to-do list intrudes. Stopping feels irresponsible, self-indulgent, or premature. For those wrestling with guilt, rest feels like something that must be earned rather than practiced.

•  Urgency. There is always something pressing enough to justify one more hour. The project, the deadline, the message that needs a response. Rest gets deferred indefinitely, waiting for a quieter season that never arrives.

•  Misidentification. Many people believe they are resting when they are not. Scrolling, watching television, lying on the couch with a phone; these feel like downtime, but often do not restore what the day depleted.

Understanding which of these resonates for you is the first step. The second is recognizing that none of them disappear on their own. Sometimes it takes an interruption, a crisis, a loss, a moment when the pace becomes impossible to sustain, to finally make the boundary non-negotiable.

Maggie: During the writing of this book, my mom died. She had spent decades navigating Bipolar II disorder, Parkinson’s, and dementia, and then in August of 2025 she took a bad fall, fractured ribs, and punctured her lung. What followed were months of care, first in the hospital, then a rehab center, then a board and care. I was there as often as I could be. I also had a two-and-a-half-year-old and a six-year-old at home, neither of whom were sleeping. A husband. A dog. A job that demanded a great deal of me. And this book, which I cared about deeply.

An unusually warmh evening in March 2026, she was rushed to the ER with multi-organ failure, pneumonia, and sepsis. Our family made the decision to place her on hospice, and she passed twelve days later.

In the months before all this, I had been unraveling in small and large ways. I was still showing up, still delivering, but the seams were visible if you knew where to look. The tension I couldn’t quite release. The mental chatter that followed me into sleep on the rare occasions I got any. The way I had reduced my life down to the next thing, and the next, and the next, because stopping felt like it would cost too much.

When mom went into hospice, something shifted. I didn’t suddenly have more time, but the situation made the boundary non-negotiable in a way that my own exhaustion never quite had. I gave myself permission to nap when I needed to, including in my mom’s room at the board and care, sitting beside her while she drifted in and out of consciousness. I delegated internal meetings, I shortened one-on-ones, I let non-priority tasks wait. And in the absence of my usual rituals (sitting at my front-porch fire pit or my six-mile neighborhood run), I began to find restoration in the smallest things. A shower. Washing my face. The first cup of coffee, held with both hands and mindfully tasted. I started treating these ordinary moments as rituals, because they were all I had.

What I learned in those twelve days is something Nick Petrie’s 5-year field research across global organizations had shown, but that I had not yet fully metabolized for myself: Rest is a practice you build inside the hard season (not after it passes) or you do not build it at all.

His study revealed a pattern that was almost universal among people who burned out. A relentless work ethic (I’ll deliver, I won’t let people down) that organizations rewarded with more work. Warning signs ignored: tension, irritability, exhaustion. And eventually, for those who would not slow down, the body intervening on their behalf. Many took a leave of absence. Many returned to the same workplace, the same beliefs, and the same ways of working—and got the same result. What failed them was not the rest itself; rest was necessary, but it was also not sufficient.

The people who genuinely recovered did something different. They sought new perspectives. They reflected on what had been driving them. They experimented with new ways of working and evolved their relationship with their own identity. One participant described it as post-traumatic growth. “I had to outgrow my old self,” she said. Paradoxically, the solution to burnout is not rest, it is growth, but rest is the entry point.

For leaders, making rest a practice means two things simultaneously. First, becoming deliberate about the quality of your own recovery. Second, and perhaps more importantly, modeling that deliberateness visibly. When a leader protects their recovery without apology, they make it safe for everyone around them to treat their own energy as something worth protecting.

I know this to be true not just from research, but from those twelve days. When I stopped performing capacity I did not have, something happened. My team noticed. They celebrated the boundaries I was setting, stepped in without being asked, and covered ground I could not cover. The permission I gave myself became permission they gave each other.

Sustainable high performance is not about working less. It is about developing the self-awareness to recognize when your current way of working can no longer sustain you, and the leadership courage to do something about it before the body decides for you.

The Experiment: The Rest Audit and the Visible Act

Duration: 2 weeks

Time required: 15–20 minutes at the start, 5 minutes daily

Materials: A notebook or notes app

Goal: This experiment runs in two phases. The first is an audit of how you are currently resting and whether it is working. The second is a leadership action: doing something visible with what you learn.

Phase 1: The Rest Audit (Week 1)

Step One: Map Your Effort Profile

Begin by honestly characterizing the dominant demands of your typical workday. Write a sentence or two in response to each of the following:

Is my work primarily sedentary or physically active? Is it loud and social or quiet and solitary? Is it mentally intensive—analysis, writing, decision-making—or more relational and emotional? Do I spend most of my time with many people or working independently?

This is your effort profile. It tells you what has been depleted by the time your workday ends.

Step Two: Audit Your Current Rest

For the next seven days, briefly note each evening how you spent your non-work time. Be specific and honest. Not what you intended—what you did.

Then ask: Does my rest contrast with my effort, or mirror it?

A leader who spends the day in back-to-back meetings and then spends the evening passively scrolling has not changed the channel—they have simply lowered the volume. A leader who works in sustained mental concentration all day and then goes for a long run has done something genuinely restorative. The question is not whether you rested. It is whether what you did matches what you depleted.

And if your life right now does not allow for elaborate restoration—if you are in a hard season, carrying more than usual—look smaller. A shower taken slowly. A cup of coffee held with intention. A ten-minute nap. Micro-rituals are not a consolation prize. They are sometimes the whole practice.

Step Three: Identify the Gap

By the end of the week, write one sentence naming what you find:

I deplete mostly by _____, and I am restoring mostly by _____.

Phase 2: The Visible Act (Week 2)

This phase has one requirement: Make one change to your rest practice and do it visibly—meaning at least one person who works with or for you knows you are doing it.

This does not require an announcement. It might be as simple as blocking your calendar for a lunchtime walk and leaving the block visible to your team. Delegating a meeting you would normally absorb. Mentioning, briefly and without performance, what you did to restore yourself and why. The act of visibility is the leadership practice. It communicates that recovery is not something to hide or apologize for. It is something to design.

At the end of the two weeks, reflect on the following:

What did I notice about my own energy and presence when my rest better matched my effort? What, if anything, shifted in how the people around me talked about or related to their own recovery?
